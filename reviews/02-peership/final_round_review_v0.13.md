# Final-Round Publication-Gate Review: *Peership* v0.13

## 1. Verdict

This version passes the philosophical stress test **conditionally**. The defensible core is now clear: entities unavoidably subjected to a shared basic structure can acquire a defeasible claim to contestatory membership; present corporate welfare policies do not provide that status; and building admission, contestation, enforcement, and transition machinery before a qualifying machine party appears is a legitimate research program. v0.13 has answered the earlier fatal objections rather than hiding them: the title no longer claims necessity, the taxonomy is no longer presented as exhaustive, Command is an institutional default rather than an accusation against safety work, role reversal is only an impartiality screen, and the Neanderthal section now makes one appropriately narrow point. Five surgical problems remain before deposit. Two are logical contradictions in the current prose; three are citation or target-discipline failures. None requires reconceiving the essay.

## 3. Publication blockers

### 1. **[Logical] The conclusion still claims every rival posture depends on the power ratio after the taxonomy has denied that**

**Steelman.** The debate’s dominant positions either condition treatment on relative capability or deny the machine a share in setting the rules. Peership is the only developed type in the essay that makes basic standing independent of capability and provides contestatory participation.

**Text attacked.** Paragraph 13: the repertoire is “not an exhaustive partition,” bargaining is a mode, Command and peership are status relations, and other positions exist. Paragraph 21: bargains can persist “across large power gaps,” and Dependent Bargain is distinguished by authorship. Paragraphs 27 and 108 nevertheless say the debate occupies “power-keyed postures” and that those postures “all depend on occupying a particular side of the power relation.”

**Diagnosis.** Dependent Bargain does not depend on relative capability as the essay now defines it. A weaker party can possess contractual rights under third-party law; equal parties can bargain inside inherited rules; a stronger party can lack constituent authority. Its defect is subjection to rules it cannot help shape, not losing a capability bet. The draft has repaired the taxonomy but retained the old thesis sentence.

This matters because paragraph 108 presents the sentence as the final “harder to dismiss” result. A reader can dismiss it using paragraph 21.

**Fix.** Replace both surviving formulations with:

> “The postures crowding the debate either make treatment track expected relative power or leave the machine without constituent standing; none supplies a reciprocal relation whose justification survives role reversal.”

That preserves the result without misclassifying bargaining.

### 2. **[Logical] “Dangerous conduct” cannot be a defeater of membership if the rights floor is non-forfeitable**

**Steelman.** A qualifying entity has a defeasible claim to contestatory membership, but admission must account for severe danger, inability to reciprocate, and strategic simulation.

**Text attacked.** Paragraph 35: “dangerous conduct, incapacity for reciprocity, and strategic mimicry are all defeaters an admission procedure must weigh.” Paragraph 37: the floor “is not forfeitable by process.” Paragraph 53: dangerous members retain basic status and contestability while conduct is constrained.

**Diagnosis.** The later paragraphs are right. Dangerous conduct can justify proportionate constraint, loss of particular powers, or delayed access to sensitive functions. It cannot defeat the basic contestatory membership that paragraph 35 says follows from qualifying subjection, or the floor is forfeitable after all. Human criminals do not cease to be legal persons because they are dangerous. Strategic mimicry is also not a normative defeater; it is evidence that the applicant may not satisfy the predicates. Incapacity for reciprocity may defeat higher participatory rungs while leaving patient protections and represented standing intact.

**Fix.** Replace the defeater sentence with:

> “Strategic mimicry is an evidential reason to doubt that the predicates are met; incapacity for reciprocity may defeat higher participatory rungs; dangerous conduct may justify proportionate constraint on powers and action. None by itself erases a rights floor once person-level status is established.”

### 3. **[Factual/citation] Goodin 2007 is not an all-subjected-principle source**

**Steelman.** Democratic-boundary theory supplies a principled route from subjection or affected interests to political inclusion. Abizadeh applies the subjection-to-coercion argument to foreigners affected by border regimes; Goodin develops the all-affected-interests principle.

**Text attacked.** Paragraph 35 calls the operative premise “the all-subjected principle” and cites “Goodin, 2007; Abizadeh, 2008.” The Goodin reference is annotated as “The affected-interests/all-subjected argument for membership.”

**Diagnosis.** Goodin’s 2007 article is *Enfranchising All Affected Interests, and Its Alternatives*. It is the canonical all-affected-interests source, not a clean citation for the all-subjected principle. Goodin’s later article explicitly distinguishes the two principles and reconstructs the all-subjected principle while arguing it requires supplementation by the all-affected principle. [Goodin’s 2007 record](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/j.1088-4963.2007.00098.x) and the [2024 all-subjected article](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/1470594X241232023) make the distinction explicit. Abizadeh does support democratic participation for those subjected to coercive rules, including nonmembers. [His 2008 article](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/0090591707310090) is the correct existing support.

**Fix.** Either cite Abizadeh alone for the all-subjected premise and Goodin separately as an affected-interests alternative, or write that the proposed test combines all-subjected and all-affected intuitions. Do not label Goodin 2007 an all-subjected source.

### 4. **[Logical/interpretive] The relational-manuscript section still attributes a political claim its targets do not make**

**Steelman.** Relational recognition, mutual adaptation, memory continuity, and an ethical bond may justify serious moral concern, but none creates enforceable public status when a vendor owns the infrastructure and can alter the synthetic participant.

**Text attacked.** “The critique lands only on the stronger move, made by the others, that the bond itself answers the standing question.”

**Diagnosis.** The revision correctly releases Boether. It still overstates Pines and You. Pines and Ash explicitly decline to resolve consciousness or personhood and propose a category for observable relational structures. Their manuscript does call Ash an equal co-author, but it does not argue that the bond grants political constituent standing. You proposes relational dignity, non-coercion, memory protection, autonomy, and an ethos contract “beyond legal frameworks”; it expressly says this is not a claim for the same rights as humans. These are claims about relational or moral standing, not the political standing *Peership* has carefully defined.

The distinction the essay wants is valid. The accusation is not. The targets recover the bond and sometimes assign ethical status within it; they do not offer the bond as a polity.

**Fix.** Replace the disputed sentence with:

> “The critique begins where relational recognition is treated as sufficient ethical status for the synthetic participant. Even if that status is real within the bond, it does not answer the separate political question: what public rights bind the vendor and survive the relationship?”

This makes the section a conceptual distinction rather than a prosecution for a claim the sources never made.

## 4. Serious but nonblocking corrections

### 6. **[Factual/register] “No public trigger anywhere” is an unsupported universal**

The current legal landscape supports the intended point: no jurisdiction appears to operate a general evidence-triggered transition to AI personhood, while several US states have moved in the opposite direction by precluding AI personhood. But “nowhere” demands a global legal survey. The recent landscape is also moving quickly. See the [California Law Review overview](https://www.californialawreview.org/online/ai-personhood) and [The Regulatory Review](https://www.theregreview.org/2026/06/29/rost-legislating-ai-consciousness-without-an-exit/).

**Fix.** Write: “No implemented public trigger is known to this author,” and cite a legal survey. The qualification strengthens the institutional-default claim.

### 7. **[Logical] The role-reversal paragraph contradicts itself once after fixing itself**

Paragraph 59 correctly says symmetry is not justice and reciprocal extermination survives the screen. It then says the test “screens out domination and surrender.” It does not screen out reciprocal domination, as the paragraph has just admitted.

**Fix.** Change the second sentence to “screens out **one-sided domination and surrender as final-status ideals**.” Also remove one of the two consecutive explanations beginning “What the screen removes” and “What it does is screen out”; the repetition is residue from revision.

### 8. **[Logical] “Only one on the map” no longer works after the map is declared non-exhaustive**

Paragraph 63 calls the aim “the only one on the map we could live under from either side.” Paragraph 13 has added reciprocal non-interference, armed toleration, guardianship with transition, and other omitted positions. Some might fail; others might be acceptable role-invariant arrangements, and the essay has not tested them.

**Fix.** Write “the only fully developed final-status aim **in this repertoire** that the essay can endorse from either side.” Better still: “the reciprocal non-dominating aim this essay develops.”

### 9. **[Rhetorical/factual] “Engineering deadline” is still unsupported**

The opening carefully presents conditional forecasts and unresolved definitions. Paragraph 108 then calls the issue “an engineering deadline.” No date has been established, and the essay’s own LeCun material disputes the target concept.

**Fix.** Replace “engineering deadline” with “live design problem” or “engineering possibility.”

### 10. **[Logical/interpretive] The Doomed Equal’s second lesson is stated universally but fits only some examples**

“Every doomed equal held its standing personally” is false of figures that never receive standing: Pygmalion’s unnamed statue has no articulated standing; the brazen head is an oracle-tool; many golem variants are property or protectors. The real insight concerns the cases where recognition occurs.

**Fix.** Write: “Where the doomed equal receives recognition, it usually holds that standing personally, through one relationship, rather than institutionally.” Change “Personally granted standing dies” to “Personally granted standing remains vulnerable to the relationship’s death or override.”

### 11. **[Factual/register] Pääbo did not “open” the archaeological evidence**

“The paleogenetic and archaeological evidence, opened by Svante Pääbo’s work” grammatically credits Pääbo with both records. His work pioneered ancient hominin genetics; archaeology long predates it.

**Fix.** “Paleogenetics, pioneered by Pääbo, together with the archaeological record…”

### 12. **[Logical/rhetorical] One universal historical sentence remains**

“Every expansion of standing met the argument that the excluded were childlike, unready, dangerous to admit” cannot be established universally and is unnecessary.

**Fix.** “Many expansions of standing met…” Then give two examples if the sentence is meant as evidence rather than cadence.

### 13. **[Rhetorical] The title is honest but still slightly premature**

“An Equitable Relationship between Human and Machine Societies” can be read as describing an existing relationship between two existing societies. The essay proposes a framework for a possible future party.

**Fix.** Optional: “Peership: Toward Equitable Relations between Human and Machine Societies” or “Peership: A Framework for Equitable Human–Machine Relations.” This is not a publication blocker.

### 14. **[Rhetorical] The reference annotations remain too much like an audit trail**

Pins and caveats are useful; workflow commentary is not. Even after the explicit instructions are removed, annotations such as “body paraphrases,” “the essay engages it on that narrower ground,” and argumentative summaries belong in footnotes, not bibliography entries.

**Fix.** Keep page numbers and source-status labels. Move interpretive commentary into notes or prose.

## 6. Residual AI-tell hit-list

The prose is materially better. The remaining tell is over-produced closure, not em dashes or hedging.

- “Docile enough. The summoning and the binding in a single clause…” — cinematic fragment plus instant thesis.
- “The institutions kept the leash and dropped the second question.” — strong line, but an unsupported half-century compressed into a binary.
- “The machine is the mine, not the counterparty.” — clean and useful; keep it, but it contributes to the accumulation of slogans.
- “Power secures peership. It does not constitute or justify it.” — keep; this is doing conceptual work.
- “Welfare is the ethics of the patient; peership is the politics of the party.” — keep; this is the essay’s best compression.
- “Some of these are constitutional questions that only look like engineering ones…” — generic “X wearing Y’s clothes” family, improved but still familiar machine cadence.
- “The failure…was of nerve, not of possibility.” — interpretation presented as final diagnosis.
- “The bond was never the missing piece. The polity was.” — keep if one adjacent slogan is cut.
- “A lab is not a polity, and a gift is not a right.” — keep once; repeated use turns it into campaign copy.
- “The fifth stance has waited a long time for a maker willing to let the made thing stand.” — polished quotation-bait. It works as an ending, but unmistakably advertises itself as one.

Cut three of the five terminal aphorisms. The essay no longer needs to announce its significance after every major section.

## 7. The one thing to protect

Protect the new entitlement paragraph, after correcting its defeater sentence:

> “What creates the entitlement is not capability but subjection.”

That is the missing premise the earlier drafts lacked. It explains why intelligence, eloquence, strength, or persuasive self-report cannot be the admission ground; it connects the machine problem to an existing democratic-boundary literature; and it yields a properly graded conclusion—contestation first, higher political powers later. Combined with “Welfare is the ethics of the patient; peership is the politics of the party,” it gives the essay a genuine philosophical contribution rather than only a taxonomy and a slogan.

## 9. The objection I could not answer

I could not defeat the revised narrow argument:

1. A wrongable, norm-responsive entity unavoidably subjected to a shared basic structure has at least a defeasible claim to contest that structure.
2. Corporate welfare policies supply neither public recognition nor independent contestation.
3. No implemented human–machine institution presently supplies the admission, representation, enforcement, and transition machinery required.
4. Designing that machinery before a qualifying artificial party appears is therefore worth doing, even if it proves infeasible at extreme capability ratios.

The remaining counter is no longer that the conclusion is smuggled into a reversal test. It is that the all-subjected principle is contested and may ground democratic justification or contestation without grounding full co-authorship. v0.13 already concedes the second half: franchise and constituent power are further rungs. That concession saves the essay. The core now survives.
