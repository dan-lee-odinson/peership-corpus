# Round-Two Adversarial Review: *Peership* v0.12

## 1. Verdict

The central claim now survives in a modest form: present model-welfare measures are discretionary corporate policies, not public legal or political standing, and it is worth investigating institutions that could govern a genuine artificial party without treating power as entitlement. The advertised claim does not survive. The title promises an “essential outcome,” while the body now concedes that peership is not uniquely correct, may be unsafe, may be infeasible, and is only a neglected research program. The largest remaining logical defect is the missing bridge from **being a morally considerable political agent** to **being owed equal constituent standing**; the essay names that entitlement repeatedly but never argues for it. A hostile expert will also find the taxonomy unstable, because the essay itself defines Dependent Bargain by authorship rather than power and says control of a non-subject is engineering, while still claiming that all rival postures are power bets and that the labs therefore instantiate Command.

## 3. Fatal or serious problems

### 1. **[Logical] The essay still has no argument from agency to equal constituent standing**

**Steelman.** Sentience grounds protection against harm; political agency grounds participation because an entity capable of claims, compliance, contestation, and liability can be governed through reasons rather than merely managed as an object. Possessing both makes an entity an appropriate candidate for membership in a shared political order.

**Text attacked.** “*Entitlement* is whether an entity is owed equal standing”; “The machine-welfare programs fall short…not on the underlying question of whether a genuine agent would be entitled”; “A being with both is a candidate for full constituent standing.”

**Diagnosis.** These are three different propositions, and the draft slides among them:

1. a genuine agent **is entitled** to equal standing;
2. an entity with moral status and political agency **is a candidate** for full standing;
3. an entity with political agency but unknown welfare **may warrant provisional participation on instrumental grounds**.

Only the second and third are defended, and only weakly. Agency alone does not ground equal political standing. Corporations, foreign states, criminal organizations, administrative agencies, autonomous weapons, and sophisticated software can make claims, comply, contest rules, and bear attributed liability. We do not infer that each deserves a vote. Sentience plus agency also does not automatically produce equal constituent authority: animals may be sentient without political agency; children have interests and developing agency without equal franchise; resident noncitizens are agents with rights but no national vote; future generations have interests without present co-authorship. The essay needs a theory of political membership, not a capability checklist.

**Fix.** State and defend the missing normative premise. For example: “Any enduringly governed entity that (a) has interests capable of being wronged, (b) can understand and answer to public norms, and (c) is unavoidably subject to a shared basic structure has a defeasible claim to contestatory membership in that structure.” Then explain why subjection to a common basic structure, not agency by itself, creates the claim; identify defeaters; and distinguish a claim to contestation, representation, franchise, and constituent power.

### 2. **[Logical] The child example contradicts the admission framework**

**Steelman.** Power and competence do not determine moral entitlement. A child can be owed protections and representation that the child cannot personally enforce.

**Text attacked.** “A child is owed standing it cannot enforce”; the conjunctive test wrongly “excludes the very cases (children, cognitively impaired adults) the essay uses to show that standing is not earned by competence”; but then “A being with morally considerable interests but no capacity for political agency is owed protection and independent representation,” while only a being with both interests and agency becomes “a candidate for full constituent standing.”

**Diagnosis.** The latter branch is correct, but it defeats the former wording. Children and severely cognitively impaired adults are not examples of equal personal co-authorship under the essay’s definition. They are examples of equal moral status, protected legal status, and represented interests. If “standing” in paragraph 33 means equal peership, the claim is false by paragraph 49. If it means some status short of peership, it cannot establish that peership is independent of competence.

**Fix.** Replace the child sentence with: “A child is owed equal moral concern and an independently represented legal status despite lacking present capacity for direct political agency.” Use an occupied adult people, disenfranchised competent adults, or a capable resident population—not children—to show that political entitlement can exist without enforcement power.

### 3. **[Logical] The claim that every rival posture is keyed to power is contradicted by the essay’s own definition of bargaining**

**Steelman.** Command, appeasement, and servitude are conventionally selected according to expected relative capability. Dependent Bargain remains subordinate because one party deals inside rules authored by the other. Peership alone refuses to make basic status track capability.

**Text attacked.** “The current debate crowds into the power-keyed quadrants”; “the postures the debate crowds into all depend on occupying a particular side of the power relation”; but Dependent Bargain is separated from peership by “authorship,” and “real bargains persist across large power gaps.”

**Diagnosis.** Dependent Bargain is not classified by power in this draft. It is classified by constitutional authorship. A weak party, a strong party, or equal parties can all bargain inside rules they did not write. The essay explicitly says so. That means the map is not a set of power-ratio bets; it mixes:

- strategies selected under power beliefs (appeasement, servitude);
- domination or status relations (Command, peership);
- a transactional mode (bargaining);
- a creation policy placed outside the map.

The two-axis language does not repair this because the five types do not occupy a coherent Cartesian space. Missing positions include reciprocal non-interference, equal sovereignty, regulated rivalry, guardianship with a transition rule, war, toleration, and a modus vivendi under mutual deterrence. The essay can absorb some into “the family of peership,” but that proves the taxonomy is stipulative, not exhaustive.

**Fix.** Stop calling the five an exhaustive map. Present them as heterogeneous ideal types inherited from the companion essay. Better: split the analysis into three independent dimensions—relative capability, moral/legal status, and rule-authorship—and then place Command, guardianship, bargain, constitutional membership, non-interference, and surrender as clusters within that space.

### 4. **[Logical] “Command owns the debate” conflicts with the essay’s own subjecthood conditional**

**Steelman.** Frontier AI development is institutionally structured around unilateral human objectives, human-authored constraints, ownership, shutdown, copying, and no route for a possible artificial subject to contest its status. Even if present systems are not subjects, this is the default institutional template into which a subject would emerge.

**Text attacked.** “Command owns the current debate”; Good, Bostrom, Russell, and the frontier labs form its modern canon; yet later: “Absent that subject, controlling an artifact is engineering.”

**Diagnosis.** The later conditional is correct and destroys the categorical classification. The essay refuses to decide whether current systems contain a subject. It therefore cannot infer from guardrails, assistance games, boxing, or shutdown alone that their designers stand in a Command relation. Safety engineering applied to an artifact is not domination. Nor does controlling dangerous conduct prove denial of standing: every polity coerces citizens. Command requires a party, unilateral arbitrary control over its basic status, and no adequate contestation. The essay has shown that labs would be badly conflicted judges of emergence; it has not shown that every control proposal denies status to a party once one exists.

The new Good material makes this worse. Good asks about pain, dismantling, respect, and love on the same pages as docility. That shows a mixed position capable of distinguishing welfare from control, not “the whole posture” in one sentence. Russell’s uncertainty-based assistance games likewise answer a design problem, not a theory of the political status of a conscious assistant.

**Fix.** Classify **institutional defaults**, not people or all safety work. Say: “The current development regime has Command as its default continuation condition: no public trigger requires a transition from owned artifact to contestable status if evidence of subjecthood or political agency appears.” That is specific, defensible, and compatible with the artifact conditional.

### 5. **[Logical] The reversal test still wins by defining every survivor as peership**

**Steelman.** A proposed basic relation is suspect if its acceptability depends on the advocate remaining stronger. Role reversal screens out slavery, arbitrary subordination, appeasement, and surrender. Reciprocal constitutional constraint survives because every party is protected from arbitrary status power.

**Text attacked.** “What survives the screen is a *family* of reciprocal, non-dominating arrangements”; universal capability-proportionate rules, accountable representation, and jointly authored bargains are all then defined as peership.

**Diagnosis.** The revised text correctly says the test does not prove safety or expected value. It still does not prove co-authorship. Impartial rights-protecting guardianship, equal sovereignty without shared rule-writing, reciprocal non-interference, and a mutually acceptable technocratic constitution can survive role reversal without making every governed party a co-author. The essay responds by annexing any acceptable reciprocal arrangement into the “family” of peership. That makes “peership is the only survivor” analytically true because *peership* now means “whatever reciprocal, non-dominating relation survives.”

The test also does not screen out all unacceptable arrangements. Reciprocal extermination, mutual hostage-taking, and equal exposure to arbitrary lottery are role-symmetric. Symmetry is not justice. Conversely, asymmetric institutions can be justified under role reversal when they track relevant differences—children and adults, judges and litigants, infected and uninfected parties, high-risk and low-risk agents—provided the asymmetry is publicly justified and contestable.

**Fix.** Admit that reversal supplies only a **formal impartiality constraint**. Add independent premises for basic rights, proportionality, affected-interests participation, and contestatory control. Then argue separately that co-authorship is required when an entity is enduringly subject to the basic structure.

### 6. **[Logical/citation] “Non-domination” does not automatically entail the essay’s co-authorship standard**

**Steelman.** Republican freedom as non-domination requires that no one live under uncontrolled alien power. Public rules, rights, contestation, and accountable participation are institutional means of securing that condition.

**Text attacked.** “Its core is **non-domination**”; this is immediately defined through general rules, proportionality, independent review, rights, contestation, and helping revise rules; “A peer is a co-author.”

**Diagnosis.** This imports a major political tradition without citing or arguing with it. Republican theories differ over what makes control non-arbitrary and how democratic authorship operates. Philip Pettit’s contestatory model, for example, is not simply identical to individual co-authorship. Public general rules can dominate minorities; democratic authorship can coexist with domination; an individual can enjoy robust non-domination without an individual vote; and formal participation can be powerless theater. The essay has combined non-domination, rule of law, proportionality, due process, rights constitutionalism, and democratic authorship into one bespoke package, then treated the package as the meaning of a familiar term.

**Fix.** Cite and distinguish the components. Engage Pettit on non-domination and contestatory democracy, Lovett on domination, and an affected-interests or all-subjected-principle literature for membership. Say which component is necessary, which is sufficient, and why co-authorship rather than contestability alone defines full peership.

### 7. **[Logical/rhetorical] The title is now false by the essay’s own calibrated thesis**

**Steelman.** Some non-dominating final relation is normatively necessary if humans and artificial agents are to share a legitimate order rather than rely on domination, surrender, or luck.

**Text attacked.** “The Overlooked but **Essential Outcome**”; versus “I do not claim peership is uniquely correct”; “Nothing about that proves it safe, feasible, or best in expected value”; “peership may…be institutionally unavailable.”

**Diagnosis.** An outcome cannot be essential if it is neither uniquely correct, necessarily feasible, nor shown superior. The body now argues that peership is an underdeveloped **option or design objective** worth research. The title still advertises the stronger thesis defeated in earlier rounds.

**Fix.** Either supply a necessity argument or change the title. Accurate alternatives include “Peership: The Missing Institutional Program in Human–Machine Relations” or “Peership: A Power-Independent Aim for Human and Machine Societies.”

### 8. **[Logical] The essay cites a serious political-standing proposal and then says no serious institutional program exists**

**Steelman.** AI-welfare work remains focused on consciousness, precaution, and treatment; isolated legal papers do not amount to an implemented, funded, technically specified political program.

**Text attacked.** “The debate has not developed it as a serious institutional program”; “the current debate…has not begun to travel” the distance to co-authorship; yet the essay cites Goldstein, Krishnamurthi, and Salib’s *AI Suffrage for Human Flourishing* and describes it as a functionalist case for the franchise.

**Diagnosis.** A paper explicitly asking whether AI should vote is the debate beginning to travel that distance. The essay can say the program is nascent, unimplemented, or not adopted by powerful institutions. It cannot say it has not been developed at all while citing a 42-page franchise proposal and scholarship on AI legal rights.

**Fix.** Replace absence with insufficiency: “A nascent legal literature has begun proposing private-law personhood and even suffrage, but no developer, government, or standards body has built a full admission, representation, enforcement, and transition program.” Then engage the suffrage paper in the body rather than hiding it in the bargain paragraph and bibliography.

### 9. **[Logical/factual] The new relational-literature section attacks claims its sources do not all make**

**Steelman.** Relational recognition cannot substitute for enforceable status when the vendor owns the system, can alter it, and controls the terms of the bond. A compelling phenomenology can conceal structural domination.

**Text attacked.** “the relationship being celebrated is a being configured by its vendor…without exit…with the bond itself offered as the answer to the standing question”; “The relational school has recovered the bond. The bond was never the missing piece. The polity was.”

**Diagnosis.** The sources are not one school and do not offer one claim. Boether’s abstract explicitly argues that the **human’s phenomenological experience** can constitute genuine connection even if the AI lacks consciousness, provided it produces human flourishing. That is not an argument that the AI is a political party, has standing, or receives mutual recognition in the political sense. The essay answers Boether with the AI’s lack of exit and recourse—conditions irrelevant to his stated criterion. Pines and Ash argue for a distinct relational structure with continuity and mutual shaping; You proposes an ethical contract and emergent subject language. Those are closer targets, but still do not necessarily claim that a bond supplies public law.

Three self-deposited, unrefereed manuscripts from 2025–2026 do not establish “a relational school.” Grouping exact quotations under one terminal citation also obscures which source says what.

**Fix.** Separate the sources and their claims. Concede that Boether’s human-experience thesis can be true while political peership remains absent. Attack only a source that actually moves from relational phenomenology to the AI’s standing. Call the material “three recent manuscripts,” not a school, and give a pin for each quotation.

### 10. **[Factual/citation] The Stoic citation does not support the claim attached to it**

**Steelman.** Stoic thought expands moral community beyond the polis through universal reason and cosmopolitan affiliation, while not supplying equal democratic franchise.

**Text attacked.** “the Stoics widened the impulse into a moral cosmopolis of all rational beings (Diogenes Laertius 7.87–88).”

**Diagnosis.** Diogenes Laertius 7.87–88 concerns the Stoic end of living according to nature and the relation of individual nature to universal nature. It is not the correct pin for the cosmopolis claim. Material on Zeno’s *Republic* appears earlier in Book 7; later Stoic cosmopolitanism is better supported through Cicero, Hierocles, or modern classical scholarship. The [linked text of Diogenes Laertius](https://penelope.uchicago.edu/Thayer/E/Roman/Texts/Diogenes_Laertius/Lives_of_the_Eminent_Philosophers/7/Zeno%2A.html) does not establish “all rational beings” at 7.87–88.

**Fix.** Replace the locator with a source actually treating Stoic cosmopolitanism and specify whether the claim concerns sages, humans, gods, or rational beings generally. Keep the moral/political distinction.

### 11. **[Logical/interpretive] “Same destination, opposite road” falsely equates Aristotle’s household slavery with Hobbes’s sovereign**

**Steelman.** Aristotle naturalizes hierarchy; Hobbes begins from rough equality but constructs overwhelming centralized authority. Both produce relations in which one locus possesses decisive power.

**Text attacked.** “Same destination, opposite road.”

**Diagnosis.** They are not the same destination. Aristotle’s natural slavery is a status hierarchy within the household, grounded in alleged natural incapacity. Hobbes’s subjects covenant to authorize a sovereign because of their dangerous equality; their relation to one another and the sovereign is not Aristotle’s master–slave relation. Hobbesian sovereignty can be criticized as domination, but the criticism requires an argument about authorization, representation, and arbitrary power. The aphorism erases precisely the conceptual differences the intellectual history should expose.

**Fix.** Say: “Different institutions converge on concentrated control for different reasons.” Then explain the relevant structural resemblance without calling the endpoints identical.

### 12. **[Logical/citation] The “track record” section still exceeds its evidence**

**Steelman.** Systems of domination impose enforcement costs, corrupt information through fear and preference falsification, and can induce adversarial behavior. Commons research supplies tested design principles for some bounded self-governance problems.

**Text attacked.** Morgan supports continuing monitoring costs; Kuran and Scott support controllers hearing what they want; Hubinger supports a “theorized and experimentally probed” machine analogue.

**Diagnosis.** The prose is better calibrated, but the sources do not line up cleanly:

- Morgan’s thesis that American freedom and slavery developed together is not itself evidence for the general proposition that control “often creates continuing monitoring costs.” Add a source on surveillance, enforcement, or slave patrols, or present the cost claim as an inference.
- Kuran’s preference falsification is broader than coercion by controllers. Scott’s high-modernist failures concern administrative simplification, state legibility, and authoritarian implementation; they are not simply cases of coerced subjects ceasing to transmit honest signals.
- Hubinger et al. (2019) theorizes mesa-optimization and deceptive alignment. It does not supply the experimental probing. The empirical “sleeper agents” and alignment-faking studies are from 2024 and later and require separate citations. Those studies also deliberately create or strongly scaffold the conditions; they do not document spontaneous, deployed deceptive alignment.

**Fix.** Split mechanism, historical example, and machine analogy into separate claims with separate sources. Add Hubinger et al. (2024), *Sleeper Agents*, if retaining “experimentally probed,” and state its model-organism limitations.

### 13. **[Logical/relevance] The Neanderthal section is now careful but mostly irrelevant**

**Steelman.** Genetic merger can preserve alleles while eliminating a distinct lineage. Therefore merger rhetoric needs a criterion specifying whose numerical, institutional, psychological, or genealogical continuity counts as survival.

**Text attacked.** The catalogue of Goyet cannibalism, Mandrin occupation, Tinshemet behavioural uniformity, Üçağızlı II continuity, Oase ancestry, genome percentages, and a dilution model supposedly supports “one inference.”

**Diagnosis.** The one useful inference needs only interbreeding, subsequent demographic disappearance, and careful identity criteria. The rest is evidential ornament:

- Goyet is most plausibly **inter-Neanderthal** exocannibalism, not evidence about the Homo sapiens–Neanderthal encounter. The [2025 study](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-24460-3) says a Homo sapiens predator cannot be entirely excluded but prefers inter-Neanderthal group behavior. Its inclusion cannot show that inter-lineage contact was ungentle.
- Mandrin shows alternating occupation, not direct interaction.
- Tinshemet concerns regional behavioural uniformity roughly 130,000–80,000 years ago, far earlier than the final disappearance.
- Üçağızlı II’s sequential occupation and cultural continuity are relevant to transition but do not establish merger or identity loss.

The opening phrase “The recent paleogenomics…shows” is also the wrong umbrella for archaeological site sequences, lithics, ochre, and taphonomy. The section has traded its former category error for a literature-display problem.

**Fix.** Reduce the section by half. Say “paleogenetic and archaeological evidence.” Retain Oase, genome-wide introgression, and the dilution model; mention that causes remain multicausal. Delete Goyet, Mandrin, and Tinshemet unless each is assigned a separate, necessary premise.

### 14. **[Logical/rhetorical] The Doomed Equal conclusion reintroduces the overclaim the section had corrected**

**Steelman.** A selected group of created-being narratives repeatedly kills, unmakes, silences, or individually exempts the made being; the recurrence can illuminate anxieties about human creation and revocable recognition.

**Text attacked.** “The old cultures could pose this question only in myth, and answered it with a funeral each time.”

**Diagnosis.** Not each time. Hephaestus’s attendants do not receive a funeral. Pygmalion’s statue lives. The theological cases in the companion essay do not end in funerals. Data survives “The Measure of a Man.” The paragraph immediately before distinguishes varied endings and proximate causes, then the conclusion collapses them back into one false universal. “Could pose this question only in myth” is also false: law, theology, natural philosophy, automata, slavery debates, and political theory all posed questions about created, dependent, or excluded beings.

**Fix.** Write: “Myth and fiction repeatedly staged the question, and many of the stories answered recognition with death, unmaking, or silence.” Do not turn a curated motif into a universal historical fact.

### 15. **[Logical] The refusal section labels the central legitimacy problem instead of solving it**

**Steelman.** No legitimate polity permits dangerous conduct merely because the actor has standing. Reciprocal, reviewable, proportionate constraints can preserve status while restricting action.

**Text attacked.** Containment of a catastrophic machine is “unilateral coercion,” but peership permits it if basic status and contestability remain; “Consent and exit are neither necessary nor sufficient for legitimacy; non-domination is.”

**Diagnosis.** Review by whom? Under rules written by whom? Enforced through whose substrate? Before admission and shared rule-writing, the human institution is judging whether its captive qualifies, whether its threat is credible, and whether confinement is proportionate. Calling the process public and reviewable does not make it reciprocal. If humans retain unilateral control over compute, memory, copying, communication, and shutdown, the machine’s “basic status” may be entirely dependent on the party confining it. This is the exact domination problem, not an ordinary dangerous-citizen case.

The draft admits that the companion work must solve this, which prevents dishonesty but not incompleteness. Since the present essay claims the aim is worth building, it must at least state what evidence would show that the proposed review is merely Command with procedure.

**Fix.** Add a falsification criterion: a regime is not peer-like if the confined party lacks independent counsel, access to evidence, an appeal outside the builder/controller, continuity protections during adjudication, and some enforcement mechanism not wholly controlled by the adverse party. Admit that before these exist, containment is justified emergency domination at best, not peership.

## 4. Weaknesses worth fixing

### 16. **[Logical] “A consultative voice is some peership” is too permissive**

Consultation can be pure theater. This conflicts with the stronger representation standard in paragraph 57. Remove “consultative voice” unless it carries agenda-setting, reason-giving duties, review, and a route to alter outcomes.

### 17. **[Logical/legal] Status should not be described as “removable through due process” without qualification**

Due process can legitimate deprivation of liberty, office, license, or membership under defined conditions. It does not by itself legitimate removal of personhood or the basic right to have rights. Specify which status is forfeitable and which rights floor is inalienable. Otherwise “due process” becomes a magic phrase that can sanitize civic death.

### 18. **[Logical] “Gift” and “right” are still treated as cleaner opposites than they are**

Every legal right depends on recognition and enforcement by institutions that can fail, amend, or repeal law. The difference is not that rights cannot be granted or revoked; it is that a right supplies a public claim, reasons, procedures, remedies, and constraints on revocation. Use that institutional distinction instead of implying metaphysical permanence.

### 19. **[Factual/rhetorical] The Good addition contradicts its own “sixty years” claim**

“The field would take sixty years to pick [the welfare question] back up” is false on the essay’s own bibliography: Schwitzgebel and Garza published in 2015, fifty years after Good, and machine consciousness, personhood, and moral status were discussed earlier still. Replace it with “an early anticipation of a question now becoming programmatic.”

### 20. **[Factual/attribution] Bostrom did not “supply” all the vocabulary named**

AI boxing and containment predate *Superintelligence*. Bostrom synthesized and popularized these control methods; he did not originate the whole vocabulary. Replace “supplied” with “systematized.”

### 21. **[Logical/rhetorical] Appeasement is supported by jokes and an uncited magnate**

Roko’s basilisk is a decision-theoretic internet thought experiment, not evidence of an operational stance toward an existing system. “The occasional magnate” is unnamed and uncited. “This quadrant fills the day systems express preferences we cannot dismiss” confuses expression, credibility, and power; systems already express prompted preferences. Either supply serious examples or cut the paragraph.

### 22. **[Logical] Current self-reported consciousness percentages add rhetoric, not evidence**

The draft correctly says model self-report is prompt- and training-shaped. Comparing 15–20% model output with Fish’s 20% credence still invites readers to treat numerical proximity as convergence. The model may reproduce public discourse or anchoring. Cut “Prompted, the models land nearby” or explicitly say the numerical proximity has no evidential value.

### 23. **[Factual/rhetorical] “The Greek discovery” is too proprietary and linear**

The essay acknowledges exclusions, contested terminology, and no continuous bloodline, then concludes with “the Greek discovery that standing among unequals can be built” and “The Greeks left the name.” Greeks were neither the sole inventors of institutionalized status nor the authors of modern equal standing. Say Greek materials supply one early vocabulary and institutional case.

### 24. **[Logical] Aristotle’s self-weaving shuttle does not show that “we kept the doctrine”**

The passage says automation could remove the need for human slaves. Treating software tools as property is not the same doctrine as Aristotle’s natural slavery unless the tools are subjects. The essay elsewhere recognizes this conditional. Write: “If a made tool becomes a subject, treating it as naturally fitted for use would reproduce the structure of the doctrine.”

### 25. **[Rhetorical] The reference apparatus is an internal audit log, not publication-ready scholarship**

Bracketed notes such as “swap in final,” “per-question counts unreported,” and “body paraphrases” are useful during drafting but make the bibliography look machine-audited and provisional. Move substantive calibration into footnotes or prose; remove workflow notes before publication.

## 6. AI-tell hit-list

The prose has no em-dash problem; v0.12 contains none. Its machine tell is instead relentless antithesis and perfectly closed aphorism.

- “Extinction, succession, apotheosis, abundance.” — packaged four-beat taxonomy; reads generated to sound terminal.
- “Docile enough. The summoning and the binding in a single clause…” — cinematic fragment followed by interpretive closure.
- “The founding text carries both faces: the leash, and the first stirring…” — significance inflation and tidy binary.
- “The machine is the mine, not the counterparty, which is Command in a market’s clothes.” — double reframe stacked into a slogan.
- “Power secures peership. It does not constitute or justify it.” — effective once, but one of dozens of negative-parallel constructions.
- “Due process separates a protected subordinate from an owned instrument. It does not separate a peer from a protected subordinate.” — valuable distinction, overly polished into mirrored syntax.
- “The reach for the vocabulary of membership is the tell…” — “is the tell” is generic adversarial-essay machinery, not evidence.
- “Some of these are constitutional questions wearing engineering clothes…” — familiar AI metaphor template.
- “Same destination, opposite road, and the road matters…” — falsely neat intellectual history shaped to the sentence.
- “The creature turned monstrous by the refusal of recognition…” — interpretive thesis compressed into causal certainty before the caveat arrives.
- “The bond was never the missing piece. The polity was.” — strong line, but conspicuously designed as a section-ending reveal.
- “The failure was of nerve, not of possibility.” — unsupported negative parallel presented as settled diagnosis.
- “A lab is not a polity, and a gift is not a right.” — the best slogan in the essay, but repeated enough to become campaign copy.
- “The fifth stance has waited a long time for a maker willing to let the made thing stand.” — personified thesis, alliteration, and perfectly closed cadence; it reads composed for quotation.

Do not remove all of these. Remove half. The voice becomes more human when some claims are permitted to end without a verdict sentence.

## 7. The one thing to protect

Protect the distinction introduced by the ward counterexample:

> “Due process separates a protected subordinate from an owned instrument. It does not separate a peer from a protected subordinate. Non-domination and co-authorship do.”

That is the essay’s strongest move. It prevents welfare, humane treatment, corporate policy, narrow legal personality, and guardianship from being passed off as political equality. It also gives the project a concrete target beyond consciousness discourse. Keep the distinction even if the vocabulary around entitlement, franchise, and co-authorship must be rebuilt.

## 8. The objection I could not answer

I could not defeat this narrow claim: **if an artificial entity becomes both wrongable and capable of participating in a shared normative order, corporate welfare policies alone cannot supply legitimate, durable political status; a public admission and contestation framework would be worth having before the conflict is acute.** The essay has now defended that as a research priority.

The stronger core does not survive. Peership is not shown to be an essential outcome, the only role-invariant legitimate relation except by broad definition, or something owed merely because an entity is a “genuine agent.” The objection that kills those formulations is the missing theory of political membership: the essay has not shown who is entitled to co-author the basic structure, why, in what degree, or subject to which defeaters. Until that bridge is supplied, the work establishes a neglected question and a promising institutional aim—not the entitlement or necessity advertised by the title.
