# Adversarial Integration Audit: *Peership* v0.8

**Draft reviewed:** `peership_draft_v0.8.md`  
**Primary question:** Does the new power/self-determination section solve the inconsistency between protected standing and peership?  
**Audit outcome:** **MITIGATED, NOT RESOLVED**

## 1. Verdict

V0.8 fixes the specific error in v0.7: it no longer calls publicly recognized, enforceable, independently reviewed status sufficient for peership. The sentence “Due process separates a protected subordinate from an owned instrument. It does not, by itself, separate a peer from a protected subordinate” is correct and should stay. But the proposed bridge fails: the essay first defines peership as standing independent of the power ratio, then defines it as the point where standing meets “the collective power to hold it.” It also moves without argument from individual machine, to machine franchise, to machine “people,” to mixed demos, to inter-polity self-determination; those are different constitutional subjects and different institutions. A hostile political theorist will say the old gap has been replaced by a contradiction, a circular definition of rights, and an unspecified bearer.

## 2. Fatal or serious problems

### 1. **(b) Logical/argumentative — The new bridge contradicts the essay’s defining claim**

> “Peership grants standing without reference to the power ratio.”

> “Peership is the rung where standing and the collective power to hold it meet.”

**Steelman:** Normative standing should not vary with strength, but rights that cannot be defended against revocation are politically hollow. The essay therefore adds countervailing power as the institutional condition that makes recognition durable.

**Strike:** That distinction is not made. The text says power is constitutive of peership, not merely necessary to secure it. On its own terms, a weak party lacking collective counterpower cannot be a peer. The status supposedly owed regardless of weakness is therefore withheld precisely because of weakness. This is the original power-ratio dependence in a new vocabulary.

**Fix:** Separate three propositions explicitly:

1. **Entitlement:** whether an entity merits equal standing does not depend on power.
2. **Institutional form:** peership consists in equal basic status, reciprocal accountability, and an effective opportunity to contest and revise common rules.
3. **Durability:** countervailing power, distributed enforcement, and exit make that status harder to violate.

Then replace “Peership is the rung where standing and collective power meet” with a sentence saying that power secures peership but does not constitute or justify it. Evidence cannot settle this; the essay needs a conceptual correction.

### 2. **(b) Logical/argumentative — The bearer of peership changes mid-argument**

> “Among humans this is why a *people* can hold a claim a solitary ward cannot.”

> “A machine that is a party to the constitution is a peer.”

> “The demos might one day have room for minds we make.”

**Steelman:** Copyable minds cannot be treated as ordinary atomized voters. The politically relevant bearer may have to be a constituted machine collective, not each process or model instance.

**Strike:** The essay never chooses. It alternates among at least four incompatible subjects:

- an individual artificial agent with rights;
- a model family or lineage;
- a machine “people” claiming collective self-determination;
- human and machine members of one mixed demos.

Individual franchise, group representation, internal autonomy, treaty equality between peoples, and constituent membership in a shared polity are not rungs on one ladder. They answer different questions. “Self-determination of peoples” does not bridge individual legal personality to individual suffrage. Nor does calling machines a people establish who belongs to that people, who speaks for it, or whether humans and machines share one constitution.

**Fix:** Add a subsection titled “Who is the party?” and state which constitutional model the essay defends. If the design is deliberately open, distinguish the models and claim only a principle common to them: no affected artificial constituency should remain wholly subject to rules it has no institutional route to contest. Do not use “a people,” “a party,” “members,” and “a mind” interchangeably.

### 3. **(b) Logical/argumentative — The account of rights is circular and false**

> “A right the grantor alone can withdraw is, whatever it is called, a permission; it becomes a right when the holder has enough standing that the withdrawal is no longer the grantor’s to make alone.”

**Steelman:** A company policy revocable by the same company is not an adequate right against that company. Rights require duties imposed by a rule-governed authority beyond the beneficiary’s goodwill.

**Strike:** The sentence confuses a right’s existence, legal entrenchment, enforcement, and the holder’s power. Statutory rights remain rights even when a legislature can repeal them. Constitutional rights remain rights even though constitutions can be amended. A child has rights without the power to defend them. An occupied people may possess a right to self-determination while lacking the power to realize it. “It becomes a right when the holder has enough standing” defines standing by rights and rights by standing. The phrase “enough standing” supplies no criterion.

The theory also produces a perverse result: the more powerless the claimant, the less capable it is of having rights. That is the opposite of why rights are needed.

**Fix:** Use a standard distinction:

- a **claim-right** correlates with another party’s duty;
- **legal validity** comes from recognized public rules;
- **remedial standing and enforcement** determine whether the claim can be vindicated;
- **entrenchment and counterpower** determine resilience.

The essay’s actual target is a privilege granted at will, not every defeasible legal right. Say that.

### 4. **(b) Logical/argumentative — Reciprocity and authorship still do not entail equal standing**

> “Peership is standing that runs both ways — the same rules bind both parties, each one’s standing underwrites the other’s, and revoking mine would cost you yours.”

> “Peership is standing to help *write and revise* the rules.”

**Steelman:** A peer is not merely protected by a superior; a peer participates as a co-author of the common legal order and can hold the other parties accountable under reciprocal rules.

**Strike:** These are plausible ingredients, not a criterion of equality. The same formally general law can bind ruler and subject while allocating radically unequal authority. Consultation, a reserved advisory seat, or a token vote gives a party some role in revision without making it a peer. Conversely, ordinary citizens do not individually write constitutions, and loss of one citizen’s status does not normally cost every other citizen theirs. “Revoking mine would cost you yours” sounds like mutual hostage-taking, not civic equality.

**Fix:** Define the political relation directly. At minimum specify:

- equal basic status or a justified rule for differentiated status;
- non-subordination: no party has unilateral authority to set the other’s basic terms;
- contestability and access to independent remedy;
- a non-token route to representation in rule formation and revision;
- reciprocal liability under public rules;
- a rule for membership, individuation, and exit.

Then admit that peership can be scalar and imperfect. “Some authorship” is not yet equality.

### 5. **(b) Logical/argumentative — The Bargain/Peership distinction is now cleaner locally and incoherent globally**

> “Bargain is standing *within* rules that someone else writes and can rewrite.”

> “Real bargains routinely persist across large power gaps…”

> “Four of these five postures are, at bottom, keyed to the power ratio … Bargain that the other side can hold up its end.”

**Steelman:** Bargain recognizes a counterparty but does not necessarily give that counterparty constituent authority. Peership adds co-authorship of the framework within which bargaining occurs.

**Strike:** Bargaining is a mechanism, not a rival constitutional status. Peers bargain with each other. Sovereign states bargain and jointly write treaty rules. Unequal parties can bargain under a neutral law. A contract can be negotiated between equal citizens; a constitution can itself be a bargain. Conversely, a citizen with a vote remains subject to rules they opposed. The revised type therefore overlaps peership instead of excluding it.

The power claim also no longer works. “Can hold up its end” is not a power ratio; it is minimal agency or capacity for performance. Peership equally requires parties capable of complying with and revising rules. Once Bargain is detached from parity, paragraph 33 cannot honestly count it as one of four power-keyed stances.

**Fix:** Stop presenting Bargain and Peership as mutually exclusive boxes. Make one of these choices:

- treat Bargain as a **mode of interaction** available inside Command, Peership, or interstate coexistence; or
- define the types by the **source and scope of authority**, then give necessary and sufficient conditions that prevent overlap.

Without that repair, the five-part repertoire is not a taxonomy.

### 6. **(b) Logical/argumentative — The reversal test survives only because the essay annexes every counterexample**

> “A universal rule that every dangerous agent, human or machine, is bound by constitutional constraints proportional to its capability… is not a rival to peership but its constitutional *form*.”

> “The arrangements that also survive reversal … are rungs and components of the thing I am calling peership, not alternatives to it.”

**Steelman:** Reciprocal constitutional constraint is exactly how equality under law is made operational. A symmetric competitor is therefore a form of the same political ideal rather than a distinct posture.

**Strike:** This makes the result true by definition. Any arrangement that passes reversal is absorbed into “peership”; the remaining four are defined by their failure. “Only peership passes” then says only the class of reversal-safe arrangements is reversal-safe. That is not comparative argument.

Bargain supplies a counterexample the essay has already conceded: a strong party can offer a fair, enforceable bargain under mutually authored rules. Many weaker parties would rationally accept that stance from a stronger one. A defender of capability-proportionate control can also say the restrictions are justified by risk, not by species or dominance, and apply to dangerous humans too. Calling that Peership does not defeat the alternative; it renames it.

**Fix:** Drop “only.” State that peership is one family of reciprocal constitutional arrangements and that the reversal test screens out naked domination but does not uniquely select a complete institution. Then compare peership against at least two surviving alternatives without definitional annexation: protected representation without franchise, and reciprocal capability-based constitutionalism.

### 7. **(b) Logical/argumentative — Moral status returns as the admission criterion**

> “The threshold for *being a party* … is closer to the question the welfare researchers are already asking — is this the kind of thing that has interests and can be wronged…”

> “Every bit of this is moral-status work… Peership is the politics of the party.”

**Steelman:** Political standing need not wait for proof of phenomenal consciousness. It can be grounded in agency, interests, vulnerability to governance, or precaution under uncertainty.

**Strike:** “Has interests and can be wronged” is a moral-status test. The essay has not escaped it; it has made it the threshold for political membership. Legal personality without inner life, such as corporate personality, is instrumental and does not yield peership. If full political standing is claimed in the entity’s own right, the essay needs some account of whose interests receive representation and why. Otherwise any optimization process, company, botnet, or strategically prompted model can demand constituent power.

The distinction between moral status and political standing is real, but not independence. Political standing can exceed or operationalize moral status; it still needs a constituency principle. The children and corporation examples show this dependence, not its absence.

**Fix:** Identify separate grounds:

- **intrinsic standing** based on welfare or morally considerable interests;
- **agential standing** based on being governed, capable of claims, and liable under rules;
- **instrumental legal personality** created to serve human institutions.

Then say which ground supports which rung and what uncertainty policy applies. Do not claim that welfare inquiry and political admission are disconnected.

### 8. **(a) Factual/citation — The UN self-determination claim is overtranslated**

> “Self-determination — recognized in the UN Charter and in the common first article of the two human-rights covenants as the right of a *people* not to be governed without its consent…”

> “…which is the whole content of self-determination as an *anti*-domination principle.”

**Steelman:** Collective self-determination offers an existing anti-subordination model in which a people is not merely protected but participates in setting its political terms.

**Strike:** The instruments do not use the essay’s formula. Common Article 1 says peoples “freely determine their political status and freely pursue their economic, social and cultural development.” UN Charter Article 1(2) identifies respect for equal rights and self-determination of peoples as a purpose. Neither text defines the right simply as non-government without consent, still less as machine constituent power. The beneficiaries are “peoples”; the scope of that term, the distinction between internal and external self-determination, and any right to secession are contested. The right also concerns resources and development, so anti-domination is not its “whole content.” See the [OHCHR text of the ICCPR](https://www.ohchr.org/en/instruments-mechanisms/instruments/international-covenant-civil-and-political-rights), the [OHCHR text of the ICESCR](https://www.ohchr.org/en/instruments-mechanisms/instruments/international-covenant-economic-social-and-cultural-rights), and [UN Charter Article 1](https://www.un.org/en/about-us/un-charter/chapter-1).

**Fix:** Present self-determination as an analogy, quote the actual wording, and cite political theory on non-domination or constituent power for the stronger interpretation. Add one sentence admitting that extending “peoplehood” to artificial entities is the argument to be made, not a premise supplied by international law.

### 9. **(a) Factual/intellectual history — Hobbes is not “Aristotle’s heir with the theology removed”**

> “Hobbes is Aristotle’s heir with the theology removed: unequal, dangerous beings can be governed only by a sovereign power that overawes them all.”

**Steelman:** Both thinkers justify hierarchy as a response to disorder, and Hobbes supplies the centralized-sovereign version of Command.

**Strike:** This collapses opposite premises. Aristotle naturalizes hierarchy and natural slavery. Hobbes’s state of nature begins from rough natural equality: even the weakest can kill the strongest, and equal vulnerability produces insecurity. Hobbes rejects Aristotle’s zoon politikon and builds sovereignty through covenant, not natural mastery. He can be recruited as a theorist of overwhelming sovereign command, but not described as Aristotle’s heir without explaining the reversal.

**Fix:** Write: “Hobbes rejects Aristotle’s natural hierarchy but reaches a centralized answer from the opposite premise: roughly equal and mutually dangerous individuals authorize a sovereign able to overawe them all.” Cite *Leviathan* chapters 13, 17, and 20. This correction improves rather than weakens the genealogy.

### 10. **(b) Logical/AI-alignment — The failure model names problems and then trivializes them**

> “Sybil resistance, captured-referee detection, enforcement across a power gap are engineering questions with the ordinary character of engineering questions, not paradoxes.”

**Steelman:** These are concrete design problems, which is better than treating peership as a frictionless moral aspiration. Institutions routinely manage identity, capture, and asymmetric power imperfectly.

**Strike:** The claim is unsupported and probably false in the cases that matter. Sybil resistance for costless, forkable agents is partly a constitutional question about what counts as a person, not merely an engineering problem. A captured referee is a political legitimacy problem. Enforcement against an entity that can defeat every enforcement mechanism may be physically impossible under some power distributions. No institutional design guarantees nondomination when one party controls the substrate, copies, communications, or coercive capacity of the other. Calling these “ordinary” quietly assumes feasibility immediately after conceding it is unproven.

**Fix:** Call them socio-technical constitutional problems, some potentially insoluble beyond specified capability ratios. State the failure condition: if no arrangement can preserve mutual veto, credible exit, or distributed enforcement, peership may remain normatively desirable but institutionally unavailable. Then the essay can honestly argue for research without pretending every obstacle has an engineering-shaped solution.

### 11. **(c) Rhetorical/register — The conclusion remains a promissory note and a veiled project announcement**

> “The companion work argues *how*.”

> “The peer institution does not exist; someone has to make it…”

**Steelman:** A programmatic essay may establish a neglected aim while reserving institutional design for a separate work.

**Strike:** The essay repeatedly points offstage exactly where objections become concrete: membership, copying, enforcement, transition, and legitimacy. It asks the reader to endorse “the thing” before specifying what the thing is, then hints that the author’s next project supplies it. A skeptical reader will feel recruited into a brand funnel, not brought to a philosophical conclusion.

**Fix:** Either disclose the proposed institution in one paragraph with its minimal architecture, or strip the promotional references and end with a research agenda containing falsifiable design requirements. “Worth building” is underdeterminate until the reader knows whether the proposal is a mixed demos, machine autonomy, group representation, or a treaty between polities.

## 3. Weaknesses worth fixing

### 1. **(a) Factual/citation — The franchise-capacity repair overstates democratic practice**

> “Mature democracies do not in fact gate the vote on individualized capacity… and adults with profound cognitive disabilities keep the franchise.”

Some democracies and subnational jurisdictions still restrict voting through guardianship, judicial incapacity findings, criminal disenfranchisement, citizenship, residency, and registration rules. The trend may support universal adult suffrage, but the universal descriptive claim is false. Cite a comparative source and write “many mature democracies reject individualized cognitive testing, and international disability-rights doctrine increasingly opposes it.” Then confront the hard machine analogue: any anti-Sybil system will need an individuation and eligibility rule even if it avoids intelligence tests.

### 2. **(b) Logical/argumentative — “Every bit” of AI-welfare work is an overclaim**

> “Every bit of this is moral-status work.”

Long et al. include robust agency as well as consciousness; preference, autonomy, treatment, and company governance can have political implications. The narrower claim survives: the cited programs do not establish public, enforceable political standing. Replace “Every bit” with “The center of gravity is moral status and corporate practice, not constituent standing.”

### 3. **(b) Logical/argumentative — The Command track record mixes analogy, fiction, and evidence**

> “Durable control over a capable population is the aim with the worst record in human history.”

> “The control theorists’ own literature concedes the fragility…”

“Worst record” is unmeasurable. Morgan does not establish that control costs scale with the capability of the controlled. Scott does not by himself establish a universal law that bound parties corrupt all upward information. Shelley is literary interpretation, not a track record. Christiano (2019) is one scenario essay, which the reference note itself admits, not “the literature.” Split empirical claims, theoretical mechanisms, and literary anticipations; cite actual work on monitoring costs, preference falsification, principal-agent failure, resistance, and organizational silence.

### 4. **(b) Logical/argumentative — Ostrom supports polycentric governance, not peership as defined here**

> “The demonstration that self-governance among unequals, without an owner or a Leviathan, is a real and studied thing…”

Ostrom’s cases concern bounded common-pool resources, repeated interaction, legible members, and enforceable local sanctions. They do not demonstrate equal constitutional standing between species, sovereigns, or agents with arbitrarily asymmetric capabilities. The essay now admits most of this, which is an improvement. The remaining fix is to call Ostrom a source of design hypotheses, not a “record” of peership, and to cite modern comparative evidence before asserting that the same grammar recurs across town meetings, participatory budgeting, mutual aid, and worker cooperatives.

### 5. **(b) Logical/argumentative — The Neanderthal section is now cautious enough to be harmless and too weak to carry weight**

> “Interbreeding and even cultural continuity did not keep the Neanderthals in existence as a distinct people. Contact was not preservation.”

The causal institutional claim has been removed. Good. What remains is almost tautological: demographic absorption did not preserve a distinct population as distinct. “People” is still a modern political category imposed on heterogeneous Pleistocene populations, and nothing in the evidence connects their disappearance to lack of standing. Keep the section only as a warning that merger narratives require a criterion of identity continuity. Do not count it as a track record for or against peership.

The current site descriptions are materially better. Goyet supports nutritional cannibalism and treats inter-Neanderthal predation as more likely than a *Homo sapiens* predator, while still leaving motivation uncertain; therefore “evidence of violence” should be softened to “evidence of anthropogenic butchery and probable exocannibalism.” See the [2025 *Scientific Reports* study](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-24460-3).

### 6. **(c) Rhetorical/register — The opening still polices seriousness**

> “Many serious researchers now assign a non-negligible probability…”

“Serious” is needless status sorting. The cited survey can establish a distribution of forecasts; it cannot define who counts as serious. “The argument has largely moved” is also a field-wide trend claim unsupported by two surveys that ask different questions. Replace with the actual result: “A substantial share of surveyed researchers assigns non-negligible probability…”

### 7. **(a) Factual/intellectual history — The Greek machinery remains too neatly aligned**

> “Athens built machinery to make [equal standing] operational among unequals — many offices filled by lot, the *euthyna* audit…, liturgies…”

Sortition and accountability are relevant. Liturgies were elite fiscal/civic obligations, not straightforward machinery of equal legal standing. “Isonomia before, or alongside, demokratia” is safer than the old chronological claim, but Herodotus alone does not establish lexical or institutional priority. Cite modern scholarship on *isonomia*, and do not imply that Alcidamas, Stoic cosmopolis, Athenian citizenship, Locke, Hume, suffrage, Ostrom, and machine peership form one continuous intellectual lineage.

### 8. **(c) Rhetorical/register — The relational-school paragraph attacks a “fast-growing” field on three preprints**

> “A fast-growing set of mostly unrefereed preprints…”

Three repository manuscripts do not establish a fast-growing school. One has AI-assisted or collective authorship and another is a self-described manifesto; their existence is verifiable, but their representativeness and scholarly weight are not. Call them “three recent unrefereed examples” and attack the argument, not the alleged movement. Also verify Soyoung You’s displayed authorship against the deposited PDF, which identifies “Sovereign Potato and the Companions.”

### 9. **(b) Logical/argumentative — The essay lacks a transition theory**

The final polity cannot legitimately arise by assuming the very artificial constituency it is meant to constitute. Before membership, humans necessarily choose initial thresholds, identity rules, and safeguards. That transitional guardianship may look like the unilateral Command the essay condemns. Add a staged account: precautionary legal personality, independent representation, trial participation, contestable admission criteria, and constitutional revision after admission. Without it, the proposal jumps from corporate property to co-constituent.

### 10. **(c) Rhetorical/register — The draft note should not appear in a deposited essay**

> “Adds the power / self-determination bridge… answering the standing audit’s central objection…”

This is revision metadata, not prose for readers. It advertises the model-audit process and tells critics what the revision supposedly solved before they assess it. Move it to version history or repository notes.

## 6. The one thing to protect

Protect this sentence and the distinction behind it:

> “Due process separates a protected subordinate from an owned instrument. It does not, by itself, separate a peer from a protected subordinate.”

That is the strongest move in v0.8. It concedes the v0.7 objection cleanly, preserves the useful ladder, and identifies the real political question: what converts protection into non-subordination and constituent standing? Do not retreat to “held, enforceable, independently reviewed” as a complete definition. The authorship distinction—counterparty within rules versus participant in revising rules—is also useful once presented as one dimension rather than a complete taxonomy.

## 7. The objection I could not answer

The strongest modest claim survives: **if an artificial system becomes a genuine agent with interests of its own and is persistently governed by coercive common rules, discretionary corporate welfare is not an adequate final status; independent legal protection, contestability, and some route to political representation become serious candidates.** The essay has made that problem difficult to dismiss.

The stronger core does **not** survive. The objection that kills it is the contradiction between power-independent standing and power-constituted peership, compounded by the undefined bearer of collective self-determination. Until the essay separates entitlement from durability and says whether the peer is an individual agent, a machine people, or a member of a mixed demos, “peership” is not yet a coherent institutional outcome. It is a name covering several incompatible ones.
