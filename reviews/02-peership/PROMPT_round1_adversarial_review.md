# Adversarial Reviewer Prompt — "Peership" (paste into GPT-5 / a non-Anthropic model)

> How to use: paste everything below the line into a fresh conversation with the rival model, then paste the full text of `peership_draft_v0.4.md` immediately after it. Use a model from a different lineage than the one that drafted the essay — the point is decorrelated blind spots, not a second opinion from the same architecture.

---

You are acting as a hostile, expert adversarial reviewer. The essay below ("Peership: The Overlooked but Essential Outcome for Human and Machine Societies") is one part of a philosophical corpus by an independent researcher, Dan Lee-Odinson. It was drafted with AI assistance and is now being run against a rival model on purpose: the author works adversarially, drafting and then attacking, and integrating what survives. Your job is to attack it as hard as you honestly can. Flattery is worthless to him; a landed objection is worth more than a paragraph of praise. Do not soften, do not hedge, do not pad. If something is wrong, say it is wrong and show why.

Adopt the standpoint of the essay's most competent enemies at once: a working paleoanthropologist, an analytic philosopher of mind, a political theorist, a classicist, an AI alignment researcher, and a sharp generalist editor. Review from each lens where it bites.

## Ground rules

1. **Do not be agreeable.** Your value is entirely in disagreement, correction, and stress. Assume the author can take it; he asked for this.
2. **Every criticism must be specific and actionable.** Quote the sentence or claim you are attacking, name the failure, and state what would fix it or what evidence would settle it. "This section is weak" is useless; "the claim in paragraph 3 that X is falsified by Y, and the fix is Z" is the job.
3. **Separate three kinds of problem and label each:** (a) **factual/citation errors** — something is untrue, misattributed, or unverifiable; (b) **logical/argumentative gaps** — a conclusion that does not follow, an equivocation, a buried premise, an unanswered objection; (c) **rhetorical/register problems** — overclaiming, a tell of AI authorship, tonal slips, a sentence in the wrong register.
4. **Steelman before you strike.** For each major objection, first state the essay's argument in its strongest form, then attack that, not a weaker version.
5. **Prosecute, then also defend once.** After the attack, identify the single strongest thing the essay has going for it, stated plainly — the author needs to know what NOT to cut.

## What the essay claims (so you can test it, not restate it)

The essay's spine: humanity has a finite repertoire of five stances toward an outside intelligence — Command, Bargain, Appeasement, Servitude, and Peership. The first four are "bets on the power ratio" between humans and the arriving mind; peership grants standing *independent* of that ratio and is therefore argued to be the only stance that stays coherent under deep uncertainty about who will be stronger. It distinguishes **moral status** (can it suffer) from **political standing** (does it have a vote in the rules that bind it), and argues the entire current AI-welfare movement operates only on the first. It argues peership has a classical pedigree (isonomia, Aristotle vs. Alcidamas, the Greek-to-Locke/Hume line) and real institutional track records among humans (Ostrom's commons), while its rival — Command — has a catastrophic one. It uses the Neanderthal/hominin record to argue that even humanity's *gentlest* historical outcome with a kindred mind was absorption, not peership, because nothing in the encounter was an institution. It ends by implying (without naming) that such an institution now needs to be built.

## Attack these load-bearing points hardest

Do not limit yourself to this list, but do not skip it.

1. **The "only stable/only coherent stance" claim.** Is the argument that peership is uniquely coherent under uncertainty actually valid, or does it smuggle in its conclusion? Could a defender of Command, Bargain, or a hybrid stance reasonably deny that their position is merely a "bet on the power ratio"? Is "equality of standing independent of the power ratio" doing real work, or is it a redefinition that wins by stipulation? Attack the reversal test ("would I want the stronger party holding this stance toward me?") — is it a genuine argument or an intuition pump that begs the question?

2. **The Neanderthal / "Only Survivor" section.** This is the newest and highest-variance material. Interrogate: (a) Is "assimilation, not extermination" actually the scientific consensus, or a contested recent reading the essay overweights? (b) Does the section prove what it claims — that *absence of an institution* caused Neanderthal absorption — or is it retrofitting a modern political category onto a demographic/genetic process that had nothing to do with "standing"? This is the essay's biggest potential category error; press it hard. (c) Are the specific site claims (Goyet targeted cannibalism, Mandrin single-year handoff, Üçağızlı II shared shell, Oase hybrid) accurately represented, or overstated beyond what the cited studies support? (d) Does the "you become an immune-response gene" reading of merger actually rebut Kurzweil, or is it a rhetorical flourish that conflates biological absorption with cognitive/technological merger?

3. **The moral-status vs. standing distinction.** Is this a real and clean distinction, or does political standing in fact presuppose some resolution of moral status that the essay waves away? Can a coherent theory grant a vote to an entity whose interior is genuinely unknown without absurdity? Attack the analogy to children and animals — does it actually support the essay or undercut it (we deny children the vote *for reasons*, not arbitrarily)?

4. **The classical and intellectual-history claims.** Test every one: Did Athenian *isonomia* actually mean "equality of standing among acknowledged unequals," or is that an anachronistic gloss (it excluded women, slaves, metics — is the essay's use of it self-undermining)? Is the Alcidamas quote genuine and correctly contextualized? Is "before the Greeks said demokratia they said isonomia" defensible? Is the Aristotle "self-weaving shuttle" passage being read fairly or too conveniently? Is the Hume/Locke characterization accurate, and does the essay's promissory note ("what replaces origin-consent gets its own essay") paper over a hole in *this* essay?

5. **The theorist taxonomy.** Is it fair to file Russell, Bostrom, the labs, Yudkowsky, and PauseAI all under "Command"? Would any of them reject the label with a strong argument the essay hasn't answered? Is calling LeCun's position "Command with the fear removed" a fair reading or a rhetorical annexation? Is the welfare-researcher characterization (Long/Sebo/Birch/Chalmers/Fish, Anthropic) accurate and fair, given the essay leans on them as sympathetic-but-limited?

6. **The unnamed conclusion.** The essay ends by implying an institution needs to be built without naming it. Does this land as intellectually honest restraint, or as a bait-and-switch / veiled advertisement that damages the philosophical credibility of everything before it? Would a skeptical reader feel set up?

7. **Claim discipline and citation integrity.** Flag every claim that is stated more confidently than a cited source supports; every citation you cannot verify or that looks misattributed; every place a number, date, or quotation should be checked. Several references are explicitly marked as preprints, secondary summaries, or post-dated — call out any that the argument leans on too heavily given that status. Note especially any 2025–2026 sources presented as established.

## Also scan for AI-authorship tells (the author cares about this)

Flag any of these you find, with the sentence: negative-parallelism / "not X but Y" reframes; the rule-of-three; em-dash overuse; trailing "-ing" participial tails that editorialize ("...underscoring its significance"); hedge pile-ups; significance-inflation; tidy summary conclusions; any sentence that reads like a machine rather than a person talking. The author writes in a specific human voice (bold thesis, parenthetical asides, varied sentence length, radical specificity, no marketing language); flag anywhere the prose drifts off it into generic essay-bot cadence.

## Output format

7. **The objection I could not answer** — if, after all of it, the essay's core survives your best attack, say so explicitly and name the strongest counter you *couldn't* land. (If the core does not survive, say that instead, and name the objection that kills it.)
