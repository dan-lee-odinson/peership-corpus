# Integration Audit: *Peership* Draft v0.7

**Baseline:** Draft v0.5, the prior integration audit, and the same hostile-integration standard used in the previous round.

## 1. Integration verdict

Draft v0.7 is a substantive improvement: it retires the false engineering/conceptual firewall, separates relational postures from creation policy and ontology, corrects most anthropology references, and states a non-trivial institutional minimum. The Round 1 circularity objection no longer kills the entire essay because the thesis has moved from unique correctness to a defeasible call to build. It still bites against the claim that peership is “the only” acceptable aim, because the reversal result remains manufactured by defining four types as power-dependent and the fifth as symmetrical. The Neanderthal repair remains dishonest: the text disclaims institutional causation, then says the unbuilt institution is exactly what “would be enough.” The essay now survives as a manifesto for non-vendor-controlled legal recognition, but it has not shown that this principle amounts to **peership** rather than ordinary rule-of-law protection.

## 2. Disclosure audit

### 1. Central thesis → “worth building,” not “worth choosing”

**MITIGATED** — the thesis is genuinely narrower and no longer depends on proving unique coherence, but “its aim is the only one of the five we could live under from either side” preserves the same stipulated exclusivity as a manifesto verdict.

### 2. Taxonomy → transhistorical ideal types on a separate axis

**MITIGATED** — calling the types non-exhaustive and separating relation, creation policy, and ontology is a real repair, but the draft immediately re-annexes Yudkowsky and LeCun through an unsupported counterfactual claim that both “fall back” into Command if an entity arrives.

### 3. Minimum peership rung → held status, enforceability, independent review

**MITIGATED** — the revision finally states an institutional minimum, but that minimum describes due process and non-unilateral revocability, which protected nonpeers can possess; it is necessary for peership, not “the whole difference” between peer and instrument.

### 4. Engineering/conceptual asymmetry removed

**RESOLVED** — paragraph 55 explicitly says identity, copying, franchise, and enforcement are not mere implementation details and disclaims present feasibility, so peership no longer receives a special exemption denied to its rivals.

### 5. Neanderthal section → bounded warning

**COSMETIC** — “I am *not* claiming ... that any institution would have prevented it” is contradicted two sentences later by “What would be enough is exactly the unbuilt thing this essay is arguing for,” which restores the unsupported counterfactual in explicit form.

### 6. Sequel dependence → manifesto/companion-work division

**MITIGATED** — changing the thesis to a call for work makes deferral more legitimate, but “worth building” still presupposes at least plausible subjecthood and feasibility, while the draft expressly declines both and assigns them to later papers.

### 7. Artistic/register choices retained

**MITIGATED** — most manifesto cadence is the author’s legitimate call, but “We have tried every other posture on each other and buried the results” still asserts historical exhaustiveness after the taxonomy has been declared non-exhaustive, and “the fifth stance has waited” turns an unbuilt proposal into destiny.

## 3. Newly broken or newly exposed

### 1. The minimum criterion is not peership

> “The irreducible peership claim ... [is] that the entity’s status is held, not granted at pleasure ... backed by an enforceable claim and independent review.”

This is a good rule-of-law principle. It does not establish peer status. Children, foreign nationals, corporations, wards, prisoners, protected animals, and property owners can hold legally enforceable claims subject to independent review while lacking equal civic standing. A slave can even possess limited enforceable rights without becoming a peer. The criterion distinguishes legal protection from owner discretion; it does not distinguish peer from protected subordinate.

The essay admits this by saying legal personality merely makes a system “closer to peership.” That means the announced “irreducible” property is not sufficient after all.

**Fix:** State it as a necessary threshold. Add the feature that makes the relationship peer-like: reciprocal public status, equal protection, presumptive participation, nondomination, or another defended incident. Do not use “the whole difference.”

### 2. The revision may have eliminated the need for a novel polity

If private-law personality that is enforceable against a maker satisfies the structural minimum, ordinary legislation and courts can provide it. Salib and Goldstein’s proposal already sketches that route using corporate personhood, contract, property, and tort. The essay simultaneously says this is a peership move and that “no such institution exists today.”

Either existing private-law institutions can be extended, in which case the missing object is an amendment to ordinary law rather than a new demos; or peership requires more than private-law personality, in which case the minimum remains undefined.

**Fix:** Decide whether the project is legal extension, constitutional status, or a new polity. They are different institutional programs.

### 3. “Held, not granted” is conceptually careless

Legal status is granted or recognized by a legal order and can be revised through legislation, adjudication, or constitutional amendment. What matters is not metaphysical nongranting or absolute irrevocability; it is that an interested private party cannot revoke status unilaterally and that removal follows public law and due process.

**Fix:** Use the precise claim already latent in the paragraph: *recognized by public law, enforceable against the maker, and removable only through independent due process*.

### 4. The franchise-capacity analogy is inaccurate and dangerous

> “Those are qualifications of the same kind we already use for the franchise.”

Modern democracies generally use citizenship, residence, and age proxies, not individualized tests of whether adults understand rules or possess stable interests. Adults with severe cognitive disabilities often retain voting rights. Literacy and competence tests have a notorious history as exclusion devices. The draft invokes the historical abuse of “not ready” immediately after proposing a new readiness test.

**Fix:** Separate direct franchise from representation and legal personality. If individualized capacity review is retained, specify the decision-maker, burden of proof, appeal, periodic reassessment, and safeguards against vendor or state capture.

### 5. The target of the proposed institution is still unstable

The essay alternates among current language models, future robust agents, “machine minds,” and any system granted legal personality. Current model-welfare precautions do not purport to be constitutional status, so calling their revocability “inadequate” is meaningful only relative to a future rights-bearing subject they may not yet contain.

**Fix:** State the proposal prospectively: *if and when a system meets specified agency or interest criteria, its status determination must leave the vendor’s unilateral control*. Do not treat exploratory welfare safeguards as failed constitutions.

### 6. The reversal test still wins by construction

Command, Bargain, Appeasement, and Servitude are defined as asymmetric power postures; peership is defined as ratio-independent. The result that only peership “reads the same from both ends” therefore remains true by stipulation. Downgrading it from proof to “a real reason” prevents circularity from killing the essay, but it does not make the comparison informative.

There is also an omitted counterexample: a universal rule that all dangerous agents—human or machine—face capability-proportionate constitutional constraints can be endorsed from either side. It contains control without opportunistic domination. Because the draft admits real positions mix, this hybrid cannot be excluded by definition.

**Fix:** Compare peership with reciprocal constitutional constraint, representation without franchise, and private-law personality—not only with pure hostile ideal types.

### 7. Bargain is still rigged to fail

The type is defined as depending on rough parity, then rejected because parity may break. Real bargains persist across large power inequalities when law, coalition, repeated exchange, reputational costs, or asymmetric dependence make breach costly. Enforceability requires legal institutions; it does not require equal civic standing.

The revision correctly says Salib and Goldstein remain on private-law rungs, but then calls placing a machine “on the ladder” the move the whole essay is about. That collapses Bargain into the revised minimum of peership and leaves the distinction unstable.

**Fix:** Define Bargain by transactional recognition rather than parity. Explain what peership adds beyond durable private-law capacity.

### 8. The taxonomy repair still contains speculative annexation

> “the abstentionist ... and the skeptic ... both fall back on treating it as a hazard ... or a tool.”

This is not evidence about Yudkowsky or LeCun; it is a counterfactual prediction written to keep them near Command. Yudkowsky would probably classify an unprevented system as a hazard, but LeCun could update his ontology in response to the very evidence stipulated by the hypothetical. The sentence makes the map unfalsifiable: deny the Other and the essay predicts Command; accept it and any safety constraint also becomes Command.

**Fix:** Stop after separating the axes. Do not classify hypothetical future reactions without primary evidence.

### 9. The comparison is still ideal peership versus actual domination

Command receives plantations, colonialism, surveillance, revolt, and deceptive alignment. Peership receives its normative aim plus Ostrom’s best cases, while capture, oligarchy, exclusion, free-riding, and failed commons are acknowledged but not made part of its “record.” An unbuilt machine polity cannot be credited with avoiding failure modes merely because its definition forbids them.

**Fix:** Compare ideals to ideals and institutions to institutions. Give peership a failure model: formal equality masking vendor control, copy-based oligarchy, captured review, strategic refusal, or unenforceable rights.

### 10. The Neanderthal section still contradicts its own evidentiary limits

The draft correctly distinguishes sequential occupation, possible cultural continuity, regional behavioural uniformity, and admixture. It then calls the contact “gentle—exchange, learning, children,” calls Neanderthals “teachers,” and says informal bonds have a poor preservation record. Direct teaching is not established at Mandrin or Üçağızlı II, and one extinct lineage is not a comparative “record.”

“Every one of those populations is gone” is also overbroad after the paragraph has discussed multiple *H. sapiens* populations whose relationship to later groups is not established.

**Fix:** Remove “teachers,” “learning” as fact, “every one,” and “poor record.” The supportable analogy is one sentence: interbreeding and possible cultural continuity did not guarantee Neanderthal lineage persistence.

The ledger has shrunk from eight moves to five and is less evasive because v0.7 openly presents itself as a manifesto. The remaining problem is the verb **build**. “Worth researching” requires a serious problem and a plausible direction; this essay supplies both. “Worth building” implies that the object is coherent enough to construct and likely enough to justify costs. The essay expressly withholds that showing. Change the claim to “worth developing and testing” or provide a minimum feasibility sketch.

## 6. Does the core survive now?

Yes, more clearly than in v0.5—but the surviving core is not yet the title concept at full strength.

The defensible non-trivial thesis is: **if a machine system qualifies for legal or political status, that status should not remain unilaterally revocable by the vendor that benefits from denying it; it should be recognized through public rules, enforceable against the maker, and reviewable by an independent institution. Designing and testing such arrangements is worth doing before capability changes force the question.** The revision now argues that thesis honestly.

The strongest objection is that this is a principle of **public legal recognition and due process**, not yet peership. Nonpeers can possess enforceable, independently reviewed rights, and the essay has not identified the additional feature that converts protected status into equality of standing. Until it supplies that bridge, v0.7 justifies building a rights regime for possible machine agents; it does not yet justify the stronger language of peers, a demos, or “equal standing under law.”
