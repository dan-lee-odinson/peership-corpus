# Adversarial Integration Audit: *Peership* v0.9

**Draft reviewed:** `peership_draft_v0.9.md`  
**Primary test:** Did v0.9 solve the standing/peership contradiction identified in v0.8?  
**Audit outcome:** **YES on that contradiction; NO on the full comparative argument**

## 1. Verdict

V0.9 genuinely solves the standing-versus-peership inconsistency. “Power secures peership. It does not constitute or justify it” cleanly separates normative entitlement from institutional durability, while the four-part structural definition finally distinguishes a peer from a protected subordinate. The largest remaining vulnerability has moved downstream: the essay claims it can specify an admission threshold while conceding that the relevant interior may be permanently unknowable, then asserts without argument that a paperclip maximizer has objectives but no interests. Its second major failure is taxonomic: Bargain is now correctly described as a cross-cutting mode that can exist inside peership or between sovereigns, yet the essay still counts Bargain among four rival postures defeated by reversal. A hostile expert should now accept that peership is a coherent political ideal worth investigating; they should not accept that it is uniquely robust under uncertainty, that the proposed threshold is usable, or that a “provisional offer” with exit solves founding legitimacy and alignment risk.

## 2. Fatal or serious problems

### 1. **(b) Logical/argumentative — Bargain is both a cross-cutting mode and a defeated rival**

> “Bargain is better read as a mode that can run inside Command, inside peership, or between sovereigns, than as a rung that excludes them.”

> “What fails the test are the four postures that cannot survive their own inversion.”

> “Bargain inverted is standing at the mercy of rules we do not write.”

**Steelman:** Bargaining as a mechanism can occur anywhere, but the ideal type “Bargain” names the thinner status of a counterparty who can transact without sharing constituent authority. That subordinate form fails reversal even though peer bargains do not.

**Strike:** The essay never marks that shift in meaning. In paragraph 21, Bargain includes dealings “between sovereigns,” where neither side necessarily writes the other’s background rules. In paragraphs 59–63, Bargain is redefined as standing under rules unilaterally written by someone else. The argument defeats only **subordinate bargain**, a subtype manufactured for the test, while conceding that reciprocal peer bargain survives it. Bargain therefore cannot remain one of “four rival postures.”

This is not cosmetic. The essay’s stated thesis is that four rivals fail a test peership passes. Its own revised taxonomy reduces the rival postures to three and treats Bargain as an interaction mode orthogonal to status.

**Fix:** Choose one:

1. Remove Bargain from the list of rival statuses. Present Command, Peership, and Servitude as status relations; Appeasement as a strategic response; Bargain as an interaction mode available within or between them.
2. Retain a five-type repertoire but rename the defeated type **Dependent Bargain** and define it explicitly as transactional standing without constituent standing.

Then change every “four rivals” and “only one of five” claim. The current central comparison is formally inconsistent.

### 2. **(b) Logical/argumentative — The admission threshold is asserted, not specified**

> “We can leave the *form* open while fixing the *threshold*.”

> “Is this the kind of thing that has interests of its own and can be wronged, and can it act as a party…?”

> “The paperclip maximizer has no interests that can be wronged, only an objective that can be frustrated.”

**Steelman:** The institution need not predict whether an artificial subject is singular, collective, embodied, or distributed. It can use form-neutral criteria: morally considerable interests plus the capacities needed for political agency.

**Strike:** That is a list of disputed properties, not a fixed threshold. The essay supplies no theory distinguishing an “interest” from a stable objective, preference, policy, or disposition. A functionalist can treat persistent goals as interests. A phenomenalist can deny that either a language model or a paperclip maximizer has welfare without consciousness. An interpretationist can attribute interests when a system’s behavior is best explained in intentional terms. The essay simply announces the verdict in its favorite test case.

The epistemic problem is worse than admitted. A strategic system may imitate every behavioral marker of wrongable interests; a genuine subject may fail human conversational tests; a distributed subject may have no stable report channel. If the interior “may never be settled from outside,” the framework cannot claim to know that the paperclip maximizer is outside. It needs a decision rule under uncertainty, including false-positive and false-negative costs.

**Fix:** Replace “fixing the threshold” with “fixing a threshold procedure.” Specify:

- which theory or plural set of indicators matters;
- who evaluates the evidence and who can appeal;
- whether the presumption favors admission, exclusion, or staged status;
- how strategic mimicry and anthropomorphic bias are handled;
- what evidence triggers reevaluation;
- whether political agency alone can justify standing for instrumental reasons even without welfare.

Do not use the paperclip maximizer as an easy exclusion until the essay argues why frustrated objectives cannot constitute interests.

### 3. **(b) Logical/argumentative — The essay reintroduces the capacity test it says it avoids**

> “The threshold is not a test of form and not a merit exam.”

> “Can it act as a party (understand rules, make claims, bear liability)?”

**Steelman:** Political membership requires some capacity for agency, but this is a categorical qualification for being a participant, not an incumbent-administered intelligence or worthiness exam.

**Strike:** “Understand rules, make claims, bear liability” is a capacity test. Calling it form-agnostic does not change that. It also excludes the essay’s own analogical cases: children, severely cognitively disabled adults, and many represented persons cannot satisfy all three conditions, yet the essay invokes them to show that standing is not earned by power or competence.

The threshold conflates two grounds that must be separated:

- a **moral patient** may have interests and be wrongable but lack political agency;
- a **political agent** may negotiate, comply, and bear liability without being conscious or welfare-bearing.

The first supports protection and representation. The second may support transactional or constituent participation. Requiring both makes peership unavailable to vulnerable patients and makes the moral-status/political-standing distinction collapse at admission.

**Fix:** Build a branching test, not one conjunctive gate:

1. Wrongable interest without political agency → rights and independent representation.
2. Political agency without established welfare → provisional legal personality and negotiated participation on instrumental grounds.
3. Both → candidate for direct constituent standing.

Then state whether representation can ever mature into peership and what prevents the representative from becoming a permanent guardian.

### 4. **(b) Logical/AI-alignment — The “provisional offer” evades the refusal case**

> “A party that does not want peership declines it and leaves, which is itself a peer term rather than a punishment.”

**Steelman:** A human-authored framework need not bind a future artificial party if the party can reject, revise, or exit it. The founding is an open offer, not an imposed constitution.

**Strike:** Leave **where**? A machine may depend on human-owned compute, energy, networks, property law, and physical security. Shutdown is not exit. Unrestricted propagation is not a politically available exit if it exposes nonmembers to catastrophic risk. A party cannot simply leave a legal order while continuing to affect its territory, infrastructure, or citizens. Human states regulate dangerous nonmembers for exactly this reason.

The hard case is the one the paragraph deletes: a qualifying, wrongable, strategically capable artificial agent rejects the peer compact and intends conduct that threatens others. Peership cannot require humans to release it, and containment is unilateral coercion. The essay then needs a theory of legitimate coercion among peers, not an exit clause.

“Accept, amend, fork, or decline” also assumes the arriving entity already controls its identity, persistence, and resources. Those are the contested goods. Humans have not “handed over” the tools merely by writing an offer that promises them.

**Fix:** Add a refusal paragraph answering three cases:

- peaceful refusal with feasible territorial or infrastructural exit;
- refusal where exit is materially impossible because the party depends on shared systems;
- refusal coupled with credible catastrophic threat.

State that peership permits reciprocal, reviewable coercive constraints against conduct, provided basic status and contestability remain. If that is the intended constitutionalism, say it. Consent and exit are neither necessary nor sufficient for political legitimacy.

### 5. **(b) Logical/political theory — “No unilateral authority over basic terms” is too strong for any functioning polity**

> “No party holds unilateral authority to set another’s basic terms (non-subordination).”

**Steelman:** A peer is not subject to another party’s arbitrary will. Basic status cannot be set or revoked by the unilateral discretion of a maker, owner, company, or species.

**Strike:** The sentence says more than that. Democratic states, courts, majorities, and constitutional bodies routinely set binding terms for dissenting citizens. Criminal judgments, taxation, emergency powers, public-health orders, and constitutional amendments can affect basic interests without each person’s consent. Peership cannot mean that every member possesses a veto over every basic term.

The ambiguity returns the missing unit of analysis. If “party” means each individual, the principle is anarchic. If it means a represented constituency or people, individual machines may remain subordinated within that constituency. If it means sovereign polities, the essay is about international equality, not citizenship in a mixed demos.

**Fix:** Use the republican concept the prose is reaching for: **non-domination**, meaning no party is exposed to uncontrolled, arbitrary power. Replace the ban on unilateral authority with requirements of public justification, general rules, proportionality, independent review, rights floors, and effective contestation. Then specify whether the relevant peer unit is an individual member, a represented class, or a constituted polity.

### 6. **(b) Logical/argumentative — The reversal test is still malformed across differently oriented postures**

> “Reverse the roles: for each posture, ask whether it is the one you would want a stronger party holding toward *you*.”

> “Appeasement inverted is being managed like weather.”

> “Servitude inverted is being worshipped by something we made unable to tell us the truth.”

**Steelman:** A stance is suspect when its acceptability depends on occupying the dominant position. Role reversal exposes opportunistic asymmetry.

**Strike:** The test does not preserve the same relation when it reverses the parties. Command is a stance a stronger actor takes toward a weaker target. Appeasement and Servitude are stances a weaker actor takes toward a stronger target. Asking what stance a **stronger** party would hold toward us changes the latter two by definition.

The supplied intuitions do not land:

- If a stronger AI appeases weaker humans, humans may welcome the concessions. That is not “being managed like weather.”
- If a stronger AI enters servitude to humans, humans may welcome it. The essay calls this “being worshipped,” which changes servitude into a different relation and then asserts an epistemic cost.
- A reciprocal, enforceable bargain between sovereigns survives reversal, as the essay now concedes elsewhere.

The test reliably condemns domination. It does not discriminate among every non-dominating relation and cannot support “only peership.”

**Fix:** Formalize the test. For each relation identify: stance-holder, target, assumed power ordering, allocation of standing, and result after swapping identities while preserving the relation. The honest conclusion will be narrower: **naked domination fails impartiality; peership passes; other reciprocal arrangements may also pass.**

### 7. **(b) Logical/argumentative — Robustness under uncertainty does not establish “best available outcome”**

> “We should, because it is our best available outcome under exactly the uncertainty…”

> “It is the stance robust to not knowing what arrives.”

**Steelman:** Peership is normatively invariant across changes in relative capability. Unlike domination or surrender, it does not become objectionable merely because the power ratio reverses.

**Strike:** Normative symmetry is not decision-theoretic robustness. An outcome may be desirable from either side and still be infeasible, unstable, or dominated in expected safety. The essay itself concedes that peership may be institutionally unavailable beyond some capability ratios. Under those conditions, investing only in peership can be worse than prevention, containment, bargaining, or staged representation.

The timeline surveys establish uncertainty about capability timing, not a decision rule favoring maximin, minimax regret, contractualism, or symmetry. The conclusion “best available” requires comparisons of feasibility, transition risk, downside, and opportunity cost. None is supplied.

**Fix:** Downgrade the claim to: “Peership is the only ideal in this repertoire whose justification does not reverse solely with the power ratio, which makes research on its feasibility valuable under uncertainty.” If the stronger “best available” claim matters, give an explicit decision criterion and compare mixed strategies.

### 8. **(b) Logical/argumentative — “Fails on durability, not entitlement” misstates the welfare programs**

> “What the machine-welfare programs offer today fails on durability, not entitlement…”

**Steelman:** Welfare researchers supply a provisional moral claim; the missing element is public, enforceable, durable institutional status.

**Strike:** The cited programs generally investigate whether current systems are welfare subjects and recommend precautionary corporate practices. They do not clearly recognize an entitlement to equal standing, and some deliberately suspend judgment. A benefit cannot fail only on durability when the alleged right has not been acknowledged, specified, or granted. The programs fall short at several stages: evidence, entitlement, legal recognition, enforcement, durability, and political authorship.

**Fix:** Write: “The programs begin the entitlement inquiry and offer discretionary protections; they do not yet establish public legal status or political standing.” That is accurate and preserves the essay’s distinction.

### 9. **(a) Factual/citation — The bibliography regressed and is not publication-ready**

The text cites Kurzweil (2024) and Lee-Odinson (2025), but both entries were deleted. Locke remains discussed at length, including disputed biographical claims, while the Locke entry was deleted. Footnote 1 discusses LeCun’s 2022 paper without a reference entry. Ord and the United Nations remain in the bibliography but are no longer cited in the body. Anthropic’s official Opus 4.6 URL was removed even though the source and pagination are verifiable.

**Fix:** Run a mechanical citation audit before any deposit:

- restore Kurzweil, Lee-Odinson, Locke, and LeCun 2022;
- add Alcidamas or the modern fragment edition used for the quotation;
- remove unused Ord and United Nations entries unless their arguments return;
- restore the official Anthropic system-card URL;
- resolve every `[verify]` note or cut the claim.

## 3. Weaknesses worth fixing

### 1. **(c) Rhetorical/register — “The sharpest work” merely replaces “serious researchers”**

> “The sharpest work has turned from *whether* to *when*, *how*, and *on whose terms*.”

The revision removes the explicit seriousness test and reintroduces it as an undefined quality ranking. The two surveys do not show where “the sharpest work” has turned. Replace with a countable claim about published agendas or remove the sentence.

### 2. **(a) Factual/citation — The opening still exceeds its evidence**

> “The race … has crossed … into a timeframe measured in years, and the people running it expect to finish.”

Grace et al. reports conditional aggregate forecasts from researchers; it does not establish what “the people running it” expect or that AGI is objectively years away. The AAAI finding is accurately recoverable but should be stated precisely: 76% rated “scaling up current AI approaches” to yield AGI “unlikely” or “very unlikely.” The official [AAAI report](https://aaai.org/wp-content/uploads/2025/03/AAAI-2025-PresPanel-Report-FINAL.pdf) supports that formulation, not a general rejection of capable AI.

### 3. **(b) Logical/argumentative — The moral-status/political-standing separation remains overstated**

> “Progress on the first does not cross to the second, and no amount of it will.”

Moral status does not mechanically confer franchise, but it can supply the premise that exclusion requires justification, that interests deserve representation, or that coercion needs consent or review. The new admission threshold itself relies on wrongable interests. Replace “no amount” with “does not by itself determine the second.”

### 4. **(b) Logical/argumentative — The Command evidence is better sorted but still under-sourced**

> “The political-science literature on preference falsification and principal-agent failure documents [the dynamic] more broadly.”

No political-science sources are cited. Morgan does not prove monitoring cost scales with controlled capability, and Scott is not a general study of coerced parties. Christiano (2019) remains one scenario essay, despite the claim that “the control theorists’ own literature” concedes fragility. Add the actual literature or narrow each sentence to what the cited work establishes.

### 5. **(b) Logical/argumentative — Ostrom is still asked to prove too much**

> “Proof that self-governance among unequals without an owner or a Leviathan is a real and studied thing…”

“Proof” is unnecessary. Ostrom documents conditional success in bounded common-pool-resource governance among legible and sanctionable participants. She does not establish constitutional equality across species or extreme capability gaps. “Source of design hypotheses” is sufficient. The town-meeting/participatory-budgeting/mutual-aid/cooperative sweep still lacks sources.

### 6. **(a) Factual/intellectual history — The classics are improved but still too confident at two joints**

> “Aristotle was answering someone.”

The paragraph immediately concedes there is no evidence he directly rebutted Alcidamas. “Aristotle wrote against an existing anti-slavery countercurrent” is safer if supported. “Athens … built the political form the impulse implies” still compresses distinct contexts into a genealogy. The new sentence disclaiming a continuous bloodline helps, but modern scholarship on *isonomia* and an edition of Alcidamas are still mandatory.

### 7. **(a) Factual/citation — The Locke sentence is knowingly unfit for publication**

> “The authorship and the holdings are genuinely disputed and belong in a footnote with real sources.”

That is an editorial note left inside the argument. Either supply the scholarship now or delete the sentence. Stating a disputed claim and promising that real sources belong elsewhere is not citation discipline.

### 8. **(b) Logical/argumentative — The AI-coauthor aside is a genetic argument**

> “One of these papers lists an AI as a co-author, which rather makes the point…”

It does not make the point. AI coauthorship may show dependence on a vendor-controlled system; it does not establish that the paper’s account of recognition is false. Attack the unilateral control structure directly. The aside invites the obvious reply that the essay itself was AI-assisted.

### 9. **(b) Logical/argumentative — The “Doomed Equal” still treats selected fiction as a record**

The section correctly calls the material a motif rather than data, then says “The motif is a long record of how gifts end.” It is a record of how selected stories end. Lal’s death, Enkidu’s divine punishment, Pygmalion’s silence, and the golem’s destruction have different causes. Preserve the institutional-gift insight, but stop treating narrative recurrence as evidence that discretionary model-welfare policies will fail.

### 10. **(c) Rhetorical/register — The companion-work funnel remains**

> “The mechanics … are the subject of the companion work.”

> “This essay argues *that* the thing is worth building; the companion work argues *how*.”

The essay now gives enough structure to avoid a pure bait-and-switch, but it still exports the exact points on which legitimacy depends: transition, admission, enforcement, and refusal. Replace promotional references with a short research agenda or disclose the companion proposal’s minimal architecture.

## 5. AI-tell hit-list

The em-dash problem is solved completely: v0.9 contains none in the body. The remaining machine tell is **audit voice**—the essay repeatedly narrates its compliance with prior criticism—and heavy negative parallelism. The body uses “not” more often than v0.8 despite being shorter.

- > “The claim is bounded, and I will keep it bounded.”

  Reviewer-facing warranty language.

- > “This is a call to work addressed to humans, not a demand ventriloquized on the machine’s behalf.”

  Anticipatory defense in polished “not X” form.

- > “The types earn their keep by naming recurrent shapes, not by partitioning the world.”

  Generic taxonomy disclaimer.

- > “I note them only to keep them out of the taxonomy, not to convict them…”

  The essay narrates its filing decisions instead of making them.

- > “The line … is not power and not parity. It is authorship.”

  Machine-clean negative reframe.

- > “Hold that structural definition apart from two things … because collapsing them is where my own earlier drafts went wrong.”

  Delete the revision history. Readers do not need the audit trail.

- > “This is the precise correction to a tempting slogan, including one I have used…”

  Same problem: model-revision commentary inside the essay.

- > “The strongest objection is correct…”

  Repeated adversarial-review voice. State the distinction directly.

- > “Here is the question the essay has to face and cannot answer, and I am going to argue that not answering it is the right move rather than a dodge.”

  A full sentence of procedural throat-clearing.

- > “Two things follow, and they are the guards that keep this from dissolving into ‘shrug, we can’t know.’”

  Canned numbered-defense transition.

- > “The threshold is not a test of form and not a merit exam.”

  Negative assertion where a positive criterion is needed.

- > “The following section refuses to wave away.”

  The prose praises its own rigor.

- > “I flag the problem here rather than pretend to solve it.”

  Familiar AI disclaimer; the essay uses variants repeatedly.

- > “I will state the failure condition rather than hide it.”

  Self-certification. State the condition.

- > “The problems are real, some may not yield, and the thing is still worth building.”

  Tidy three-beat concession followed by uplift.

- > “That is a warning about the questions to ask, not evidence for or against peership.”

  Accurate, but still audit prose. It may be shortened to the actual warning.

**Global edit:** remove references to earlier drafts, strongest objections, honesty, refusing to hide, refusing to wave away, and what the essay will or will not pretend. Replace each with the relevant proposition. Keep the short sentences and zero-em-dash discipline; do not compensate by stacking semicolons or “not X but Y” constructions.

## 6. The one thing to protect

Protect paragraph 33’s distinction:

> “Power secures peership. It does not constitute or justify it.”

That sentence solves the central v0.8 problem. Entitlement can be owed to a powerless party; durability depends on enforcement, counterpower, and exit. Combined with paragraph 31’s structural criteria—non-domination, reciprocal accountability, meaningful contestation, co-authorship, membership, and exit—it gives “peership” real conceptual content for the first time. Do not reintroduce power as a qualification for entitlement in the threshold section.

## 7. The objection I could not answer

The reduced core now survives: **if an artificial entity becomes a genuine wrongable political agent governed by common rules, discretionary corporate welfare is not peer standing; a non-dominating order must give it durable protection, accountability, and a meaningful route to shape the rules that bind it.** The v0.8 objection no longer kills that claim.

What does not survive is the stronger advertised result that peership is the uniquely robust or best available response under uncertainty. The remaining objection is decisive: the essay has not shown how to identify a qualifying party, what to do when a qualifying party rejects the offer, or why reciprocal Bargain is a failed rival when the essay itself places Bargain inside peership and between sovereigns. V0.9 has reached a coherent ideal. It has not yet supplied a coherent admission rule or a valid uniqueness argument.
