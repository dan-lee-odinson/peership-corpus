# Integration Audit: *Peership* Draft v0.5

**Documents audited:** `peership_draft_v0.5.md` against the Round 1 adversarial review and the author’s disclosed deviations.

## 1. Integration verdict

The revision answers several secondary objections honestly: the standing ladder is real, the reversal test is properly bounded, the colonial analogy is now conditional, and the anthropology citations are materially cleaner. It does **not** answer the Round 1 killing objection; it relocates it from “peership is uniquely coherent” to “peership is the only stance worth choosing,” then protects the same conclusion by declaring peership’s assumptions “engineering” and its rivals’ assumptions “in-principle incoherence.” The Neanderthal section does the same thing: it formally disclaims causation, then restores the missing-institution causal story in the section’s load-bearing conclusion. The essay is stronger and safer, but also split between a defensible modest thesis and an unsupported ambitious one. It now has better armor, not a repaired spine.

## 2. Disclosure audit

### 1. Exclusivity claim → “argument, not theorem”

**COSMETIC** — “these are *engineering* uncertainties ... rather than the *in-principle* incoherence that sinks the other four” merely labels peership’s equally serious bets nonfatal without an argument, while the conclusion still says “only peership does not require us to win a bet about the future.”

### 2. Neanderthal causation → analogy

**COSMETIC** — after disclaiming the counterfactual, the draft restores it in substance with “it is institutions, not feelings, that hold a smaller party as a distinct equal” and calls prehistory “a long warning about what tends to happen when [peership] is absent.”

### 3. Requirements list → open problems

**MITIGATED** — converting the product-shaped specification into explicit questions removes the false appearance of a finished design, but “I do not solve them here” leaves identity, Sybil capture, enforcement, and exit—the conditions that would make peership a selectable stance rather than a wish—entirely unpaid.

### 4. Standing → institutional ladder

**MITIGATED** — the ladder is substantive and used in the welfare discussion, but the draft never identifies peership’s minimum rung and later slides back to franchise, “the demos,” and undifferentiated “standing” when it needs the stronger conclusion.

### 5. Reversal test → diagnostic, not proof

**RESOLVED** — the explicit Hobbesian and tool counterexamples correctly limit the test to exposing opportunism, which is legitimate work even though applying the test to every rival remains contestable.

### 6. Colonialism/paternalism → conditional analogy

**RESOLVED** — “Absent that subject, controlling an artifact is just engineering” genuinely suspends the disputed personhood premise, and the separate vendor-conflict argument no longer depends on colonial moral contagion.

### 7. Artistic/register choices retained

**MITIGATED** — the forecast quartet, reduced negative parallels, and reader imperatives are legitimate manifesto choices, but “We have run every other stance on each other and buried the results” and “Biology ... gave us the template that produces a single survivor” still use cadence to assert historical exhaustiveness and a biological default the evidence does not establish.

## 3. Newly broken or newly exposed

### 1. The essay now has two theses, and only the weaker one survives

The weak thesis is stated in paragraph 41: “a revocable corporate safeguard is not a constitutional right.” That is correct but close to a legal truism; no serious welfare theorist needs persuading that a voluntary company policy is not constitutional law. The strong thesis remains that peership is “the stance worth choosing” because the four rivals fail conceptually. That thesis is nontrivial but still unproved. The rewrite oscillates between the weak thesis when challenged and the strong thesis when concluding.

**Fix:** Choose one. If the essay argues for research, say peership is *worth investigating*. If it argues for adoption, pay the feasibility and comparative burdens in this essay.

### 2. Bargain is now a vestigial category

Altman and Andreessen are correctly reclassified as “Command wearing a market’s clothes.” The only living Bargain occupant is Salib and Goldstein, whose proposal is immediately said to “slide toward peership.” Appeasement is “nearly vacant.” The advertised five-stance map of the actual debate therefore contains two nearly empty categories and one category whose only occupant is annexed to the preferred stance.

This matters because the conclusion still says “the four stances the loud debate actually offers” fail. The revision has shown that the loud debate does **not** actually offer all four as defined.

**Fix:** Either find genuine living occupants, present the taxonomy strictly as transhistorical ideal types, or remove the claim that it indexes the current debate.

### 3. The map now mixes relations, development policies, and ontologies

The draft concedes that Yudkowsky and Soares offer “a policy about development, not a posture of mastery toward an existing mind,” then keeps them inside Command because they want to prevent the entity. It concedes LeCun has no stance toward an Other he denies exists, then says his practice “does the work Command does.” These admissions do not refine the categories; they show that the map is classifying three different objects:

- how to treat an existing party;
- whether to create that party;
- whether such a party is possible.

A taxonomy cannot claim exhaustiveness while changing its unit of analysis to retain occupants.

**Fix:** Separate development policy, ontology of machine mind, and post-creation political relation into different axes.

### 4. The standing ladder breaks the “Bargain slides toward peership” inference

The ladder correctly distinguishes legal personality and contract capacity from franchise and membership in the demos. But paragraph 23 still treats enforceable contracts as a slide into peership. Corporations, trusts, municipalities, foreign states, and noncitizens can contract without equal civic standing. The new distinction invalidates the old inference.

**Fix:** Say enforceable AI contracts would place a system on the legal-personality rung; then argue separately for why that should lead further.

### 5. “On the ladder” is too indeterminate to carry peership

Peership is now “the claim that a made mind of sufficient agency belongs *on the ladder as a party*.” Which rung is the minimum? Moral considerability is already the welfare position the essay says is insufficient. Legal personality needs no inner life. Representation is compatible with guardianship. Franchise is explicitly disclaimed as automatic. Without a minimum incident—an enforceable right, independent representation, capacity to sue, or something else—peership has become a label for taking the question seriously.

**Fix:** Define the irreducible peership bundle. “Its status cannot be revoked at a vendor’s pleasure” is a start, but the draft must state who grants, enforces, and reviews that status.

### 6. “Higher rungs earned and conditioned” reopens the exclusion problem

The draft removed “standing earned by verified work,” but now says “the higher rungs [are] earned and conditioned.” That may be defensible for office, liability, or franchise, but it is dangerously vague. Earned by what—competence, contribution, obedience, property, labor, examination? The entire historical section condemns “unready” tests administered by beneficiaries of exclusion.

**Fix:** Name the conditions and distinguish capacity qualifications from productivity or ideological conformity.

### 7. “Engineering uncertainty” is functioning as an exemption, not a distinction

Identity, rule-following, stable interests, enforcement across capability gaps, and capture-resistance are not implementation details downstream of a complete political concept. They determine whether there is a party, whether collective choice is possible, and whether formal standing can exist. A voting system with no answer to who counts as one voter does not have a sound political theory awaiting engineering; it has an undefined demos.

The contrast also applies asymmetrically. Command and Bargain face engineering problems too; their failure modes are not “in-principle incoherence.” Risk-based containment is conceptually coherent even if morally objectionable. Bargains among unequal parties are common and can be stabilized by law, repeated interaction, coalition, and asymmetric interdependence.

**Fix:** Replace the engineering/conceptual binary with a common feasibility test applied to all five stances.

### 8. “Theorem versus argument” is a false epistemic distinction

> “I am not claiming a proof ... that would be a theorem, and this is an argument.”

A theorem is a proposition proved within a formal system; an argument is what may or may not establish a conclusion. Calling the essay “an argument” does not lower its burden to make the conclusion follow. This is precisely the kind of confident cadence that smuggles an unpaid claim.

**Fix:** Say only: “I am offering defeasible comparative reasons, not demonstrating uniqueness.”

### 9. The anthropology disclaimer and conclusion contradict each other

Paragraph 85 says archaeology cannot establish that lack of standing caused absorption. Paragraph 89 then says institutions “hold a smaller party as a distinct equal,” biology supplies the single-survivor template, and prehistory warns what happens when peership is absent. That is the causal counterfactual in three pieces.

The evidence supports only this: interbreeding and possible cultural continuity occurred, and Neanderthals ceased to exist as a distinct lineage. It does not establish a sustained benign “bond,” that learning passed between the populations, that institutions would have preserved them, or that absorption is biology’s default.

**Fix:** Cut paragraph 89’s institutional lesson and reduce the section to the limited analogy: contact and admixture do not guarantee collective continuity.

### 10. The merger analogy remains overclaimed

The draft now labels the Oase/Neanderthal comparison a metaphor, which is honest, then calls it “the nearest thing we have to data on how fusion looks from the absorbed side” and says whether merger or disappearance is true “depends entirely” on standing. Introgression contains no first-person perspective, and political standing does not determine the correct metaphysics of personal or collective identity.

**Fix:** Keep the metaphor or the evidentiary claim, not both. The defensible version is one sentence: merger narratives should specify whose continuity counts.

## 4. The deferral ledger

| Location | Deferral | What is postponed | Judgment |
|---|---|---|---|
| Paragraph 51 | “at length in later work” | Individuation, rule-following, stable interests, enforcement, capture-resistance | **Load-bearing.** These determine whether peership can exist. |
| Paragraph 65 | “defers to its sequel” | Political obligation without origin consent for a created mind | **Load-bearing.** A polity cannot bind a made party without an account of membership and authority. |
| Paragraph 77 | “the part no essay settles and this one will not pretend to” | Citizen identity, copying, forks, rollback, merges, edits | **Evasive phrasing.** No one asked for final settlement; a minimum viable account is required. |
| Paragraph 77 | “I do not solve them here; ... *why*, not *how*” | Sybil capture, vendor ownership, reputation, exit, enforcement against a dominant system | **False division.** The “why” depends on prima facie feasibility of the “how.” |

There are eight explicit deferral moves covering three clusters: political subjecthood, political obligation, and institutional feasibility. The raw count is not the problem; their location is. All three are premises of the recommendation, not optional implementation detail. One sequel reference would be legitimate division of labor. Repeating the deferral while continuing to say peership is “worth choosing” is evasion. Change the present thesis to “worth researching,” or supply a minimum feasibility argument now.

## 5. Citation spot-check results

### PASS — Goyet/Cosnefroy

The author order is corrected, the victims are described substantially accurately, and the draft no longer presents the assemblage as *H. sapiens*-on-Neanderthal predation. The 2025 paper identifies at least six individuals, including four adult/adolescent females and two immature individuals, with evidence of nonlocal origins and exocannibalism; the earlier taphonomic study considered other Neanderthals the likeliest processors because modern humans were not documented locally. [Cosnefroy et al., 2025](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-24460-3); [Rougier et al., 2016](https://www.nature.com/articles/srep29005).

Residue: “most plausibly by other Neanderthals” should be attached to Rougier et al. 2016 or stated as an inference, not attributed solely to Cosnefroy et al. 2025.

### PASS WITH QUALIFICATION — Üçağızlı II/Baykara

The revision correctly says the cave records sequential Neanderthal-then-*H. sapiens* layers and correctly treats direct contact as possible rather than proved. Continuity in lithic practices, prey use, and selection of *Columbella rustica* shells is supported. [Baykara et al., 2026](https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.2609061123).

Residue: “ornamental shell” is stronger than necessary; use “non-utilitarian shell,” because ornamental use is inferred. The paper was published in July 2026 and is too new to carry “established” status.

### PASS — Goldfeder/Wyder/LeCun/Shwartz-Ziv

The order and initials are corrected: Judah Goldfeder, Philippe Wyder, Yann LeCun, and Ravid Shwartz-Ziv. The draft fairly states that the paper attacks AGI as an overloaded or flawed concept and proposes specialized superhuman adaptable intelligence. [Primary arXiv record](https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.23643).

Residue: mark it explicitly as an unrefereed preprint in the reference entry, as the references note promises to do.

### FAIL — Amadei/Lin/Fattorini bibliography

The reference is still wrong. The authors are **Andrea Amadei, Giulia Lin, and Simone Fattorini**, not “M., T., and P.” The title is **“A simple analytical model for Neanderthal disappearance due to genetic dilution by recurrent small-scale immigrations of modern humans,”** *Scientific Reports* 15, article 38593 (2025), DOI 10.1038/s41598-025-22376-6. [Primary article](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-22376-6).

The prose correctly restores the paper’s “intense debate” framing and treats assimilation as possible and multicausal rather than consensus. But “a growing body of modeling” cites only this one new model. Say “one recent model” or cite the body.

### FAIL — Tinshemet

“Tinshemet Cave excavation reports” is a placeholder, not a citation. The primary source is Zaidner et al., “Evidence from Tinshemet Cave in Israel suggests behavioural uniformity across Homo groups in the Levantine mid-Middle Palaeolithic circa 130,000–80,000 years ago,” *Nature Human Behaviour* 9, 886–901 (2025), DOI 10.1038/s41562-025-02110-y. [Primary article](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41562-025-02110-y).

The sentence “at Tinshemet ... both populations appear to have used ochre and buried their dead” is misleading. Tinshemet yielded articulated *Homo* remains whose precise taxonomic affinity is debated; the paper argues for **regional** behavioural uniformity across morphologically variable *Homo* groups using comparisons among Tinshemet, Skhul, Qafzeh, and Nesher Ramla. It does not establish identified Neanderthal and *H. sapiens* populations both burying their dead at Tinshemet.

### PASS — Oase 1

“Four to six generations” is accurate and appropriately avoids the earlier overprecise great-great-grandparent formulation. [Fu et al., 2015](https://www.nature.com/articles/nature14558).

Residue: this supports recent admixture, not a socially “gentle” relation or any perspective from the absorbed side.

### FAIL — Neanderthal ancestry percentage

The revised prose gives “on the order of one to two percent in people outside Africa” but the reference list no longer contains Green et al. or a later genomic source. The added caveat about refined distribution is sensible, but the numerical claim remains uncited and “outside Africa” needs qualification because back-migration produced Neanderthal ancestry in African populations too.

### PASS — AAAI 76% correction

The revision accurately identifies 76% of 475 community-survey respondents, rather than three quarters of the 25-person panel, as judging that scaling current AI approaches is unlikely or very unlikely to yield AGI. [Official AAAI report](https://aaai.org/wp-content/uploads/2025/03/AAAI-2025-PresPanel-Report-Digital-3.7.25.pdf).

Residue: “the center of gravity has moved from whether ... to when” remains an uncited field-wide overclaim and sits awkwardly beside evidence that definitions and feasibility remain disputed.

### UPDATE REQUIRED — Salib and Goldstein

The reference still says *Virginia Law Review* “forthcoming.” The article is now published in the *Virginia Law Review* and argues for private-law rights—contract, property, and tort claims—not franchise. [Published article](https://virginialawreview.org/articles/ai-rights-for-human-safety/).

This strengthens the ladder objection: private-law personality is a specific rung, not evidence that Bargain necessarily becomes political peership.

### NOT PUBLICATION-READY — acknowledged placeholders

The reference list still contains instructions to the author rather than citations: “verify,” “add episode URL,” “cite page,” “fill author initials,” and “add the primary Tinshemet publication.” Honest disclosure is better than fabricated completion, but a bibliography with unresolved editorial notes is not integrated scholarship. At minimum, Amodei, Anthropic 2026, Pines & Ash, Locke/RAC/Carolina, Tinshemet, and the modern commons examples remain unfinished.

## 6. Does the core survive now?

Yes, but only after separating it from the essay’s advertised comparative victory.

The defensible, non-trivial thesis is this: **if a made system develops individuable, diachronic agency and interests, its legal and political status should not remain wholly revocable at the discretion of the vendor that benefits from denying it status; political recognition should be treated as a ladder, and at least some enforceable rung may be warranted independently of raw capability.** That claim survives. It is more than “be nice to models,” and the distinction between welfare policy and rights enforceable against the benefactor is worth keeping.

The strongest objection still landing is the **engineering firewall**: the essay has not identified the bearer of status, the minimum peership rung, or a plausible way to prevent copying and capability asymmetry from destroying collective rule, yet it declares those problems engineering while treating rival failure modes as conceptual. Until that asymmetry is removed, the essay has justified peership as a research program, not as “the stance worth choosing.”
