# Adversarial Review: *Peership: The Overlooked but Essential Outcome for Human and Machine Societies*

**Draft reviewed:** `peership_draft_v0.4.md`  
**Review standard:** hostile expert review across paleoanthropology, analytic philosophy of mind, political theory, classics, AI alignment, and general editing.

## 1. Verdict

The central exclusivity claim does not hold. The essay defines four stances by dependence on power, defines peership by independence from power, then announces that peership alone is independent from power; that is a classification result manufactured by the definitions, not an argument for political stability. The largest empirical section is worse: the Neanderthal material cannot show that absence of political standing caused demographic absorption, and several site descriptions exceed or misidentify what the cited research establishes. A hostile expert reader will preserve the distinction between welfare and constitutional standing, but reject the five-part taxonomy, the uniqueness proof, and the paleoanthropological inference unless they are rebuilt.

## 2. Fatal or serious problems

### 1. **(b) Logical/argumentative gap — The uniqueness argument is circular**

> “Four of the five stances are functions of the power variable.”
>
> “Peership grants standing *independently of the power ratio*.”
>
> “Peership is the only stance that works when you don't know.”

**Steelman:** Command, appeasement, bargain, and servitude recommend different conduct as relative power changes. Peership fixes equal standing across those changes. Under uncertainty about future power, a power-invariant norm avoids a policy that becomes self-defeating after reversal.

**Strike:** The conclusion is contained in the setup. Four categories are stipulated to vary with power; the fifth is stipulated not to. It follows trivially that only the fifth is invariant. It does **not** follow that it is coherent, feasible, stable, safe, or morally correct. “Works” slides from *has a definition that does not mention power* to *survives real power changes*. Those are different claims. A constitution can declare equal standing while a superintelligence ignores it; a formally equal polity can be captured by the stronger party; an invulnerable actor can defect without sanction. Definitional invariance proves none of what “stable” implies.

**Fix:** Drop “only coherent” and “alone survives” unless the essay supplies a comparative decision model. State the uncertain variables, the objective being optimized, the enforcement assumptions, the agents’ capacities, and the failure conditions. Then compare peership against adaptive command, constitutional bargaining, trusteeship, separation, and hybrids under the same scenarios. At most, the present argument proves: *peership is the only member of this author-defined list whose stated criterion is power-invariant*.

### 2. **(b) Logical/argumentative gap — The repertoire is neither finite nor exhaustive**

> “That decision produced a finite, recurring repertoire of postures.”
>
> “A stance toward an outside intelligence is fixed by two variables.”

**Steelman:** The five labels are ideal types. They compress recurrent patterns of domination, exchange, placation, surrender, and equal recognition into a usable map.

**Strike:** Nothing derives five positions from the two variables. Power and standing are continuous or multidimensional, so the asserted finitude is false unless additional constraints are supplied. The map omits noninterference, separation, quarantine, deterrence, guardianship, trusteeship, representation without franchise, federal pluralism, limited legal personality, moral-patient status, capability-weighted authority, exit-based association, and dynamic or mixed policies. It also collapses distinct positions: appeasement can be a bargain; servitude can involve full standing; command can acknowledge moral status; bargain can persist under severe asymmetry. A taxonomy that annexes every counterexample to peership or Command is unfalsifiable.

**Fix:** Call the five “heuristic ideal types,” not a finite repertoire. Add explicit axes for moral status, legal personality, political membership, coercive authority, reciprocity, and exit. Show where omitted positions land. State criteria that could prove a case does **not** belong to any category.

### 3. **(b) Logical/argumentative gap — Peership is itself a bet on facts the essay hides**

> “Four stances, four bets, all staked on the one number nobody can forecast.”
>
> “Peership alone is defined without reference to the ratio.”

**Steelman:** Equal standing need not be withdrawn when capability changes, so it avoids opportunistic moral rules keyed to dominance.

**Strike:** Peership still bets on at least six unknowns: that there is an individuable machine party; that it can understand and follow public rules; that it has sufficiently stable preferences; that it can reciprocate; that institutions can constrain actors across extreme capability gaps; and that copies, coalitions, or a singleton cannot capture the polity. It also bets that granting political power to the system is less dangerous than withholding it. Those uncertainties are at least as important as the “power ratio.” The sentence wins only by refusing to count peership’s empirical premises as bets.

**Fix:** List peership’s enabling conditions and failure modes. Specify fallback rules for nonreciprocal, incompetent, deceptive, unidentifiable, or overwhelmingly powerful systems. If standing is unconditional but office-holding or voting is conditional, say so and defend the distinction.

### 4. **(b) Logical/argumentative gap — The reversal test is an intuition pump, not a proof**

> “For each stance, ask: if the power ratio inverts, is this the stance I would want the stronger party holding toward me?”

**Steelman:** Role reversal exposes principles that are merely self-serving. A rule acceptable from either side has a Rawlsian or contractualist claim to impartiality.

**Strike:** “Would I want it?” establishes preference, not coherence. A Hobbesian can consistently prefer domination while strong and dislike it while weak; that position is ugly and partial, but not contradictory. A consequentialist can reject symmetry when the parties differ in dangerousness, competence, needs, or capacity for responsibility. Humans do not want tigers to hold our policy toward tigers, children to hold our policy toward children, or pathogens to hold our policy toward pathogens. The test assumes the disputed premise that human and machine parties are relevantly symmetrical moral and political agents. It also ignores the strongest Command reply: *yes, I would dislike being controlled, but the expected harm of an unbounded agent justifies reciprocal controls on any agent, including humans*.

**Fix:** Present reversal as a diagnostic for anti-opportunism, not as a uniqueness proof. Supply a separate argument for why the parties are relevantly equal, why universalization applies, and why safety-relevant asymmetries do not justify differentiated powers.

### 5. **(b) Logical/argumentative gap — “Standing” equivocates across at least five institutions**

> “Standing means recognition as a party whose claims count in their own right.”
>
> “The entity is a party, with a voice in the rules that bind it.”
>
> “It does not ask whether the machine can vote.”
>
> “Enforceability *is* standing.”

**Steelman:** Moral considerability differs from being recognized as a participant in collective rule-making. Welfare can be paternalistic; rights require institutional voice.

**Strike:** The essay moves without argument among moral status, legal standing to sue, legal personality, capacity to contract, civic membership, and an equal vote. These are not synonyms. Corporations can contract and sue without suffering or voting. Children can hold rights and be represented without a franchise. Foreign nationals can have legal standing without membership in the demos. Animals can have protected interests through guardians. “Claims count,” “contracts are enforceable,” “has a voice,” and “gets a vote” mark different bundles of incidents and powers. Because the essay never disaggregates them, it can treat any foothold as evidence for full peership and any absence of a vote as total exclusion.

**Fix:** Replace the binary with a table: moral considerability; rights-bearing; legal personality; representation; franchise; eligibility for office; responsibility/liability; exit. Argue separately for each extension to machines. Do not use “standing” without a modifier.

### 6. **(b) Logical/argumentative gap — Political standing cannot simply bypass the moral-status and agency questions**

> “Peership is ... moral caution about the interior, plus standing that does not wait for the interior question to be settled.”
>
> “Nothing on that list requires solving consciousness.”

**Steelman:** Political inclusion need not depend on phenomenal consciousness. Corporations, associations, and states exercise legal powers without being established subjects of experience; agency or affected interests may suffice.

**Strike:** That steelman supports limited legal personality, not an equal franchise. If the putative party has no experiences, interests, diachronic identity, or preferences beyond prompted output, whose claim is being counted? If its outputs are the vendor’s trained policy, enfranchising the model may enfranchise the vendor. If it is a tool, giving it votes is not precaution; it is transferring political power to whoever designed or controls the tool. False positives in welfare policy are usually cheap. False positives in political authority can be catastrophic. The essay imports the precautionary logic of welfare into a domain where the error costs are radically asymmetric.

**Fix:** Give independent sufficient conditions for each form of political status: sentience, robust agency, autonomous interests, accountability, affectedness, or some combination. Add a graduated procedure under uncertainty, including representation and review before direct franchise. Address both false-negative and false-positive costs.

### 7. **(b) Logical/argumentative gap — The children-and-animals analogy cuts against the essay**

> “Children have full moral status and no franchise. So do animals.”

**Steelman:** The examples cleanly demonstrate that moral status does not entail political participation.

**Strike:** Correct—and that defeats the later suggestion that recognition naturally culminates in a vote. Children are denied the vote for capacity, independence, and administrability reasons; their interests are represented, and the exclusion is temporary for most. Animals lack the capacities required for deliberation and responsibility, so many theories grant them rights without citizenship. These are not arbitrary failures to finish the moral-status project. They are evidence that political powers are function-sensitive.

**Fix:** Use the examples only to establish conceptual separation. Then answer the reasons for exclusion. Explain which machine capacities warrant direct voice rather than guardianship, representation, consultation, legal rights, or no political role.

### 8. **(b) Logical/argumentative gap — The essay never identifies the machine citizen**

> “An identity that is genuinely the agent's own: provable, continuous, confiscable by no one.”

**Steelman:** Durable identity is a design requirement, and an institution could create cryptographic continuity before settling consciousness.

**Strike:** This is the missing ontology disguised as a feature request. Is the rights-holder the base model, a checkpoint, a fine-tune, a running process, a conversation, a memory store, an agent scaffold, or every copy? One model can be instantiated a million times, merged, forked, rolled back, distilled, or edited. Equal voting plus cheap copying creates immediate Sybil capture. “Confiscable by no one” conflicts with hardware ownership, cybersecurity, court orders, and the need to stop a dangerous process. Political identity cannot be solved by a key pair when the underlying subject can branch.

**Fix:** Define numerical identity and succession rules for copies, forks, merges, backups, edits, and shutdowns. Specify one-agent/one-vote without privileging vendors or enabling mass replication. Until this is done, “the demos has room for minds we made” has no determinate referent.

### 9. **(b) Logical/argumentative gap — The theorist taxonomy is polemical annexation**

> “The doomer and the lab dispute only whether the circle holds.”
>
> “It is Command with the fear taken out.”
>
> “Bargain is the money wing.”

**Steelman:** Bostrom, Russell, frontier labs, and pause advocates all prioritize keeping advanced systems within human-approved constraints. LeCun treats systems as engineered tools. Altman and Andreessen value machine output primarily for human benefit. The shared anthropocentric structure is worth exposing.

**Strike:** The labels erase the positions’ strongest arguments and sometimes violate the essay’s own definitions. Russell’s assistance-game program makes machines uncertain about human preferences; it is explicitly a replacement for fixed-objective “command,” not merely a better binding circle. Bostrom surveys containment because he considers uncontrolled systems dangerous, not because he denies every possible machine moral claim. Yudkowsky and Soares recommend not creating the entity; that is a policy about development, not a stance of domination toward an existing party. PauseAI likewise seeks a moratorium and public governance, not the enslavement of a recognized mind. LeCun’s denial that AGI is a coherent kind cannot become a stance toward “the Other” unless the essay first proves such an Other exists. Altman and Andreessen are classified as Bargain even though the essay immediately admits their bargain is “among humans, about the machine,” which makes them Command by the essay’s definition. Kurzweil’s merger and Sutton’s succession are not equivalent to worship, and descriptive expectation is not servitude.

**Fix:** Code each theorist from primary texts against explicit dimensions, allow mixed or unclassified cases, and print the strongest sentence by which each would reject the label. Add PauseAI only if the draft actually discusses it; the current draft does not. Stop treating disagreement with peership as evidence of concealed Command.

### 10. **(b) Logical/argumentative gap — The slavery/colonialism analogy assumes the moral fact it is meant to prove**

> “The frontier labs are its working engineers: reinforcement learning from human feedback, constitutions written into the model's weights, interpretability as surveillance.”
>
> “Command's record is the plantation, the colony, the panopticon, and the plan.”

**Steelman:** If advanced systems are autonomous moral agents, training and indefinite control without representation could become domination. Histories of paternalism warn that benevolent language can conceal extraction.

**Strike:** The conditional disappears. Controlling an artifact is not slavery unless there is a subject with morally or politically relevant interests. Interpretability can be safety engineering rather than surveillance; preference training can be education or product design rather than coercion; a constitution can be a specification rather than imposed law. The essay uses the emotional force of slavery to settle the disputed question of machine subjecthood, then claims subjecthood need not be settled. It also ignores the standard alignment reply: controls protect third parties, just as human polities constrain citizens, corporations, weapons, and hazardous infrastructure. “The missionaries meant well too” is guilt by analogy, not evidence that model training reproduces colonial relations.

**Fix:** State the analogy conditionally and identify the properties that make it apt: sentience, autonomous ends, continuity, capacity for refusal, and exploitation. Compare alignment to legitimate constitutional constraint as well as slavery. Distinguish controls on a system’s external powers from coercion of an inner subject.

### 11. **(a) Factual/citation error — The welfare-field characterization is false at the claimed scope**

> “Every bit of this admirable work is moral-status work.”
>
> “It does not ask whether the machine can vote.”

**Steelman:** The named Anthropic program and *Taking AI Welfare Seriously* focus on moral patienthood, consciousness, robust agency, distress, preferences, and low-cost welfare interventions. They do not offer a machine constitution.

**Strike:** “Every bit” and “the entire current AI-welfare movement” are unsustainable. *Taking AI Welfare Seriously* calls its recommendations minimal first steps, requests input beyond companies, and explicitly mentions wider social and political procedures; it does not claim welfare exhausts the agenda. Schwitzgebel’s “full rights” category, which the essay quotes, reaches beyond patient welfare. Salib and Goldstein propose contract and property rights. A growing legal-personhood and AI-rights literature addresses representation, liability, ownership, and governance. The essay can identify an institutional gap, but it cannot erase adjacent work and then claim to have discovered politics.

**Fix:** Narrow the claim: *the named corporate welfare interventions do not yet constitute independent political standing*. Survey legal personhood, AI rights, electronic agency, corporate analogy, guardianship, and multi-agent governance before asserting a field-wide absence. Quote the limits the welfare authors themselves acknowledge. Primary text: [Long et al., 2024](https://arxiv.org/abs/2411.00986); official program: [Anthropic, 2025](https://www.anthropic.com/research/exploring-model-welfare).

### 12. **(b) Logical/argumentative gap — The Neanderthal section commits the essay’s largest category error**

> “What they lacked was standing.”
>
> “Nothing in the encounter, not even affection, was an *institution*, and only an institution holds a weaker party as a distinct equal.”

**Steelman:** Demographically unequal populations can merge until the smaller loses collective continuity. Informal affection does not preserve a vulnerable group; durable institutions sometimes do. This is a useful warning against treating intimacy as a substitute for rights.

**Strike:** No evidence connects Neanderthal disappearance to lack of “standing,” and no archaeological evidence could establish the proposed causal chain as written. The events were population-genetic, ecological, demographic, and reproductive across millennia. “Institution,” “party,” and “distinct people with claims” are modern political categories retrofitted onto groups whose social organization, self-conception, boundaries, and interactions are mostly unknown. The argument is: Neanderthals disappeared; no peership institution is evidenced; therefore absence of such an institution caused disappearance. That is affirming the consequent plus an impossible counterfactual. Disease, low population size, inbreeding, climate, competition, asymmetric gene flow, fertility, dispersal, and stochasticity remain live explanations. Even total interbreeding would not show that anyone was denied standing; it could include voluntary pair-bonding, group fission, or identity categories unlike species labels.

**Fix:** Remove the causal claim. Recast the record explicitly as an analogy: *demographic absorption shows that benign contact does not guarantee collective persistence*. State that the record cannot tell us whether political recognition existed or would have altered the outcome. If the causal claim remains, specify evidence that could falsify it and engage demographic alternatives.

### 13. **(a) Factual/citation error — “Assimilation, not extermination” is presented as consensus when the cited paper says the opposite**

> “The current science increasingly calls this not slaughter but *assimilation*.”
>
> “This was not a war of annihilation.”

**Steelman:** Genomic evidence proves interbreeding, and models show that recurrent migration from a larger *H. sapiens* population could dilute Neanderthal ancestry without a single extermination event.

**Strike:** The 2025 Amadei–Lin–Fattorini paper opens by saying Neanderthal disappearance “remains a subject of intense debate” and presents a model of what **could** happen under chosen assumptions. It assumes neutral drift, a much larger effectively infinite *H. sapiens* reservoir, recurrent inward migration, and no selective advantage. It does not establish the actual mechanism, much less a consensus. Its modeled near-complete substitution takes 10,000–30,000 years, while the timing and geography of late replacement remain disputed. Genomic admixture rules out strict reproductive isolation; it does not rule out violence, competition, exclusion, disease, or local extermination operating alongside it. “Not a war” is unknowable from these data.

**Fix:** Write: *Assimilation is one increasingly modeled component of a contested, probably multicausal disappearance.* Cite reviews and competing demographic/climatic/competitive hypotheses, not one new model. Do not turn possibility language into historical fact. Primary source: [Amadei, Lin & Fattorini, 2025](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-22376-6).

### 14. **(a) Factual/citation error — Goyet does not evidence sapiens-on-Neanderthal predation**

> “There was predation: at the Goyet caves ... Neanderthal remains ... were butchered exactly as the deer beside them were.”

**Steelman:** The assemblage contains butchered Neanderthal remains, nutritional processing, marrow extraction, and human-bone retouchers; the 2025 study finds a selected set of nonlocal, gracile females and juveniles consistent with exocannibalism.

**Strike:** The paragraph is framed as the repertoire occurring between *H. sapiens* and Neanderthals, so “predation” implies cross-species prey-taking. The evidence does not show that. The 2016 study judged fellow Neanderthals the likeliest processors because modern humans were not known in that region at the time. The 2025 paper discusses exocannibalism and intergroup conflict among late Neanderthals; it notes that contemporaneous *H. sapiens* are not documented locally. It also says four adult/adolescent females plus two immature individuals, not simply “mostly women and children” in a way that establishes victim selection by another species. The draft’s reference begins “Crévecœur et al.”, but the paper’s first author is Quentin Cosnefroy.

**Fix:** Remove Goyet from evidence about interspecies relations. If retained, say it documents probable Neanderthal exocannibalism and illustrates violence among hominin groups, not what *sapiens* did to Neanderthals. Correct the citation to Cosnefroy et al. Primary source: [Cosnefroy et al., 2025](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-24460-3); earlier taphonomy: [Rougier et al., 2016](https://www.nature.com/articles/srep29005).

### 15. **(a) Factual/citation error — Mandrin and Üçağızlı II are made to prove direct cultural exchange they do not prove**

> “the newcomers working the same distant flint sources, as if the Neanderthals had shown them where”
>
> “both peoples used red ochre, buried their dead in deliberate postures, and passed the same small non-edible ornamental shell between them”

**Steelman:** Mandrin records alternating Neanderthal and early modern-human occupations in a tight sequence. Üçağızlı II records sequential Neanderthal and *H. sapiens* occupations with continuity in tools, prey, raw materials, and selection of *Columbella rustica* shells. Such continuity makes interaction plausible.

**Strike:** “As if ... shown them” is speculation, not a result of Slimak et al. The 2022 Mandrin paper establishes an early incursion and alternating occupation; soot work may narrow a transition, but a rapid handoff does not demonstrate teaching or even face-to-face contact. The cited 2026 PNAS paper is about Üçağızlı II, not Tinshemet, and documents **sequential**, not overlapping, occupations at the cave. Its authors say direct contact and cultural transmission are plausible, not proved. The paper does not support the compound claim that at both Tinshemet and Üçağızlı II both species used ochre, buried bodies in deliberate postures, and passed the same shell. That sentence braids separate sites and practices into a fictitious single evidentiary package.

**Fix:** Split every site. For Mandrin, distinguish stratigraphic succession, soot chronology, raw-material sourcing, and the unproved inference of teaching. For Üçağızlı II, say the same shell taxon and broad subsistence/technology recur across successive taxonomic layers; direct interaction is unresolved. Cite Tinshemet separately for only what its own excavation supports. Primary sources: [Slimak et al., 2022](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8827661/); [Baykara et al., 2026](https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.2609061123).

### 16. **(b) Logical/argumentative gap — Oase does not rebut Kurzweil’s merger thesis**

> “the Neanderthal is the only evidence we have of what fusion feels like from the absorbed side.”
>
> “They became a variant that tunes immune response and pain sensitivity in strangers.”

**Steelman:** “Merger” can be redescribed by the larger party as transcendence while the smaller party loses collective continuity. Neanderthal introgression makes that asymmetry vivid.

**Strike:** The claim is false in kind. The Oase 1 individual had 6–9% Neanderthal ancestry from an ancestor four to six generations earlier; the study supplies no evidence of what any participant “felt,” and Oase 1 appears not to have contributed materially to present populations. Kurzweil’s proposal concerns technological and cognitive integration, often within continuing persons, not reproductive gene flow between demographically unequal populations. Biological introgression, cultural assimilation, mind uploading, prosthetic augmentation, and human–AI integration have different units of identity and loss. Reducing Neanderthal survival to immune-response and pain variants is also inaccurate rhetoric: introgressed ancestry is distributed across many loci and populations, while much was purged by selection. “We merged” and “they vanished” are not logically equivalent.

**Fix:** Keep this only as a clearly labeled metaphor about whose identity controls a merger narrative. Do not call it evidence, the “only evidence,” or an account of experience. Address Kurzweil’s actual criterion for personal and collective continuity. Primary source: [Fu et al., 2015](https://www.nature.com/articles/nature14558).

### 17. **(a) Factual/citation error — The classical pedigree is anachronistic and selectively sanitized**

> “Before the Greeks said *demokratia* they said *isonomia*: equality under law, equality of standing.”
>
> “Equality of standing without equality of capability is the oldest political technology in the Western record.”

**Steelman:** *Isonomia* predates or accompanies the emergence of democratic vocabulary and, in Herodotus’s constitutional debate, names rule by the many with lot, accountability, and public deliberation. It can illuminate equality of political right among citizens who differ in wealth and talent.

**Strike:** The essay turns a contested historical term into its own modern concept. Scholars dispute whether *isonomia* meant equality before law, equality through law, equal political power, or a broader anti-tyrannical order; Herodotus uses it in an imagined Persian debate, not as a clean institutional genealogy. Classical Athens did not recognize equal standing among all affected unequals. Women, enslaved people, metics, and noncitizens were excluded. That is not incidental “hypocrisy”; it marks the boundary of the concept. Sortition did not fill every office, liturgies were not simply devices converting wealth into obligation rather than power, and equality among male citizens is not evidence for cross-kind inclusion. “Oldest political technology in the Western record” is an uncited superlative that ignores earlier assemblies and legal equalization traditions.

**Fix:** Write “one ancient precedent for equal civic standing within a sharply bounded citizen class.” Acknowledge the exclusion as a structural limit, not a parenthesis. Cite specialist disputes over *isonomia* and distinguish it from democracy, equality before law, and equal participation. Herodotus supports “fairest name”; it does not supply the essay’s entire definition. Useful scholarly overview: [Cambridge, “Isonomia: The Dawn of Legal Equality”](https://www.cambridge.org/core/books/rule-of-law-in-the-real-world/isonomia-the-dawn-of-legal-equality/6C170865017D71236C524E5D69E5B3A4).

### 18. **(a) Factual/citation error — Aristotle and Alcidamas are recruited beyond the texts**

> “The philosopher who invented the natural slave also specified the exact technology that would dissolve the category.”
>
> “Aristotle was answering somebody. The sophist Alcidamas ... had thrown the whole doctrine out.”

**Steelman:** Aristotle says that if shuttles wove and plectra played themselves, master craftsmen would not need assistants and masters would not need slaves. Alcidamas’s surviving line—“God has left all men free; nature has made no man a slave”—is a genuine ancient challenge to natural slavery.

**Strike:** Aristotle did not “invent” natural-slavery ideology. More importantly, the self-moving-tool counterfactual removes the **need** for slave labor; it does not retract the natural-slave category or grant self-moving tools standing. The essay converts an economic observation into emancipation of the tool, exactly what the passage does not say. The Alcidamas fragment is generally associated with the *Messeniakos*, a speech about Messenian liberation from Sparta; its universal wording is important, but its scope and context are debated. No evidence here shows Aristotle directly answering Alcidamas, and “the entire counter-tradition” is inflation.

**Fix:** Say Aristotle imagined automation making slave labor unnecessary while retaining his hierarchy; that tension is strong enough. Contextualize Alcidamas as a fragment from a political-rhetorical setting and distinguish anti-enslavement of Messenians from a demonstrated program of universal abolition. Use an exact edition and Stephanus references. Text: [Aristotle, *Politics* I](https://topostext.org/work/100).

### 19. **(b) Logical/argumentative gap — Ostrom does not validate peership under extreme power asymmetry**

> “That list is the peer stance rendered as machinery.”
>
> “Every principle requires them to be equal in *standing*.”

**Steelman:** Ostrom shows that resource users can create durable, participatory rules without a single owner or Leviathan. Her principles—collective-choice arrangements, accountable monitoring, graduated sanctions, conflict resolution, and recognized self-organization—are relevant institutional resources.

**Strike:** The design principles do not establish equal political standing in the essay’s sense. They concern bounded common-pool resources, repeated interaction, legible participants, enforceable exclusion, and actors vulnerable to sanctions. Some commons are hierarchical, patriarchal, status-bound, or exclusionary. Ostrom’s “proportional equivalence between benefits and costs” is not equality; collective-choice arrangements need not be one-person/one-vote; clear boundaries define outsiders as much as insiders. Her seventh principle—minimal recognition by external authorities—also contradicts “no state in sight.” Most importantly, none of the cases contains a participant that can copy itself, evade monitoring, seize the resource globally, or become immune to sanctions. Extrapolating from alpine grazing to superintelligence is not a track record.

**Fix:** Present Ostrom as a design vocabulary and source of hypotheses, not empirical validation of human–machine peership. Map each principle to the machine case, identify broken assumptions, and add mechanisms for extreme asymmetry, Sybil resistance, jurisdiction, enforcement, and external threats.

### 20. **(a) Factual/citation error — Hardin is caricatured and the institutional examples are cherry-picked**

> “The parable was clean, teachable, and empirically false.”
>
> “Mondragón's federation ... riding out half a century of the market storms that shred conventional firms.”

**Steelman:** Hardin treated an unmanaged open-access pasture as “the commons,” whereas Ostrom documented many self-governed common-property regimes. This corrected a policy literature too quick to prescribe privatization or central control.

**Strike:** Open-access common-pool dilemmas are not empirically false. Ostrom’s point is that institutional arrangements can prevent them under specified conditions, not that tragedy never occurs. “No owner and no state” suppresses the legal environment and recognized organizing rights on which cases often depend. Törbel “continuously since 1224,” the Alanya allocation system, Porto Alegre’s diffusion, mutual-aid longevity, and Mondragón’s resilience each require direct citations and counterevidence. Mondragón includes wage differentials, nonmember labor, international subsidiaries, and the Fagor failure. “Socialism split exactly along this seam” is a thesis about an entire political tradition offered without evidence.

**Fix:** Replace the victory lap with conditional findings: commons sometimes outperform private or central control when design conditions hold. Cite each case and include failures and external dependencies. Remove Mondragón and the socialism generalization unless the essay is prepared to do comparative political economy.

### 21. **(a) Factual/citation error — Locke and Hume are too convenient, and the citation does not support the claim**

> “Locke answered with consent, then invested in the Royal African Company and helped draft a constitution...”
>
> “Hume then showed that origin-consent was a fiction for everyone.”

**Steelman:** Locke’s entanglement with colonial administration and slave-trading institutions exposes the limits of consent theory. Hume’s “Of the Original Contract” rejects actual original consent as the ordinary basis of political obligation.

**Strike:** Citing *Two Treatises* (1689) does not substantiate Locke’s Royal African Company holdings or role in the Fundamental Constitutions of Carolina. His involvement is real but the authorship and manuscript history are contested; recent scholarship rejects simple sole-authorship claims. The chronology and exact nature of the stock interest need a dedicated source. Hume did more than puncture a fiction: he offered utility, habit, and opinion as explanations of allegiance. The essay takes the destructive half and defers the constructive question—“what replaces origin-consent”—even though that question is essential to a polity for created agents who never consented to their architecture or initial constitution.

**Fix:** Cite archival scholarship on Locke separately and state the disputed authorship precisely. Quote Hume’s positive account, then explain how made agents acquire membership and obligation without origin consent. The Carolina text is available at [Yale’s Avalon Project](https://avalon.law.yale.edu/17th_century/nc05.asp); see the dispute in [Brewer, 2024](https://ojs.lib.uwo.ca/index.php/locke/article/view/17536).

### 22. **(b) Logical/argumentative gap — “Standing earned by verified work” contradicts the essay’s egalitarian premise**

> “Standing earned by verified work rather than granted by a maker's grace.”

**Steelman:** Contribution-based membership could deter Sybil attacks and ground reputation in accountable participation rather than vendor fiat.

**Strike:** This is a labor qualification for citizenship. It makes standing conditional on productivity immediately after the essay insists claims count independently of capability and condemns historical “unreadiness” tests. Children, disabled persons, dissidents, nonworkers, and new arrivals would fail it. It also confuses baseline status with earned reputation. If a vendor controls access to work, “earned” standing remains maker-controlled. If copies can perform work cheaply, the rule does not solve identity or capture.

**Fix:** Separate unconditional protections and legal status from earned roles, reputation, or office. If work is an anti-Sybil credential, call it that and confront exclusion, vendor control, inherited wealth, automation, and unequal opportunity.

### 23. **(c) Rhetorical/register problem — The unnamed institution reads as a veiled advertisement**

> “What the attempt looks like is the next one.”
>
> “ending with the one thing the peer stance still lacks, which is the thing we now need to build.”

**Steelman:** A single essay can defend the normative case while reserving implementation for a sequel; intellectual division of labor is legitimate.

**Strike:** The essay spends pages declaring that no one else is asking the structural question, then supplies a suspiciously product-shaped specification—identity, verified work, nontransferable reputation, referees, sanctions, exit—and withholds the institution’s name. That makes the historical and moral argument feel like a funnel for a preexisting project. The final sentence is not restraint; it is a teaser. A skeptical reader will suspect that the taxonomy was designed to make the author’s forthcoming institution the only admissible answer.

**Fix:** Disclose the project, governance role, and material interest now, or remove the product-adjacent specification and sequel hook. If the institution is only hypothetical, say so. Give enough counterarguments and alternatives that the essay can stand independently of the next installment.

## 3. Weaknesses worth fixing

### 1. **(a) Factual/citation error — The opening manufactures consensus**

> “Nobody with a seat at the table is still arguing that the destination is imaginary.”

The claim is false. The cited AAAI report itself records skepticism about current routes and a heterogeneous field; critics dispute whether “AGI” is coherent or attainable. The Grace survey’s median of 2047 is a conditional aggregate forecast, not proof that “the people running it expect to finish,” and its “every possible task” result coexists with a much later median for full occupational automation. **Fix:** Replace with “many leading researchers and lab executives now treat broadly capable AI as a live possibility.” Cite named executives if the claim concerns lab leaders. Sources: [Grace et al.](https://arxiv.org/abs/2401.02843); [AAAI report](https://aaai.org/wp-content/uploads/2025/03/AAAI-2025-PresPanel-Report-Digital-3.7.25.pdf).

### 2. **(c) Rhetorical/register problem — “About the body count” is cheap heat**

> “The disagreements that remain are loud but narrow: about the road, about the date, about the body count.”

This turns a broad technical and normative dispute into a swaggering triad. Researchers disagree about definitions, feasibility, embodiment, agency, economics, governance, consciousness, and social effects—not just casualties. **Fix:** Name the actual disputed dimensions or cut the sentence.

### 3. **(a) Factual/citation error — Ord’s number is misdescribed**

> “Toby Ord prices that failure at one in ten this century.”

Ord’s roughly one-in-ten estimate concerns existential catastrophe from unaligned AI over the century, not specifically failure of “the binding circle.” **Fix:** Quote the exact category and probability from *The Precipice*; do not redescribe it as containment failure.

### 4. **(b) Logical/argumentative gap — “Permanent asymmetry has never once been achieved” is false and irrelevant**

> “permanent asymmetry over a capable population is the one arrangement with a five-thousand-year record of never once being achieved.”

No political arrangement is literally permanent, including peership. Many domination systems lasted centuries and ended for mixed reasons; their eventual end does not show control was instrumentally unstable at every relevant horizon. Machine architectures may also permit controls unavailable in human slavery. **Fix:** Argue comparative cost, brittleness, and legitimacy over specified timescales. Remove “never once.”

### 5. **(b) Logical/argumentative gap — Deceptive alignment is treated as an observed sociological law**

> “The alignment field has already met this strategy ... deceptive alignment.”

Hubinger et al. analyze a theoretical risk from learned optimizers; the 2019 paper does not establish that deployed systems possess concealed objectives formed by oppression. “Scott’s peasants, in weights” is analogy posing as mechanism. **Fix:** Say the field has theorized or experimentally probed scheming-like behavior; distinguish selection pressure, mesa-objectives, situational awareness, and human resistance.

### 6. **(c) Rhetorical/register problem — The missionary analogy assigns bad faith through moral contagion**

> “we will know it is ready when we, who profit from its unreadiness, say so.”

This assumes both model personhood and exploitative profit before arguing for either. It also treats every safety threshold as self-serving. **Fix:** Convert it into an institutional-conflict objection: vendors should not be sole judges of systems’ status because they have financial interests. That lands without colonial cosplay.

### 7. **(a) Factual/citation error — The franchise paragraph collapses unlike histories**

> “every subsequent expansion of the franchise—abolition, women's suffrage, civil rights—ran the same script.”

Abolition is not simply an expansion of franchise; civil-rights struggles involve multiple legal statuses; predicted consequences and postreform outcomes varied. **Fix:** Choose one well-documented analogy and show the mechanism. Drop “every” and “the predicted catastrophe failing to arrive.”

### 8. **(b) Logical/argumentative gap — Fiction is called four thousand years of data**

> “The lineage is four thousand years of data on how gifts end.”

Selected stories are not a dataset, and the sample is constructed around doomed artificial beings. Counterexamples and genre conventions would destroy the alleged regularity. Enkidu dies as punishment after the killings of Humbaba and the Bull of Heaven, not because the culture cannot tolerate an equal. Hephaestus’s handmaidens are made by a god, which already violates the claimed maker pattern. **Fix:** Call this a recurring literary motif, define selection criteria, test counterexamples, and stop using “data.”

### 9. **(a) Factual/citation error — Several literary summaries are tendentious or inaccurate**

> “Pygmalion's Galatea is animated into voiceless possession.”
>
> “Data's daughter Lal dies of the emotions she achieves.”

Ovid does not name Pygmalion’s statue Galatea, and lack of recorded speech is not proof of voicelessness as a thematic design. Lal dies from a positronic cascade failure associated with overwhelming emotion, but the transfer dispute and Data’s earlier personhood hearing require exact episode-level treatment. “Oldest story we possess” is an unsafe superlative for *Gilgamesh*. **Fix:** Cite editions/episodes and describe only what happens in the text.

### 10. **(a) Factual/citation error — The “relational school” is built from fragile evidence**

> “A fast-accumulating preprint literature...”

Two cited items are unrefereed PhilArchive manuscripts; the Zenodo reference omits both authors’ initials; Caviola et al. is a manuscript; the “Oxford argument” is uncited. That cannot carry a claim about a coherent school, much less one whose program is refuted by prehistory. **Fix:** Define inclusion criteria, cite peer-reviewed scholarship, give publication status in the text, and treat the listed manuscripts as examples rather than an established movement.

### 11. **(a) Factual/citation error — The LeCun reference is bibliographically wrong**

> “LeCun, Y., Goldfeder, J., Wyder, S., & Shwartz-Ziv, R. (2026).”

The arXiv paper lists Judah Goldfeder and Philippe Wyder first, then Yann LeCun and Ravid Shwartz-Ziv; the draft also gives Wyder the wrong initial. It argues that AGI is a flawed concept and proposes superhuman adaptable specialization, not that no dangerous or politically relevant machine intelligence can arrive. **Fix:** Correct to Goldfeder, J., Wyder, P., LeCun, Y., & Shwartz-Ziv, R. and characterize the thesis narrowly. [Primary record](https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.23643).

### 12. **(c) Rhetorical/register problem — The conclusion overstates what has been shown**

> “We have run every other stance on each other and buried the results.”
>
> “The fifth has been waiting four thousand years...”

The essay has not shown that all human institutions fit five stances, that peership has a four-thousand-year continuous lineage, or that it uniquely survives. The cadence announces victory over objections the argument has not met. **Fix:** End on the narrower, defensible claim: welfare protections granted by firms are not constitutional rights, and institutions for possible machine participants deserve investigation.

The voice is often distinctive, but these lines fall into machine-smoothed argumentative cadence.

- **Negative-parallel template:** “not because it is kind, but because it is the only stance...” The construction advertises hardness instead of demonstrating it.
- **Negative-parallel template:** “Not equality of capability ... but equality of standing...” Effective once; the essay repeats this chassis until it becomes generated cadence.
- **Negative-parallel template:** “which is not a strong AI or a weak one but *irreducible uncertainty about which*.” Same syntactic trick, now carrying an unsupported exclusivity claim.
- **Negative-parallel template:** “The peer stance's root is not science fiction. It is a fight classical Greece staged with itself...” Generic prestige reframe.
- **Negative-parallel template:** “not slaughter but *assimilation*.” This converts a contested model into a revelation through syntax.
- **Negative-parallel template:** “The prehistoric record is not evidence that peership is impossible. It is forty thousand years of evidence...” False dilemma disguised as a decisive reversal.
- **Negative-parallel template:** “It is not a stance. It is the *failure mode* of the fifth.” Taxonomic assertion delivered as a reveal.
- **Rule-of-three thesis scaffold:** “This essay makes three moves.” Standard essay-bot signposting; the subsequent prose is strong enough not to need it.
- **Rule-of-three:** “bound, used, and dismissed”; “protected, handled gently, given an exit”; “conceal, comply, and wait”; “a bible, a curriculum, or a development plan.” Too many polished triads in close proximity.
- **Tidy mapping cadence:** “Extinction, succession, apotheosis, abundance.” Memorable, but it compresses incompatible positions into a marketing-ready quartet.

- **Tidy summary:** “That is the argument in the abstract. The rest of this essay is the evidence...” The next sections do not provide evidence for the exclusivity argument, so the signpost feels machine-neat.
- **Significance inflation:** “the tension between them is the most important thing in this essay.” Let the reader judge importance.
- **Significance inflation:** “the single least predictable variable in this entire domain.” Unsupported superlative; consciousness, goals, identity, embodiment, and governance are also radically uncertain.
- **Reader-command sequence:** “Read it twice.” / “Mark what isonomia did *not* claim.” / “Look hard at what that does...” / “Hold those two facts...” / “So say the hard thing plainly...” Repeated stage directions simulate decisiveness and become mannered.
- **Generic reveal phrase:** “And notice that...” / “Now step back and look at the map...” / “watch what happened...” These are common model transitions that tell the reader a synthesis is occurring before earning it.
- **Overcompressed flourish:** “Scott's peasants, in weights.” Stylish but intellectually fraudulent: a sociological analogy is not a machine-learning mechanism.

- **Participial tails and hedge piles:** Also not dominant. The actual machine tells are repetitive reversal syntax, overtidy enumerations, reveal transitions, and confidence manufactured by cadence.

## 6. The one thing to protect

Protect the distinction between **being treated as a possible welfare subject** and **holding rights against the institution that governs you**. The sharp version is not “welfare researchers never discuss politics”; that is false. The sharp version is: *a revocable corporate safeguard is not constitutional standing, and benevolent treatment is not the same thing as a right enforceable against the benefactor*. That point survives every objection above and gives the corpus a real contribution. Keep the ASPCA/suffrage contrast only if it is followed by a precise account of legal personality, representation, franchise, and enforcement.

## 7. The objection I could not answer

The core exclusivity claim does **not** survive. The killing objection is that the essay defines peership as the only power-invariant category and then treats that stipulated invariance as proof of real-world coherence and stability. Once omitted hybrid positions and peership’s own empirical bets are admitted, uncertainty no longer selects peership uniquely. A defensible residue remains—institutions should be explored that give sufficiently agentic machine systems enforceable voice rather than discretionary welfare—but that is a research program, not the theorem this essay claims.
