## Edit 7 — the dragon-king error (§ The Repertoire, Dependent Bargain paragraph; SUBSTANTIVE FIX)

Yang's sun-punishment victim is the city god, not the dragon-king (Yang, 1961, pp. 92–93: "the magistrate thrashing the city god under the hot sun, demonstrating his assumed temporal authority to force the god to give rain"; "the peasant masses exposing the city god to the scorching sun in order to induce him to bring on precipitation"). The dragon gods on the same pages receive sacrifices and a hired community play, not the sun treatment.

**SECOND FINDING (Hansen in hand): the "stripped of its rank by petition" claim is also unsupported as stated.** Hansen documents the upward half of the bureaucracy in detail — titles granted and promoted by petition to the throne, backed by investigated miracles (ch. 4, "The Granting of Titles," pp. 79–104) — plus an 1101 official's demand that deities in the register who "had not really performed miracles" be reexamined (p. 83), and the suppression of unregistered cults. She does not document a god's existing rank being stripped by petition. And the following sentence in v1.1 ("That demotable god is the whole point of the name") leans on the unsupported clause.

**BEFORE:**
> in a drought a magistrate might haul a dragon-king's image into the punishing sun to force the rain it owed, and a god that failed could be stripped of its rank by petition to the throne (Yang, 1961; Hansen, 1990). That demotable god is the whole point of the name: the entity is a counterparty, not a co-author of the order, and the human keeps the pen.

**AFTER (Option 1 — evidence in hand, recommended):**
> gods earned their ranks through petitions to the throne backed by investigated miracles, an official could demand that deities who failed to perform be reexamined for their place in the register of sacrifices (Hansen, 1990, ch. 4, p. 83), and in a drought a magistrate might thrash the city god's image or leave it under the punishing sun to force the rain it owed (Yang, 1961, pp. 92–93). That accountable god is the whole point of the name: the entity is a counterparty, not a co-author of the order, and the human keeps the pen.

**Option 2 — rescue the demotion claim (one check needed):** Hansen's own n. 8 points at Yang's chapter "Ethicopolitical Cults: Guidance by the Way of the Gods" (Yang, 1961, pp. 144–179) as the classic statement of the state-control view. If the demotion/degradation of deities appears there (Dan has the book), "demotable" survives with a Yang pin instead of Hansen. Check pp. 144–179 before choosing.

Reference pins either way: Yang [city-god punishment: pp. 92–93; if used, ethicopolitical cults: pp. 144–179]; Hansen [title-granting by petition: ch. 4, pp. 79–104; reexamination of non-performers: p. 83].
