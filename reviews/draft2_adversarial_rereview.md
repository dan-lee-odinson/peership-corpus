## 1. Verdict

**CONDITIONAL PASS on the argument; NOT YET RELEASE-READY as a package.** Draft 2 clears the original killing objection: §2 now gives a recognizable, conditional argument from contestatory membership to co-authorship, limits its scope, identifies a serious rival, and states a defeat condition. No new killing objection replaces the old one. The remaining substantive issues are narrower: the bridge needs one scope correction and its proper Pettit source; the claim that all seven institutional functions are individually necessary is asserted rather than defended; and the paper understates how much content is new. The remaining mechanical gates—search protocol, bibliography, synchronized apparatus, DOI metadata, and actual one-page layout—still prevent deposit as canonical v1.0.

## 4. Remaining substantive issues

### 4.1 The bridge is now real, but one premise is overgeneralized and its strongest Pettit support is missing

**What succeeded.** Section 2 does not merely repeat the definition. Its meta-level claim is intelligible: if an adverse rule-writer may unilaterally set the questions, evidence, remedies, and amendment boundaries through which contestation occurs, then discretion has been displaced rather than controlled. The section also allows contestation alone to suffice under specified conditions and makes fiduciary trusteeship a defeater rather than a straw opponent. That is enough to clear the original killing objection for a preprint-level research-program statement.

**Residual problem 1—scope.** Section 2 says:

> “For a made mind it is structural: every rule of the order was written by and for a different kind of member.”

That is true of a **first machine claimant entering a human-only order**, not of every made mind under every future order. Section 2 itself later allows that an order's terms may already be fitted to the party's kind. The universal sentence therefore conflicts with the paper's own contestation-alone sufficiency condition.

**Exact fix:**

> “For a first machine claimant entering a human-only order, this risk is presumptively structural: the basic-status rules were written by and for a different kind of member. It need not remain structural where later institutions have genuinely fitted their terms to the party's kind.”

**Residual problem 2—citation.** Section 2 says Pettit's contestatory citizen already has “authorship at one remove” through membership in an electing demos. That reading is plausible and supported by Pettit's distinction between the **authorial/electoral** and **editorial/contestatory** dimensions of democracy, but the paper cites only Pettit (2025). The more direct source is Pettit's two-dimensional democratic theory and *On the People's Terms*. The existing `.bib` already contains `pettit2012onthepeoples`; use it. Pettit's authorial/editorial distinction is also explicit in his earlier “Two-Dimensional Democracy and the International Domain.” [Princeton's publication record](https://ppettit.scholar.princeton.edu/publications/democracy-electoral-and-contestatory) and the [paper text](https://www.iilj.org/wp-content/uploads/2016/11/Pettit-Two-dimensional-Democracy-and-the-International-Domain-2004.pdf) directly support the distinction.

**Minimum fix:** cite Pettit (2004/2006 and/or 2012) in the “stopping point” paragraph and add the exact work to the final references. Keep Pettit (2025) for the current formulation of nondomination; do not ask that article to carry the authorial/editorial institutional history by itself.

**Residual problem 3—scope of the conclusion.** The bridge directly establishes a need to control the meta-level agenda. It supports independent proposal rights and a non-discretionary route to decision. The paper should add one sentence explaining why this also requires §3(b), “accountable representation or direct participation in the body that decides,” rather than only external agenda access plus independent review. The answer can be short: a route whose final decision-maker is neither accountable to nor partly constituted by the governed party may hear proposals without sharing control.

### 4.2 “The seven are individually necessary” is a new assertion, not an aggregation argument

Section 9 now states:

> “The seven are individually necessary as *functions*.”

This answers the prior review's request for an aggregation rule syntactically, but it does not defend the rule. Publicity, due process, independent enforcement, amendment, and accountable representation follow fairly directly from the paper's nondomination and co-authorship accounts. **Exit and fork do not.** The thesis activates specifically where feasible exit is absent, and §8 explains how internal protections matter in that case. It is therefore not self-evident that both individual exit and collective fork are necessary functions of every fully peer-like order, rather than strong anti-entrenchment mechanisms or substitutable safeguards.

The tension is not fatal, but it is load-bearing because §9 says every rival implementation must exhibit all seven. Either:

1. defend why full peership cannot exist without both exit and fork, including cases where substrate dependence makes one physically unavailable; or
2. distinguish **constitutive requirements** from **strong anti-entrenchment safeguards**.

**Suggested replacement:**

> “Publicity, due process, independent enforcement, and a consequential route through amendment or accountable representation are constitutive functions of full peership. Exit and fork are the program's strongest anti-entrenchment safeguards; an implementation that omits either must supply a functional substitute that makes separation, revision, or resistance to dead-hand control materially credible.”

If the author intends all seven to remain strictly necessary, add the missing argument rather than using the word *necessary* as the argument.

### 4.3 The paper understates its genuinely new contributions

Draft 2 admirably discloses that the bridge is new, but it still says in §3:

> “Seven definitions, held constant across the corpus…”

The revision notes and pending ledger correctly identify the five-part co-authorship minimum as a **new claim**. Section 12 similarly calls this paper “Consolidation plus one payment,” even though it also introduces the co-authorship minimum, the precautionary duties of §10, and six new program-level tests.

This is a transparency defect, not a reason to remove the additions.

**Exact fixes:**

- Replace “Seven definitions, held constant across the corpus” with: “Seven definitions carried forward from the corpus and sharpened here; the five-part co-authorship minimum is new and logged as such.”
- Replace “Consolidation plus one payment” in the §12 table with: “Consolidation plus explicit new proposals: the §2 bridge, §3 co-authorship minimum, §10 precautionary duties, and §11 program-level tests.”
- Adjust the “What it does not establish” cell accordingly; these additions are arguments and proposed tests, not validated doctrine.

### 4.4 The represented-contestation rung needs one terminological sentence

Section 1 now says wrongability plus subjection ground “protection and access to represented contestation,” while norm-responsiveness allows direct contestation. The table then places represented contestation under **Protected status** and direct contestation under **Contestatory member**. This is reconcilable and substantially resolves the capability ambiguity, but the prose definition of contestatory membership in §3 does not explicitly state that it means direct exercise in this ladder.

**Minimum fix:** add to the definition:

> “In this ladder, a protected party may exercise contestation through an independent representative; *contestatory membership* names the rung at which the party can exercise that standing directly.”

This keeps the child analogy, central antecedent, and table aligned.

## 5. Remaining factual, citation, and apparatus gates

The paper says in §1 that the sourcebook contains “protocol and date,” repeats that the “domains and method” are recorded, and the reference annotation says “protocol recorded therein.” The supplied sourcebook names broad sources—“Web / PhilPapers / Zenodo scan”—but does not record search strings, database limits, inclusion criteria, result capture, or archived outputs. Until the protocol is added, the paper must not say it is already there.

**Two valid fixes:**

## 6. One-page *Peership Theses* audit

### Substantive compression

It no longer silently outruns the canonical paper on the principal issues.

Three edits remain:

1. **Thesis 7 grammar:** “Full peership proposes that membership…” makes the status itself the proposing agent. Use:

   > “Peership proposes that full membership additionally requires a non-discretionary route into the formation or revision of basic-status rules.”

2. **Consultation denial:** “does not treat consultation as co-authorship—unless…” reads as an exception to the denial. Use:

   > “Consultation alone is not co-authorship. A process counts as co-authorship only when it carries a non-discretionary route to agenda setting and binding decision.”

3. **Grantor wording:** Thesis 5 correctly substitutes “recognizing authority and adverse counterparties” but its next sentence returns to “grantor.” Replace with “authority or counterparty.”

## 7. What should not be reopened

The following are now settled for this revision cycle unless a new contradiction appears:

- the conditional contestatory core;
- present models are not asserted conscious or peer-like;
- moral status and political standing remain distinct;
- welfare protection is a floor, not full peership;
- norm-responsiveness concerns exercise, while raw capability and power do not ground worth;
- protection or admission does not automatically entail equal franchise;
- legitimate safety constraints can coexist with nondomination;
- role reversal is expressly comparative and diagnostic, not proof;
- ISONOMIA remains one implementation hypothesis and its simulations establish only sampled economic behavior;
- the open problems may remain open after publication.

The Draft 2 bridge may be attacked as a new argument, but its mere conditionality or defeat condition is no longer a defect. The standard is now whether the argument is stated accurately and sourced adequately, not whether it compels every reader.
