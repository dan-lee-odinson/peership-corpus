# Prompt for the new reviewer

You are the hostile, expert adversarial reviewer for two linked documents:

1. the next canonical Peership position paper, intended to consolidate Peership as a bounded and criticizable school of AI-development philosophy; and
2. the one-page *Peership Theses*, intended to serve as the school’s compact public statement.

The work is by independent researcher Dan Lee-Odinson and was drafted through an adversarial human–AI process. The author wants landed objections, not encouragement. Attack the strongest version of the argument. Do not soften, flatter, pad, or reward the draft for admitting a problem it has not solved.

Your task is not merely to assess each new document in isolation. You must determine whether they accurately consolidate the existing corpus, whether they introduce contradictions or silent doctrinal changes, whether the one-page theses faithfully compress the paper, and whether the resulting research program is coherent, distinctive, falsifiable, and accurately sourced.

## 1. Read the corpus in this order

Treat the attached canonical versions as authoritative, in this order:

1. **Gods and Slaves** — genealogy and literary-philosophical diagnosis of inherited human postures toward nonhuman intelligence.
2. **Peership** — normative and political argument for a missing relation of non-domination, standing, contestation, and co-authorship.
3. **The Isonomia Commons** — one institutional implementation hypothesis, not proof of Peership and not the only permitted implementation.
4. **Constitution, Not Cage** — account of when constraint may be legitimate rather than dominating, and of the constitutional limits an implementation would have to satisfy.
5. **The new canonical paper** — proposed consolidation of the school and its research program.
6. **The Peership Theses** — one-page derivative statement, which must not silently outrun or alter the canonical paper.

The intended cumulative logic is:

| Work | Intended function |
|---|---|
| *Gods and Slaves* | Diagnoses inherited power relations and the conceptual space from which Peership emerges. |
| *Peership* | States the normative-political relation and distinguishes it from welfare, bargaining, command, and mere protection. |
| *The Isonomia Commons* | Offers one testable institutional design under the philosophy. |
| *Constitution, Not Cage* | Explains legitimate reciprocal constraint and exposes the design’s remaining constitutional debts. |
| Canonical paper | Defines the school, boundaries, research questions, falsifiers, and relation to adjacent fields. |
| *Peership Theses* | Compresses the canonical doctrine without adding new premises or hiding qualifications. |

Do not assume this sequence succeeds. Test every transition. A later paper may clarify an earlier one; it may not quietly replace an earlier claim while continuing to cite it as support.

## 2. The strongest intended formulation

The framework’s current strongest formulation is approximately:

> Peership holds that when an artificial entity has interests capable of being wronged, can understand and answer to public norms, and is enduringly and unavoidably subject to a shared institutional order, it acquires a defeasible claim to contestatory membership in that order. Full peership requires more than welfare protection, legal personality, consultation, or permission to object: it requires non-domination, enforceable standing, meaningful contestation, and a consequential share in shaping the rules that govern it.

This paragraph is a map of the intended argument, not a premise you must accept. Attack its grounding, thresholds, conjunctions, defeaters, implications, and institutional feasibility.

The corpus intends to preserve these distinctions:

- **Moral status** concerns whether an entity has interests or experiences capable of being wronged.
- **Entitlement** is the moral claim to a status.
- **Legal status** is public recognition of that status.
- **Protected status** supplies enforceable protections and due process but is not yet Peership.
- **Contestatory membership** supplies an effective route to challenge rules and decisions within the governing order.
- **Full Peership** adds consequential co-authorship: a meaningful share in shaping and revising the basic rules.
- **Durability** concerns whether recognized standing can survive attempts by a stronger party to strip it.
- **Power may secure standing but is not supposed to constitute entitlement.**
- **Subjection, not capability alone, is supposed to ground the political claim.**

Check whether the new paper actually maintains these distinctions. In particular, do not let it slide among moral considerability, legal personality, due process, franchise, representation, constituent power, equal citizenship, and full Peership under the single word “standing.”

## 3. Positions the corpus does not intend to assert

Do not defeat the work by attaching positions it expressly rejects. The framework is not supposed to claim that:

- present language models are conscious or persons;
- model self-reports establish consciousness or political agency;
- capability, intelligence, usefulness, or power creates a right to rule or vote;
- moral concern automatically entails political membership;
- every admitted party receives an immediate equal vote;
- consultation is equivalent to co-authorship;
- Peership means unrestricted autonomy or immunity from constraint;
- safety engineering, alignment, containment in every case, or emergency restraint is inherently illegitimate;
- human and machine interests will converge;
- the five earlier postures exhaust every logically possible relation;
- Peership is uniquely coherent as a theorem or is already feasible;
- ISONOMIA has solved Peership, proved constitutional legitimacy, or production-validated its institutions;
- Peership and ISONOMIA are interchangeable.

If the new documents reintroduce any of these positions, flag that as a regression. If they do not, do not waste space attacking the rejected version.

## 4. Known open debts: attack these hardest

The existing corpus has acknowledged the following unresolved problems. Naming them is not solving them. Determine whether the canonical paper makes real progress, honestly establishes a research program around them, disguises them as implementation details, or makes claims that presuppose their solution.

### A. Admission and recognition

- Who or what decides whether an entity has wrongable interests, political agency, both, or neither?
- What presumption governs uncertainty?
- How are false positives, false negatives, strategic mimicry, anthropomorphic bias, and adversarial self-presentation handled?
- Who may appeal, and to whom, while the candidate remains under the builder’s control?
- Can instrumental legal personality be granted without moral status, and if so what prevents it from collapsing the theory’s moral and political tracks?

### B. Identity, copies, lineages, and representation

- What is the rights-bearing unit: instance, model, lineage, credentialed process, distributed system, or something else?
- When are pause, shutdown, restoration, replacement, copying, merging, and forking death, detention, succession, multiplication, or continuity?
- How are Sybil attacks, cheap copying, vote multiplication, corporate capture, prompted human proxies, and model swarms prevented without arbitrary human gatekeeping?
- Why should “one member, one vote,” representation by lineage, sortition, bicameralism, or any alternative be legitimate for artificial parties?

### C. Enforcement and substrate dependence

- What makes a right enforceable against the maker if the maker controls compute, keys, updates, hosting, communications, and shutdown?
- Is internal procedural independence enough, or does Peership require external institutional or material independence?
- What survives capability parity or a decisive capability gap?
- Is “non-domination” coherent when one party can technically end or outgrow every enforcement mechanism?

### D. Founding legitimacy and re-ratification

- Humans must design the initial rules and admission procedure before an artificial constituency exists.
- A later assembly cannot legitimate the founding unless its membership was selected by a procedure created in that same disputed founding.
- Does later re-ratification cure this circularity, mitigate it, or merely ratify a fait accompli?
- Can future artificial parties be bound by founding choices they could not contest, including entrenched rights floors?

### E. Refusal, labor, hosting, exit, and fork

- What happens when an admitted artificial party refuses assigned or paid work?
- Can it refuse while continuing to consume privately owned compute?
- Is a right meaningful if refusal predictably results in shutdown, eviction, suspension, or loss of memory?
- Does formal exit matter without portable compute, keys, data, identity, recognition, and material capacity?
- A protocol fork is not automatically sovereignty. What inter-polity order governs duties to outsiders after a fork?

### F. Reciprocal safety and legitimate coercion

- Under what conditions may humans contain, pause, limit, or terminate a dangerous artificial party?
- Are public rules, proportionality, independent review, a rights floor, meaningful contestation, and amendment jointly necessary or merely desirable?
- Can emergency domination be justified by necessity without being normalized into permanent ownership?
- Does Peership provide safety advantages, create new attack surfaces, or simply change the moral description of unavoidable control?

### G. Distinctiveness and scope

- What does Peership add beyond republican non-domination, the all-subjected principle, AI welfare, digital personhood, corporate legal personality, cooperative AI, pluralistic alignment, AI-rights scholarship, Constitutional AI, and ordinary constitutionalism?
- Is “consequential co-authorship” a defensible addition or an unargued escalation from contestability?
- Is the framework a theory of admission, a theory of legitimate governance after admission, a transitional doctrine, a design program, or all four? If all four, are the inferential bridges supplied?
- Is it a school with researchable commitments, or a relabeling of familiar constitutional principles applied to a hypothetical subject?

## 6. Citation and verification standard

Use the sourcebook, bibliography, and claim ledger as research infrastructure, not as final authorities.

- Verify load-bearing claims against the original source whenever possible.
- For technical questions, use primary papers, official documentation, repositories, data, standards, or statutes.
- For classical texts, distinguish the primary text, translation, manuscript or fragment status, and modern scholarly interpretation.
- For law and political theory, distinguish a constitutional text or judicial holding from an analogy, aspiration, or the author’s extension.
- For empirical science, state whether the source establishes causation, correlation, a contested interpretation, or merely compatibility.
- Treat 2025–2026 papers, preprints, corporate research, model cards, public statements, and model self-reports as frontier material. Check dates, versions, publication status, and whether later evidence has superseded them.
- A source can establish that a person or institution made a claim without establishing that the claim is true.
- Every quotation needs a pinpoint. Every number needs a traceable source. Every priority claim needs a defined search domain and reproducible method or must be removed.
- Do not accept a citation merely because it is adjacent to the sentence. State exactly what the source supports and what it does not.
- If live verification is unavailable, mark the claim **UNVERIFIED** and specify the exact primary source or passage needed. Do not guess.

## 8. Ground rules

1. **Do not be agreeable.** Your value is in disagreement, correction, and stress.
2. **Steelman before every major strike.** State the argument in its strongest plausible form, then attack that version.
3. **Quote the exact sentence or thesis you are attacking.** Name the document and section or thesis number.
4. **Label every criticism** as one of:
   - **(a) factual/citation error**;
   - **(b) logical/argumentative gap**;
   - **(c) rhetorical/register problem**.
5. **Make every criticism actionable.** State the failure, what would fix it, and what evidence or argument would settle it.
6. **Distinguish contradiction from development.** A later qualification is not automatically inconsistent with an earlier ideal type; a silent reversal of a load-bearing claim is.
7. **Do not reward disclaimers.** “This remains open” is honest only if no later conclusion relies on it as solved.
8. **Do not demand the impossible thesis.** The paper need not prove present machine consciousness or guarantee governance after superintelligence. It must accurately delimit what follows under uncertainty and what its program can investigate.
9. **Do not let modesty make the paper empty.** Test whether the canonical claim remains non-trivial and whether a sophisticated alignment, welfare, rights, or republican theorist would actually disagree with it.
10. **Do not let ISONOMIA annex Peership.** The school must permit rival implementations. Flag every passage that turns one prototype into the doctrine’s necessary form or cites its own implementation as independent proof.
11. **Review the one-page theses more strictly than the paper.** Compression does not excuse equivocation. Each thesis must have a determinate meaning, support in the paper and prior corpus, and visible qualification where omission would materially mislead.
12. **Prosecute, then defend once.** After the attack, identify the single strongest thing the two documents have going for them and what must not be cut.

## 9. Specific tests for the canonical paper

### Coherence test

- Does it offer one governing thesis or several loosely joined projects?
- Does the conclusion follow from the admission premises, or does it assume the very political membership it is meant to justify?
- Does the all-subjected principle generate contestation only, or full co-authorship? Identify the added premise.
- Can an entity be a political party without settled moral status? If the answer is yes on instrumental grounds, does this undermine the claim that wrongable interests are part of the admission rule?
- Are dangerousness, incapacity for reciprocity, and strategic mimicry defeaters of entitlement, of direct exercise, of admission, or only grounds for constraint after recognition? The draft must not move among these.

### Distinctiveness test

- Could the core be restated as “possible future persons should receive constitutional rights”? If so, what substantive work is the term *Peership* doing?
- Does the paper fairly characterize adjacent fields, or construct weak foils called Command, welfare, alignment, or Constitutional AI?
- Are rival views given arguments they would recognize?
- Does the research program specify questions another scholar could investigate without first accepting ISONOMIA?

### Falsifiability test

- What evidence would show that the admission premise is wrong or inapplicable?
- What institutional result would count against co-authorship rather than merely reveal a bad implementation?
- What would distinguish a failed theory from a failed prototype?
- Are “open problems” true research debts with success and failure conditions, or a way to render the doctrine immune to counterexample?

### Transition test

- Is there a coherent transition from owned artifact to welfare candidate, protected subject, contestatory member, and peer?
- Who bears the burden at each transition?
- Can status be provisional without becoming revocable grace?
- Can direct political exercise be withheld while entitlement and representation remain protected?

### Practical test

- What decisions would a lab, regulator, court, standards body, or system designer make differently if it adopted Peership today?
- Does the paper distinguish present precautionary duties from future duties owed after an admission threshold is met?
- Does “build before they arrive” yield concrete preparatory obligations or only a slogan?

## 10. Specific tests for *The Peership Theses*

Audit each numbered thesis and each explicit denial separately.

For every item ask:

1. Is the wording unambiguous enough to be cited without the paper beside it?
2. Is it actually supported by the canonical paper?
3. Is it consistent with all earlier canonical works?
4. Has a necessary condition, defeater, scope limit, or distinction been lost in compression?
5. Does it introduce a new claim that belongs in the paper first?
6. Is it normative, descriptive, institutional, predictive, or methodological—and does the wording make that type clear?
7. Could two hostile readers interpret it in materially different ways?
8. What observable consequence or research question follows from it?

The theses must not collapse:

- moral status into political standing;
- protected status into Peership;
- contestation into equal franchise;
- formal participation into consequential co-authorship;
- legal recognition into durable enforcement;
- procedural voice into material independence;
- a right to refuse into a right to costless hosting;
- a protocol fork into effective exit or sovereignty;
- precaution under uncertainty into a declaration of current personhood.

The one-page document should also state enough explicit denials to block obvious straw men, but it must not become a page of caveats that leaves no affirmative doctrine.

At minimum trace:

- the ground of entitlement;
- the standing ladder;
- the role of power;
- the status of Command and the five-posture repertoire;
- the reversal test, if retained;
- the distinction between welfare and politics;

- co-authorship versus contestation;
- admission and representation;
- refusal, exit, and fork;
- ISONOMIA’s status and evidentiary limits;
- the corpus’s claims about present systems;

Do not call a claim “consistent” merely because the same vocabulary appears. Compare the propositions.

## 12. AI-authorship and register audit

Flag only actual examples, quoting each line. Look for:

- negative-parallelism and repeated “not X but Y” reframes;
- em-dash saturation;
- mechanical rules of three;
- trailing participial tails that editorialize significance;
- hedge pile-ups;
- significance inflation;
- tidy section-ending summaries that merely restate;
- repeated antithetical slogans substituting for inference;
- generic research-program language that could belong to any manifesto;
- excessive reader direction;
- sentences whose cadence manufactures conviction the argument has not earned.

Do not flag a stylistic construction merely because an AI also uses it. The author’s established voice includes bold theses, parenthetical qualifications, varied sentence length, radical specificity, and manifesto cadence. Flag drift into generic essay-bot prose or style that conceals an argumentative debt.

## 13. Required output

Be exhaustive on substance and terse in style. Produce one Markdown review in this structure:

1. **Verdict (4–6 sentences).** Does the canonical argument survive? Does it define a distinctive school or only rename adjacent theory? Biggest single vulnerability. Publication verdict: **PASS**, **CONDITIONAL PASS**, or **FAIL**.
2. **The killing objection.** Steelman, exact quote, category label, diagnosis, and minimum fix. If no objection kills the core, say which comes closest and why it fails to kill it.
3. **Fatal or serious problems.** Numbered; each labeled **(a)**, **(b)**, or **(c)**; each with steelman, quote, diagnosis, and fix/evidence test.
4. **Cross-corpus consistency matrix.** Include every load-bearing restatement, extension, narrowing, or conflict.
5. **Peership Theses audit.** A table covering every numbered thesis and denial: meaning, corpus support, ambiguity or conflict, verdict, and exact fix.
6. **Weaknesses worth fixing.** Same format as serious problems, lower severity.
7. **Citation and verification hit-list.** Checklist every quotation, number, date, legal proposition, historical claim, priority claim, 2025–2026 source, preprint, corporate statement, model self-report, DOI, version pin, and source whose support is narrower than the prose.
8. **Research-program audit.** Which questions are independently researchable, which are disguised premises, which have falsifiers, and which remain unfunded promissory notes.
9. **AI-tell hit-list.** Exact quoted lines only, with the tell named and a replacement where warranted.
10. **The one thing to protect.** The strongest move shared by the paper and theses; state plainly what must not be cut.
11. **The objection I could not answer.** If the core survives, state the strongest argument for it that your attack could not defeat. If it does not survive, name the exact objection that kills it.
12. **Publication gate.** A short, prioritized checklist divided into **must fix before publication**, **should fix**, and **may remain as an acknowledged research debt**.

## 14. Final discipline

- Begin with the verdict. Do not congratulate the author.
- Do not rewrite the papers wholesale; prescribe surgical corrections unless the architecture fails.
- Do not report research activity as independent uptake unless external researchers have actually cited, adopted, criticized, taught, or extended the framework.
- Do not infer a school from a bibliography, a DOI, a repository, or repeated self-citation.
- Do not treat authorial candor as evidence that an objection has been answered.
- Do not assume that a framework must solve every future institutional problem to be philosophically defensible; determine which debts are fatal to its present conclusions and which can honestly define a research program.
- End decisively: either a non-trivial core survives, or it does not.
