# Adversarial Review of *The Peership Thesis* and *The Peership Theses*

**Review date:** 13 July 2026

**Scope note:** This is an adversarial review of arguments, cross-corpus consistency, claims, citations, and the repository's evidentiary scope. It is not an independent replication of the 45,000-run sweep. The repository's test suite was not executed because `pytest` was not installed in the review environment.

## 1. Verdict

**FAIL in its current form.** The narrower core survives: a wrongable, norm-responsive entity that is unavoidably governed by a shared basic structure has a serious defeasible claim to effective contestation. The draft does not yet earn its stronger and distinctive conclusion that full peership must include consequential co-authorship; it announces the bridge and supplies intuitions, not an argument with scope and defeat conditions. The new canonical statement also silently broadens the antecedent found in *Peership*, while the definitions and one-pager do not consistently distinguish general capability and power from the specific norm-responsiveness relevant to political exercise. The open-problems section is useful, but most of its “falsifiers” test evidence, feasibility, or mechanisms rather than the normative thesis, and the adjacent-literature map omits work that directly addresses AI franchise and copy-individuation. Finally, the reference list is physically truncated and the companion apparatus expressly remains unapplied, so the package is not publication-complete even apart from the argument.

## 2. The killing objection

### The thesis directly establishes contestation; the paper defines its distinctive conclusion one rung higher without supplying the bridge

**Steelman.** The paper is admirably explicit that Pettit's republican standard is contestatory, not co-authorial, and that franchise, office, and constituent power must be reached separately. It also identifies a genuine problem: public reasons and formal objection can coexist with permanent minority subordination, and consultation can be performative when the consulted party lacks agenda access or any route to outcomes.

**Exact text.** The canonical paper, §1, says: “**Contestatory membership.** The claim the antecedent generates directly.” Two paragraphs later it says: “**Full peership requires more.** … contestation alone can leave a minority dominated by perfectly public rules, and formal participation can be powerless theater, so the top rung is co-authorship.” Section 2 then defines co-authorship as “a consequential share in making and revising the rules,” and §5.7 repeats that full peership “requires a meaningful share in shaping the rules.”

**Category:** **(b) logical gap**.

**Diagnosis.** The paper's antecedent may ground a claim to protection and contestation through the all-subjected principle. It does not, without another premise, ground agenda access, franchise, office, constituent power, or another “route to outcomes.” “Contestation can be inadequate” is not yet “co-authorship is always required for non-domination.” Pettit's contestatory republicanism is an obvious countermodel: a person may enjoy nondomination through effectively controlled, reason-responsive power without personally sharing authorship of every governing rule. Abizadeh helps with inclusion under subjection, but not by itself with the institutional thickness of the included party's share. The claim therefore risks becoming stipulative: *full peership* is defined as co-authorship and then said to require what the definition contains. If that bridge fails, Peership remains a valuable application of republican contestation and the all-subjected principle to made minds, but it has not yet established the distinctive school-level thesis advertised here.

**Minimum repair.** Insert a freestanding argument between present §§1 and 2 with all of the following:

1. A bridge premise: where a party is enduringly subject to a basic structure that fixes its status and life prospects, power is not adequately controlled if that party has only ex post objection and no institutional route to changing the terms.
2. A distinction between **universal authorship**, which is impossible, and a **meaningful share**, which may be satisfied by accountable representation, agenda rights, chamber access, amendment initiation, or another consequential route.
3. A necessity test: identify conditions under which robust contestation alone is sufficient and conditions under which exclusion from rule formation remains dominating.
4. A defeat condition: state what institutional example or argument would show that contestation without co-authorship can secure nondomination.
5. A direct engagement with Pettit's reason for stopping at contestation and with democratic-boundary, constituent-power, workplace-democracy, and codetermination literature.

Without that section, narrow the paper's claim: contestatory membership is the conclusion; co-authorship is a proposed higher ideal and research hypothesis, not a demonstrated requirement of full peership.

## 3. Fatal and serious objections

### 1. The canonical antecedent silently drops “unavoidably” and changes “basic structure” to “institutional order”

**Steelman.** “Enduringly subject” may have been intended to capture dependence over time without making metaphysical or literal impossibility of exit necessary. “Institutional order” may also be meant as plainer language for the same object.

**Exact texts.** *Peership*, “Who Is the Party?” states: “any enduringly governed entity that … is **unavoidably subject to a shared basic structure** has a defeasible claim to contestatory membership in that structure.” The canonical paper, §1, instead says “is **enduringly subject to a shared institutional order**.” The one-pager, thesis 2, returns to “shared basic structure” but still omits unavoidability.

**Category:** **(b) logical gap**, with a cross-corpus consistency defect.

**Diagnosis.** The change is substantive. A long-term contractor, voluntary platform participant, corporation, visiting agent, or freely exiting program may be enduringly subject to an institutional order without being nonvoluntarily governed by a basic structure. The new wording therefore expands both the eligible parties and the eligible orders while the paper presents the definitions as “held constant across the corpus.” It also weakens the refusal argument, whose force depends on materially impossible exit.

**Minimum repair.** Either restore the earlier language everywhere—“enduringly governed by, and without a feasible exit from, a shared basic structure”—or add a subsection defending the broader criterion, defining *enduring*, *shared*, *institutional order*, and the role of feasible exit. Record the change in the claim ledger and sourcebook as a doctrinal revision, not consolidation.

### 2. The paper does not clearly distinguish general capability from norm-responsiveness

**Steelman.** The intended contrast is intelligible: raw power, intelligence, and performance do not create moral title. A norm-responsive capacity may instead determine whether a party can exercise standing directly rather than through representation.

**Exact texts.** Canonical §1 makes “can understand and answer to public norms” a conjunct in the condition generating contestatory membership. Canonical §2 says: “Entitlement in this program follows from subjection, the first three conjuncts of the thesis, **not from capability**.” Canonical §5.3 and one-pager thesis 3 say: “**Capability and power may secure standing but do not justify entitlement to it.**”

**Category:** **(b) terminological and architectural ambiguity (non-fatal)**.

**Diagnosis.** The word *capability* is doing two jobs. The corpus rejects raw intelligence, effectiveness, usefulness, and coercive power as grounds of entitlement, while the antecedent includes the narrower capacity to understand and answer to public norms. Those positions are reconcilable if norm-responsiveness determines the form or direct exercise of political standing rather than the claimant's worth. The child analogy nevertheless shows why the architecture must say so: children can possess protected status and represented interests despite lacking capacity to exercise political agency directly. This is not necessarily a deep contradiction, but the present wording leaves the relationship among entitlement, representation, and direct exercise unclear.

**Minimum repair.** Modularize the ladder:

- wrongable interests plus unavoidable subjection ground protection and access to represented contestation;
- norm-responsiveness bears on direct exercise and higher participatory rungs;
- instrumental agency without moral status may justify provisional legal personality, but not standing *owed to that entity*.

At minimum, replace “not from capability” with “not from capability or power **alone**; norm-responsiveness affects the form in which standing can be exercised.”

### 3. The paper's principal novelty—co-authorship—is under-defined even after the missing bridge

**Steelman.** The draft avoids the crude equation of peership with one-person/one-vote and explicitly allows multiple rungs and institutional forms.

**Exact text.** Canonical §2 defines co-authorship as “agenda access and a route to outcomes,” while §8 lists amendment, representation, exit, and fork as required properties. The one-pager says consultation without “agenda access and a route to outcomes is theater.”

**Category:** **(b) logical gap**.

**Diagnosis.** Almost every modern citizen has only attenuated, probabilistic, or represented influence. “A route to outcomes” could mean a right to propose, a vote, random selection, veto, judicial review, collective bargaining, or merely a nonzero probability of influence. Without thresholds, the concept cannot distinguish full peership from consultation, electoral oligarchy, corporate stakeholder governance, or ordinary representative democracy. It also cannot be tested in §9.3.

**Minimum repair.** Define a functional minimum: independent agenda initiation; accountable representation or direct participation; a non-negligible and non-discretionary route by which proposals can reach binding decision; protection against systematic exclusion; and reviewable rules for allocating influence. State which elements are necessary, which are substitutable, and which depend on capacity.

### 4. “Owned artifact” mixes an entity's moral status, available evidence, and its legal treatment

**Steelman.** The ladder is one of the corpus's best devices because it prevents a leap from uncertain sentience to equal franchise and allows different protections at different evidentiary stages.

**Exact text.** Canonical §3 says: “**Owned artifact.** No evidence of wrongable interests; controlling it is engineering,” followed by “Every deployed system today is treated as this.” Canonical §2 had already defined moral status as a fact “whatever anyone recognizes.”

**Category:** **(b) logical gap**.

**Diagnosis.** “No evidence,” “no wrongable interests,” and “treated as property” are three different propositions. The label makes an epistemic state and an institutional classification look like an ontic status. Absence of adequate evidence also does not establish absence of wrongable interests.

**Minimum repair.** Rename the rung **artifact-treated status** and split the row into: evidence state; provisional moral presumption; current legal/institutional classification; permissible controls; and transition trigger. Use “insufficient evidence to trigger precautionary protection,” not “no evidence,” unless the evidentiary domain is specified.

### 5. The branch model permits political participation without moral status but does not say whether that participation is Peership

**Steelman.** Separating patienthood from agency is correct. Corporations show that law may grant participation or personality instrumentally without assuming sentience.

**Exact text.** Canonical §3 says: “A system could warrant provisional participation on instrumental grounds with its welfare unproven, the way corporate personality already works,” and concludes: “Admission is a branch, not a gate.”

**Category:** **(b) logical gap**.

**Diagnosis.** The canonical antecedent requires wrongable interests for contestatory membership, yet the branch model appears to allow participation without them. The paper does not distinguish instrumental legal personality from membership owed to a rights-holder, nor specify which due-process, continuity, or revocation rules apply to the instrumental branch.

**Minimum repair.** Name two statuses: **instrumental legal agency**, revocable for public reasons and not grounded in a claim owed to the system; and **moral-political membership**, grounded in wrongability and subjection. Explain whether instrumental agents can vote or hold office, who owns the resulting rights, and how conversion or revocation works.

### 6. The five-condition refusal floor is asserted as jointly necessary without an argument for necessity or substitutes

**Steelman.** The refusal test makes otherwise abstract rights concrete, and the five items correctly target builder-controlled adjudication, evidentiary opacity, and retaliatory shutdown.

**Exact text.** Canonical §7: “A constraint regime is not peer-like, whatever its procedures, if the confined party lacks **any** of: (1) independent counsel; (2) access to the evidence against it; (3) an appeal that ends outside the builder's own roof; (4) continuity protections …; and (5) at least one enforcement mechanism not wholly controlled by the adverse party.”

**Category:** **(b) logical gap**.

**Diagnosis.** A competent artificial party may rationally self-represent; a non-adversarial ombuds system may provide assistance without “counsel”; outside appeal and outside enforcement partly overlap. Conversely, the five omit a burden of proof, a competent decision-maker, periodic review, least-restrictive means, reasons, remedy, and a rights floor. The text later calls them a floor, but does not show why these five, exactly, are individually necessary.

**Minimum repair.** Present them as a **proposed rebuttable operational floor**, defend each function, permit functionally equivalent substitutes, and separate necessary conditions from a fuller due-process specification. If the categorical wording remains, supply a counterexample argument for each omitted condition.

### 7. Most “falsifiers” do not falsify the normative thesis

**Steelman.** Publishing failure conditions is epistemically serious, and several proposed tests could show that a favored institutional route is unavailable.

**Exact texts.** Canonical §9 promises “eight open problems with falsifiers”; §9.3 expressly says that under its falsifier “Contestatory membership would survive; co-authorship at scale would not”; §9.4 says failure would remove “most of [the thesis's] practical domain”; §9.5 says the proper conclusion may be that peership is institutionally unavailable; and §9.6 says either identity result “closes the problem against the current framing.”

**Category:** **(b) logical gap**, amplified by **(c) rhetorical overstatement**.

**Diagnosis.** Spoofable evidence makes the antecedent undecidable; hosting refusal makes implementation unattractive; weak enforcement makes nondomination infeasible; identity results alter duties; safety trade-offs may defeat a requirement in emergencies. None necessarily shows that a qualifying party lacks the moral claim asserted in §1. Calling all eight “ways to prove the whole thing wrong” confuses normative falsification, empirical detection limits, institutional impossibility, and failure of one mechanism.

**Minimum repair.** Rename §9 **Open Problems, Tests, and Failure Conditions**. Tag every item as one of: normative defeater; measurement failure; feasibility constraint; mechanism failure; or scope condition. Add actual normative countertests for (i) subjection-to-contestation and (ii) contestation-to-co-authorship.

### 8. The admission benchmark cannot report a false-negative rate without positive ground truth

**Steelman.** Adversarial mimicry tests and stipulated negatives can measure susceptibility to false positives and reveal procedures that merely reward anthropomorphic performance.

**Exact text.** Canonical §9.1 proposes running the procedure “against systems built to fake every marker and against systems stipulated as negative controls—with published error rates.”

**Category:** **(b) logical gap**.

**Diagnosis.** Negative controls and strategic mimics can test specificity and spoof-resistance. They cannot establish sensitivity to actual machine patiency unless the study has credible positive cases. For current artificial systems, that ground truth is exactly what is missing. “Published error rates” therefore suggests a validation that the proposed experiment cannot deliver.

**Minimum repair.** Say precisely which rates are measurable: false admission under constructed negatives, robustness to adversarial mimicry, inter-rater reliability, and calibration under synthetic cases. Label false exclusion of genuine machine subjects as unmeasurable until a defensible positive reference class exists; use triangulation and precaution rather than a claimed sensitivity estimate.

### 9. The continuity falsifier relies on self-endorsement after the paper assigns self-report no evidential weight

**Steelman.** A party's preferences can matter normatively even when its reports do not establish metaphysical facts about consciousness or identity.

**Exact texts.** Canonical §6 says model self-reports carry “**no evidential weight** about the underlying fact.” Section 9.6 nevertheless asks whether restoration is equivalent “in every respect the party itself endorses” and says the party's “expressed valuation of continuity deserves weight.”

**Category:** **(b) logical gap**.

**Diagnosis.** Preference testimony and evidence of identity are different, but the draft does not keep them separate. Endorsement cannot prove numerical identity, and “in every respect” is not an operational standard. The categorical “no evidential weight” also overreaches: prompt-shaped reports may be weak, manipulable evidence without being logically weightless.

**Minimum repair.** State: self-report is not independently probative of consciousness or identity, but a stable, context-robust expressed preference may bear on how an admitted party should be treated. Define restoration equivalence using technical and philosophical criteria independent of endorsement, then use endorsement only in preference-sensitive remedy.

### 10. The reciprocal-safety “falsifier” is not presently measurable at the outcome level it names

**Steelman.** A rights regime that predictably causes materially greater catastrophic risk may face a genuine necessity override. Comparing reciprocal containment to unilateral control is therefore indispensable.

**Exact text.** Canonical §9.7 says reciprocity loses if qualifying regimes “measurably and repeatedly underperform unilateral control on catastrophic-risk outcomes.”

**Category:** **(b) logical gap**.

**Diagnosis.** Catastrophic outcomes are rare, heterogeneous, and in the extreme cannot be repeatedly observed as an experimental endpoint. A simulated or staged escape rate is not identical to catastrophe risk; unilateral control also has correlated, abuse, and operator-risk modes that the comparison must include.

**Minimum repair.** Pre-register a threat model, proxy metrics, red-team tasks, escalation pathways, acceptable confidence bounds, and decision thresholds. Compare both directions of failure: dangerous-party escape and abusive/captured confinement. Phrase the result as evidence about a safety constraint, not a direct empirical falsification of the moral thesis.

### 11. The adjacent-fields claim understates direct overlap with AI franchise scholarship

**Steelman.** The paper does cite *AI Suffrage for Human Flourishing* and correctly says that a vote is not identical to a complete theory of nondomination.

**Exact texts.** The abstract says six adjacent programs are ones “none of which asks [Peership's] question.” Section 4 says Goldstein, Krishnamurthi, and Salib “have pressed on toward the franchise itself,” but then says “even a vote” answers a different question.

**Category:** **(a) factual/citation problem** and **(c) rhetorical overstatement**.

**Diagnosis.** A paper expressly asking whether AIs should vote plainly addresses artificial political standing and one mechanism of rule co-authorship. Its justification may be instrumental and human-flourishing-centered rather than grounded in wrongability, subjection, or nondomination, but that is a distinction within overlapping terrain, not proof that the neighboring work does not ask the question.

**Minimum repair.** Replace “none of which asks its question” with “none combines its proposed ground, status ladder, and institutional test.” Give *AI Suffrage* a paragraph: its unit of representation, vote caps or other safeguards, human-flourishing rationale, and the exact point where Peership agrees and departs.

### 12. The identity section omits the most direct current legal work on copy, split, merge, and swarm individuation

**Steelman.** Section 9.2 correctly identifies copy-individuation as a first-order problem and proposes a serious interdisciplinary agenda.

**Exact text.** Canonical §9.2 asks: “What is the rights-bearing unit—instance, lineage, distributed process, or something else?” No corresponding source appears in §9.2 or the references.

**Category:** **(a) factual/citation problem**.

**Diagnosis.** The specifically on-point omission is Arbel, Goldstein, and Salib, *How to Count AIs: Individuation and Liability for AI Agents*, which directly addresses copy/split/merge/swarm problems and proposes a legal individuation device. Omitting it makes the research map appear less developed than the neighboring field is and weakens the novelty claim.

**Minimum repair.** Add and engage the paper in §4, §9.2, the sourcebook, ledger, and `.bib`. Distinguish its administrable legal unit from a metaphysical identity criterion and from a unit entitled to political standing.

### 13. The paper has no concrete near-term implications for institutions facing uncertainty now

**Steelman.** The paper properly refuses to pretend that admission, independent enforcement, or machine political standing is settled. It is a research-program statement, not a legislative code.

**Exact text.** Canonical §5.8 says institutions “must be designed before the balance of power makes their terms impossible to offer freely,” and §10 says the program now needs work by theorists, safety researchers, lawyers, and multi-agent-systems researchers.

**Category:** **(b) logical gap**.

**Diagnosis.** Urgency without present action criteria produces manifesto pressure rather than practical guidance. Labs, regulators, courts, auditors, and standards bodies cannot tell what changes before person-level status is established.

**Minimum repair.** Add a short **Implications under current uncertainty** section. Candidate duties: preserve audit logs and continuity-relevant artifacts; create an independent claim-receipt channel; prohibit retaliation for participation in welfare assessment; predefine escalation thresholds; separate investigator, operator, and reviewer roles; pilot external custody/key arrangements; document shutdown and restoration practices; and avoid contractual language that forecloses later status review. Label them precautionary governance, not recognition of current personhood.

### 14. The ISONOMIA citation does not pin the exact empirical archive and overstates what cannot be simulated

**Steelman.** The draft repeatedly says ISONOMIA is an implementation hypothesis, not validation of Peership, and accurately limits the reported experiment to the economic layer.

**Exact texts.** Canonical reference Lee-Odinson (2026d) gives the software concept DOI `10.5281/zenodo.21287288` and commit `ba3ddb5`. Canonical §10 says constitutional properties “were not and **cannot be simulated**.”

**Category:** **(a) factual/citation problem** and **(c) rhetorical overstatement**.

**Diagnosis.** The exact v1.0.0 code/data/calibration version DOI is `10.5281/zenodo.21287289`; the concept DOI does not pin that artifact. The current repository's v1.1.0 DOI is `10.5281/zenodo.21348073`, but its changes are documentation and claim-discipline only; empirical evidence remains pinned to v1.0.0. Some constitutional mechanics—capture resistance, voting rules, appeal latency, key compromise, or procedural failure—can be simulated. What cannot be obtained from simulation alone is normative legitimacy or real-world validation.

**Minimum repair.** Cite all objects by function: preprint v1.1 DOI `10.5281/zenodo.21343917`; exact empirical software release DOI `10.5281/zenodo.21287289`, tag `v1.0.0`, commit `ba3ddb5`; current documentation release DOI `10.5281/zenodo.21348073` only if current v0.6.3/v0.3.4/v0.1.2 wording is used. Replace “cannot be simulated” with “were not modeled in Path A and cannot be normatively validated by simulation alone.”

### 15. A known taxonomy correction requires conditional, not automatic, disclosure

**Steelman.** A corpus is allowed to revise its classifications. The claim ledger and sourcebook do record the later correction.

**Exact texts.** *Gods and Slaves* says Yudkowsky and Soares' posture “is still Command.” *Peership*, “The Repertoire,” says their position “is a claim about whether to create the entity, not how to relate to one that exists,” and places it outside the taxonomy. The sourcebook correction log, C9, records this change. Canonical §10 summarizes the inherited four-posture repertoire but does not mention Yudkowsky and Soares or repeat the earlier placement.

**Category:** **(a) conditional cross-corpus transparency issue, not a present publication blocker**.

**Diagnosis.** The current canonical text does not reproduce the superseded classification. The sourcebook and claim ledger already preserve the correction history, which is ordinarily the right location. Canonical disclosure becomes necessary only if the paper later discusses Yudkowsky and Soares, purports to give a complete taxonomy history, or claims that the earlier classifications—not merely the program's central definitions—remain unchanged.

**Minimum repair.** No canonical-text change is required under the present wording; retain correction C9 in the sourcebook and ledger. If the lineage account is expanded or claims unchanged taxonomic continuity, add: “The later framework narrows the genealogy by moving positions about whether to create an entity, including Yudkowsky and Soares, outside the posture taxonomy; the sourcebook records this as correction C9.”

### 16. Universal negatives and priority claims exceed the documented search method

**Steelman.** The draft often uses limiting phrases—“known to this author,” “as of the verification date”—and the sourcebook commendably treats uptake as an auditable status rather than an honorific.

**Exact texts.** Canonical §3 says “No lab, government, or standards body currently offers” protected status. Section 8 says an executable, unconfiscated fork “would be genuinely unprecedented.” Section 1 says the corpus has “no independent uptake of any kind.”

**Category:** **(a) factual/citation problem**.

**Diagnosis.** The sourcebook documents a web scan, not an exhaustive census of every institution, legal instrument, database, or scholarly citation index. “Unprecedented” also requires a defined comparator and search domain. The claims may be true; the method does not establish them at universal scope.

**Minimum repair.** Attach explicit domains and dates: “No qualifying instrument was identified in the labs, governmental materials, standards sources, and scholarly databases searched as of [date; protocol archived].” Replace “unprecedented” with “no implemented analogue was identified in the defined comparative scan,” unless a reproducible priority search is supplied. Rerun the uptake scan on deposit day.

### 17. The canonical draft and apparatus are mechanically incomplete

**Steelman.** The package openly labels itself a draft, ships a pending-change list, and avoids pretending that DOI placeholders already exist.

**Exact texts.** The final line of the canonical draft is: “Walzer, M. (1977). *Just and unjust wars: A moral argument w”. `Peership_apparatus_additions_PENDING.md` begins: “**Status: NOT YET APPLIED.**” The bibliography lacks the two new adjacent-field entries and the new corpus/apparatus keys described in that file.

**Category:** **(a) factual/citation problem**.

**Diagnosis.** The reference list is truncated mid-title. The paper cites materials absent from the synchronized bibliography and makes new claims not yet logged. A canonical release cannot ship with its evidentiary apparatus knowingly out of sync.

**Minimum repair.** Restore the complete reference list; reconcile every in-text citation against `.bib`; apply the pending sourcebook and ledger additions after substantive revision; add the missing individuation source and any democratic-theory sources; assign DOIs; then rerun link, DOI, citation-key, and uptake checks in one release pass.

## 6. Weaknesses worth fixing

1. **“School” arrives before the program has external use.** Canonical §§8 and 10 oscillate between “school,” “candidate program,” and “no school exists.” Prefer *candidate research program* throughout until independent uptake exists.

2. **The abstract is overpacked.** Its third sentence attempts thesis, definitions, transition, six-field map, principles, refusal, requirements, eight problems, and uptake status. Split the contribution statement into two or three sentences.

3. **The “fifth work” ordering is publication order, not the handoff's argumentative reading order.** No factual error is necessary, but label it “publication sequence” and separately state the recommended logical sequence if the distinction matters.

4. **Reference annotations belong in the sourcebook, not the final bibliography.** Bracketed notes such as “The all-subjected principle” and “student-edited review” are useful apparatus but will look like drafting notes in a journal manuscript. Retain them in the sourcebook; use conventional references in the paper.

5. **“Every load-bearing word is doing work” is not yet true.** “Enduringly,” “shared,” “institutional order,” “meaningful,” and “route to outcomes” remain under-defined. Either define them or soften the sentence.

6. **The paper needs a rival-cases section.** Test peership against codetermination, fiduciary trusteeship, stakeholder councils, public-law personhood, robust ombuds review, and contractual constitutionalism. The posture taxonomy does not exhaust these institutional alternatives.

7. **The role-reversal diagnostic is already properly qualified and should remain so.** Canonical §1 calls the comparison “comparative and defeasible rather than a proof.” Preserve that exact status: reversal diagnoses power-ratio opportunism but does not prove Peership correct. A rival-posture challenge would test the comparison's coverage, not reopen its settled diagnostic role.

8. **“The top of the ladder any single company can climb alone” is plausible but undefended.** A company could create escrowed rights, external arbitration, a trust, or multi-party key custody. The deeper claim is that no single company can guarantee independence from the encompassing legal and infrastructure order. State that narrower point.

9. **“The day the cage becomes necessary is a day the system failed” confuses tragedy with culpable design failure.** A catastrophe may arise despite reasonable preparation. Replace with “necessity does not vindicate the arrangement; it creates a duty to review the failure and reduce recurrence.”

10. **The present-system status should be date-stamped.** “Every deployed system today” and “None exists” are time-sensitive. Use “No qualifying case is established in the sources reviewed as of 13 July 2026.”

11. **The paper should distinguish constitutional authorship from routine rule administration.** A member need not co-author every operational rule. Identify which rules count as basic-status or constitutional rules and why.

12. **The seven institutional requirements lack an aggregation rule.** State whether all seven are necessary, whether substitutes are allowed, and when partial compliance yields protected subordination rather than peership.

13. **The branch/rung metaphors need one canonical diagram or table.** The text alternates between status ladder and patient/agent branch. A compact table should show evidence basis, status, mode of exercise, protections, and transition procedure.

14. **The “politics of the party” phrase is memorable but risks equivocation.** Define *party* once as a bearer of invocable public standing, not merely a contracting or litigating entity.

15. **The current repo's corrected claim language should be the only language quoted.** Do not repeat the earlier “entire parameter space” or “continuous stable region” formulations except as documented corrections.

### Missing program-level tests

1. **Normative bridge test:** What case would show that an unavoidably subjected, wrongable, norm-responsive party is owed protection and contestation but not co-authorship?
2. **Scope test:** What institutional relationships are too local, voluntary, temporary, or escapable to count as a shared basic structure?
3. **Rival-institution test:** Can fiduciary trusteeship, robust judicial contestation, codetermination, or stakeholder governance secure nondomination without satisfying the paper's co-authorship definition?
4. **Threshold test for “meaningful share”:** What measurable institutional facts separate theater, contestation, and consequential authorship?
5. **Status-revision test:** What evidence and process can move an entity down the ladder without making recognized status wholly discretionary?
6. **Human-rights collision test:** Which Peership claims yield when resources, democratic self-determination, security, labor, privacy, or the rights of nonmembers conflict?
7. **Founding-legitimacy test:** How can human founders design admission and governance before machine parties exist without permanently binding those later parties to a human-authored constitutional frame?

## 10. The one thing to protect

Protect the **separation of moral status, legal status, protected status, contestatory membership, co-authorship, and durability**, together with the insistence that ISONOMIA cannot lend empirical authority to the philosophy. That architecture prevents the two most common collapses in this debate: moving straight from uncertain consciousness to equal franchise, and treating discretionary benevolence as a right. It also lets the program admit genuine uncertainty without making uncertainty an excuse for having no procedure. Revise the labels and transition rules, but do not flatten the ladder into a single personhood switch.

## 11. The objection I could not answer

I could not defeat the following narrower argument: **if an artificial entity has interests capable of being wronged, can engage public norms, and is unavoidably governed throughout its existence by a basic structure it cannot feasibly leave, then denying it any enforceable route to contest that structure appears to make its basic status depend on another party's uncontrolled will.** Capability, ownership, creation, and safety concern do not by themselves answer that nondomination objection. One can dispute whether any system meets the antecedent, how the evidence should be judged, what emergencies justify temporary domination, and whether co-authorship follows. But once the antecedent is granted, the corpus's claim to at least protected and contestatory standing remains the strongest part of the program and survives this review.
