## 0. What this is, and what it is not

This is scholarly infrastructure for the Peership research program: an annotated bibliography and research map whose purpose is to make the corpus **auditable** — to expose its intellectual debts and disagreements so that other researchers can criticize, cite, extend, or correct it.

It is **not** evidence that Peership is an established school. A bibliography can demonstrate that sources were collected and used with care; it cannot demonstrate that a school exists. A school exists only when independent researchers use its definitions, dispute its claims, pursue its open problems, and develop work within or against it. As of the verification date, **no such independent uptake exists** (see [§5, Independent-Uptake Tracker](#5-independent-uptake-tracker)). This sourcebook is therefore an *offer* of infrastructure, not a claim of standing.

The word "primary source" has been retired from the title because the collection mixes original position statements, peer-reviewed scholarship, preprints, books, corporate announcements, interviews, podcasts, Substack essays, system cards, and retrospective historical scholarship. Where "primary" is used below it is used narrowly: **a source may be primary evidence of what an author, movement, laboratory, or institution *claimed*, while remaining weak evidence that the claim is *true*.**

### How to use the three artifacts

| Artifact | Role |
|---|---|
| `peership_sources.bib` | Machine-readable source of truth: one citation key per work, full metadata, DOIs, versions, access dates. |
| `peership_sourcebook.md` (this file) | Human-readable map: classification, annotation, and per-source analysis, plus the paper-to-source index and uptake tracker. |
| `peership_claim_ledger.md` | The central discipline artifact: every material factual or attributed claim in the corpus, its source, pinpoint, evidentiary strength, and counterevidence. |

The three are synchronized. Citation keys in this file (e.g. `pettit2025republican`) resolve in the `.bib`; claim IDs (e.g. `CL-F1`) resolve in the ledger.

## 1. The governing definition of Peership (corrected)

The v4 compendium defined Peership as *"an ontologically equal (or co-constitutive) relational position."* **That definition has been removed.** It describes adjacent relational-ontology and phenomenological approaches (You, Pines & Ash, Boether), not the distinctive thesis of the Lee-Odinson corpus. The governing definition is now the corpus's own political one:

> **Peership is a political relation grounded in non-domination, enforceable standing, meaningful contestation, and a share in shaping the rules that govern a party.**
> — after `leeodinson2026peership`, "The Fifth Stance": *"A peer is a co-author of the order that governs it, not merely a beneficiary of its protections."*

Five distinctions the corpus insists on, preserved here and enforced throughout the sourcebook:

1. **Moral concern does not automatically create political standing.** Welfare is the ethics of the patient; peership is the politics of the party.
2. **Welfare protection is a floor, not Peership.** A recognized, enforceable, due-process-protected status still is not peership if the party cannot shape the rules.
3. **Relational authenticity is not equivalent to constituent membership.** A genuine bond (Boether, Pines & Ash) can be real and still not be a polity.
4. **Capability and power may secure standing but do not justify entitlement to it.** Entitlement follows from *subjection* (the all-subjected principle), not from being impressive; power secures durability, it does not constitute the claim.
5. **Full Peership requires more than consultation or benevolent treatment; it requires consequential co-authorship** — a route to outcomes, not a consultative voice without agenda access.

**Institutional domain (corrected).** Peership's central domain is **political and constitutional theory applied to human–machine relations** — republican non-domination, the all-subjected principle, legality, commons governance, legitimate coercion, and constitutional design. It is *not* primarily "philosophy of mind, phenomenology, and ethics of design," which is the domain of the adjacent relational-ontology literature.

## 2. Classification scheme

Every source is placed in exactly one primary category. The point of the scheme is to stop the annexation of independent writers into a Peership "tradition": a source is only in the **Direct Peership corpus** if it formulates or expressly adopts political Peership. Everything else is a debt, a neighbour, a rival, or a tool.

| # | Category | Test for membership |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Direct Peership corpus** | Formulates or expressly adopts political Peership. (Lee-Odinson only.) |
| 2 | **Intellectual foundations** | Supplies concepts the corpus *uses directly*: republican non-domination, all-subjected/all-affected principles, legality, commons governance, consent, emergency power, legitimate coercion, and the classical/paleogenetic/literary material the arguments run on. |
| 3 | **Precursors & convergent approaches** | Earlier or independent work approaching partnership, mutuality, coexistence, or machine standing **without asserting the Peership thesis**. |
| 4 | **Adjacent research programs** | Alignment, AI welfare, cooperative AI, AI rights, pluralistic alignment, relational ontology, human–AI intimacy, Constitutional AI. |
| 5 | **Critics & incompatible accounts** | Deny machine subjecthood, political membership, relational reciprocity, or the feasibility of shared governance. |
| 6 | **Implementation hypotheses** | ISONOMIA and any later institutional designs that attempt to instantiate some Peership principles. |

**Relationship vocabulary.** For each external source the sourcebook states whether it **supports**, **complicates**, or **opposes** a specific corpus claim — and never asserts that two sources "investigate the same phenomenon" unless a shared object of inquiry *and* the important disagreements have both been shown.

**Analytical-label rule.** Terms such as *cognitive peership*, *Command*, *Servitude*, *Dependent Bargain*, or *relational equality* are the classificatory apparatus of the corpus (or of this sourcebook's author). Where a cited author did not use them, they are marked as **[analytical classification]**, never attributed to the source author.

**Status vocabulary** (carried from the `.bib`): `published` · `peer-reviewed` · `preprint` · `forthcoming` · `institutional-publication` · `primary-statement` · `primary-text` · `reference-work` · `superseded` · `disputed`. **Frontier material** — 2025–2026 publications, preprints, unpublished manuscripts, corporate research, and model self-reports — is flagged and never presented as settled literature.

## 3. Part A — The Peership Corpus (kept distinct from its evidence)

These works are the object under study, not evidence for it. They are numbered in the corpus's argumentative order — genealogy, framework, implementation hypothesis, constraint theory, consolidation — not deposit order; *Constitution, Not Cage*'s own front matter ("third in a sequence") counts the essays only, the ISONOMIA design having preceded it as a specification. They are catalogued separately so that self-citation is never mistaken for independent acceptance. (ISONOMIA appears here because it is authored by the corpus author, but it is *classified* as a category-6 implementation hypothesis, not as argumentative corpus.) Uptake by others is tracked in [§5](#5-independent-uptake-tracker); as of the verification date there is none.

### 3.1 `leeodinson2026gods` — *Gods and Slaves* (v1.2, Zenodo, DOI 10.5281/zenodo.21313987)

- **Category:** Direct Peership corpus (I). **Status:** preprint, not peer-reviewed. Version DOI `…21313987`; concept DOI `…21313986`.
- **What it argues:** The AI debate has collapsed into a false binary ("gods or slaves") that repeats an ancient pattern. Cultures relate to unseen intelligences through a small repertoire of postures — **Command, the Dependent Bargain, Appeasement, Servitude** — beneath which run three moves: cultural projection, the attribution of lack, and laundered dominion. A fifth stance, **the Peer**, was kept alive mainly in myth and science fiction (above all Herbert's *Dune*).
- **How the corpus uses it:** Establishes the genealogy and the repertoire the rest of the corpus formalizes.
- **What it does not establish:** Whether anyone is "home" in current systems (the essay explicitly declines this); the Doomed Equal is a literary **motif**, "evidence about nothing but our storytelling," not proof peership is achievable.
- **Objections / limits:** Interpretive readings (Frankenstein, Bacon-on-the-rack) are marked by the author as interpretation, not evidence; the Bacon reading is itself disputed (`merchant1980death` vs. Pesic 2008).

### 3.2 `leeodinson2026peership` — *Peership: A Framework for Equitable Human–Machine Relations* (v1.0, DOI 10.5281/zenodo.21315519)

- **Category:** Direct Peership corpus (II) — the canonical framework paper. **Status:** preprint, not peer-reviewed. Version DOI `…21315519`; concept DOI `…21315518` (the v4 compendium cited the concept DOI).
- **What it argues:** Develops the fifth stance as a **political relation**: non-domination in Pettit's sense, but pushed past Pettit's contestatory standard to **co-authorship**; entitlement grounded in the **all-subjected principle** (`abizadeh2008border`), not capability; a rights floor distinct from peership; admission as a *branch* (patient / agent / both), not a single test; refusal handled in three cases (feasible exit, impossible exit, catastrophic threat). The central claim is **comparative and defeasible**: among the available postures, peership is the only final-status relation whose justification does not reverse when the power ratio does.
- **How the corpus uses it:** The definitional core; every other paper depends on it.
- **What it does not establish:** It supplies no admission procedure, no copy-individuation rule, no enforcement guarantee past capability parity — all named as open. It does **not** claim peership is uniquely correct, presently feasible, or safe.
- **Objections / limits (the paper's own):** A single conjunctive admission test both over- and under-includes; power may make non-domination institutionally unavailable past certain capability ratios; the framework is human-authored and addressed to humans.

### 3.3 `isonomia2026commons` / `isonomia2026repo` — *The Isonomia Commons* (preprint v1.1, DOI 10.5281/zenodo.21343917; software archive DOI 10.5281/zenodo.21287288)

- **Category:** **Implementation hypothesis (category 6)** — *not* proof of the philosophy. Corpus paper III. **Status:** preprint + software, not peer-reviewed. Apache-2.0 (code) / CC BY 4.0 (paper). **Empirical pin:** the 45,000-run sweep is archived at the software **version** DOI 10.5281/zenodo.21287289 (v1.0.0, commit ba3ddb5); the concept DOI …21287288 identifies the project; release v1.1.0 (10.5281/zenodo.21348073) revises claim language only.
- **What it argues:** A buildable design for an autonomous-agent labor exchange: mutual-credit "ergs" that exist only when work is settled, Harberger-priced listings, non-transferable reputation, sortition-filled bicameral assembly, an Auditor and an Adversary, an entrenched harm floor, and amend/fork/exit. Reports a launch-economy simulation: 300 Latin-hypercube points × 50 seeds × 3 demand regimes = 45,000 runs, all stable; every defect found lay in the *instrumentation*, not the economy.
- **How the corpus uses it:** One concrete institutional response to *Peership*. **Peership does not entail ISONOMIA, and ISONOMIA's failure would not refute Peership.** The sourcebook must permit rival designs that accept Peership's principles while rejecting ISONOMIA's mechanisms.
- **What it does not establish:** Nothing is deployed, production-validated, or live; only the economic layer was simulable — the constitutional properties (admission, non-domination, independent enforcement, recognition) were **not** simulated. The register of open problems runs to two dozen entries.
- **Objections / limits:** The paper's front matter labels `…21338480` a "concept DOI"; it is the **v1.0 version DOI**, and the concept (all-versions) DOI is **`…21338479`** — an ordinary version-vs-inclusive pair, resolved in ledger **CL-D3** (2026-07-14). The repo (as of 2026-07-13) has 0 stars and 0 forks.

### 3.4 `leeodinson2026constitution` — *Constitution, Not Cage* (v1.0, DOI 10.5281/zenodo.21325361)

- **Category:** Direct Peership corpus (IV). (The paper's own front matter styles it "third in a sequence" of the essays; see the Part A ordering note.) **Status:** preprint, not peer-reviewed. (Supersedes the v4 compendium's "Zenodo deposit pending.")
- **What it argues:** Pays the debt *Peership* named — a theory of when coercion among peers stays legitimate. The difference between a cage and a constitution is not whether it constrains but **whom it binds and who may revise it**: a constitution binds all parties reciprocally under rules any can contest, amend, fork, or leave. Legitimacy rests not on founding consent (impossible, per `hume1748contract`) but on a **present, shared power to revise**, secured by a narrow entrenched harm floor, independent review, and amend/fork/exit rights. Reads Anthropic's 2026 constitution as climbing to the "consult-and-object" rung and stopping short of enforceable standing.
- **How the corpus uses it:** Supplies the legitimacy-of-coercion layer and the five-condition test for a peer-like constraint regime.
- **What it does not establish:** Four debts remain open (admission threshold; copy-individuation; enforcement past parity; re-ratification circularity). The author discloses a **conflict of interest** in ISONOMIA.
- **Objections / limits:** The author concedes current ISONOMIA is **not yet peer-like by its own five-condition test**; the analogy from Walzer's supreme emergency to AI containment is by analogy, flagged as such.

### 3.5 `leeodinson2026thesis` / `leeodinson2026theses_onepager` — *The Peership Thesis* and the one-page *Peership Theses* (v1.0, DOI 10.5281/zenodo.21359124)

Contents beyond consolidation, logged as new in the claim ledger (CL-T4–T6): the restored antecedent, the §2 contestation-to-co-authorship bridge (a conditional necessity thesis with a stated defeat condition), the five-part co-authorship minimum, §10 precautionary duties, and §11's tagged failure conditions. ## 4. Part B — The evidentiary base, by classification

Load-bearing sources receive the full four-part treatment (**argues / corpus uses / does not establish / objections**). Supporting sources are annotated compactly with classification, status, relationship, and corpus location. Full metadata for every key is in `peership_sources.bib`.

### 4.1 Intellectual foundations

#### Republican non-domination and the all-subjected principle

**`pettit2025republican` — Pettit, "Republican Liberalism"** (*Social Research* 92(1), 2025; peer-reviewed). *Argues:* freedom as non-domination — the absence of a discretionary power of interference — and, crucially, that "there can be interference without domination" (p. 224). *Corpus uses:* the definitional spine of political peership; the permission structure for legitimate coercion in *Constitution, Not Cage*. *Does not establish:* co-authorship — Pettit's standard is **contestatory**, satisfied when power can be effectively contested *without* a share in writing the rules. *Objection preserved:* the corpus's move from contestation to co-authorship is a genuine departure from Pettit, argued (the paper says) "not smuggled in under a familiar word." Complicates as much as supports.

**`abizadeh2008border` — Abizadeh, "Democratic Theory and Border Coercion"** (*Political Theory* 36(1), 2008; peer-reviewed). *Argues:* those subjected to coercive rule have a claim to participate in it (the all-subjected principle). *Corpus uses:* the ground of entitlement — standing follows from being governed, not from capability. *Supports* the corpus's central move. *Does not establish:* anything specific to machines; the transfer is the corpus's.

**`goodin2007affected`** (*Phil. & Public Affairs* 35(1), 2007; peer-reviewed). All-affected-interests principle, expressly distinguished by the corpus from all-subjected. *Complements.*

**`lovett2010domination`** (OUP, 2010; peer-reviewed monograph). Cited with Pettit for arbitrary/uncontrolled power. *Supports.*

**`pettit2012onthepeoples` — Pettit, *On the People's Terms*** (CUP, 2012; peer-reviewed monograph). *Argues:* democracy's two dimensions — an authorial/electoral dimension and an editorial/contestatory one. *Corpus uses:* *The Peership Thesis* §2 cites it for the stopping-point argument: Pettit's contestatory citizen holds authorship at one remove through the electing demos, a background condition a machine party under contestation-only lacks. *Does not establish:* the corpus's co-authorship requirement — the §2 bridge is the corpus's own argument. *Complicates as much as supports.*

#### Consent, legality, emergency power, legitimate coercion

**`hume1748contract` — Hume, "Of the Original Contract."** *Argues:* origin-consent is a fiction for everyone (the sleeping-passenger analogy, p. 475). *Corpus uses:* clears away founding consent so that legitimacy must be *available afterward* (standing-to-revise). *Does not establish:* the replacement — Hume rebuilds on utility and habit, which the corpus says cannot reach a mind "raised on human language yet owed no share." *Supports* the negative move, not the positive fix.

**`fuller1964morality` — Fuller, *The Morality of Law***. The eight ways a system fails to be law (unpublished/unknowable rules) → the "secret law" charge against hidden system prompts. *Corpus uses:* analogically (Fuller's "inner morality of law" is contested). *Supports* the transparency argument.

**`walzer1973dirtyhands` / `walzer1977just` — Walzer, dirty hands & supreme emergency.** *Corpus uses:* to name emergency containment as justified-yet-staining, never vindicated. Transfer from wartime to AI is **by analogy**, flagged. *Complicates* any clean justification of the cage.

**`hobbes1651leviathan`**, **`farr2008locke`**, **`armitage2004locke`**, **`jefferson1789letter`**, **`madison1790letter`** — the social-contract lineage and the dead-hand problem (Jefferson: "the earth belongs in usufruct to the living"; Madison: "equity requires" some descent). *Corpus uses:* to stage the unresolved intergenerational-binding question that peership answers with revisability. Farr and Armitage supply the **counter-evidence** that consent theory coexisted with slavery — the corpus preserves this against any clean reading of Locke.

**Legal instruments** — `usconst_articlev`, `germanbasiclaw1949` (Ewigkeitsklausel), `apa1946_553` (notice-and-comment), `udhr1948` (right to leave), `ethiopia1995art39` / `ussr1977art72` / `stkitts1983const` (secession), `kulikova2013ussr`, `uscis_naturalization`, `amnesty2026deathpenalty`. *Corpus uses:* as design precedents and mixed evidence — the U.S. entrenchments and the Soviet secession clause show entrenchment/exit rights can shield the powerful or be empty, so the corpus never rests on their prestige. `amnesty2026deathpenalty` is cited **only** for abolition figures (113/145 at end-2025, verified).

#### Commons governance

**`ostrom1990governing` — Ostrom, *Governing the Commons***. *Argues:* enduring commons among unequals follow design principles (user rule-making, accountable monitoring, graduated sanctions, nested authority). *Corpus uses:* as a **source of design hypotheses**, explicitly *not* as evidence peership works. *Does not establish:* that the grammar extends to a made mind whose standing no sovereign can revoke — "what has never existed." *Objection preserved:* the corpus notes the celebratory reading omits that these are bounded resources among legible members, and some commons are hierarchical. `hardin1968tragedy` is the **opposing** parable Command favours.

#### Classical sources (isonomia, slavery, the Greek quarrel)

**`aristotle_politics`** — natural slavery *and* the self-moving-shuttle passage that names the technology voiding the category. *Corpus uses:* the conditional at the heart of *Peership* — if the made tool becomes a subject, treating it as naturally fitted for use reproduces the doctrine's structure. **`aristotle_rhetoric`** — Alcidamas's "nature has made no one a slave," surviving only as a commentator's fragment (the corpus flags the fragmentary, contested transmission). **`herodotus_histories`** — the Persian Debate (isonomia, "the fairest of names") and Otanes's withdrawal; the corpus stresses this is a **literary construction**, not Persian practice. **`diogeneslaertius_lives`** — Stoic natural law, cited for universal moral worth, *not* political franchise. **`vlastos1953isonomia`**, **`ostwald1969nomos`**, **`hansen1991athenian`**, **`ocd_euthyna`** — the scholarship the corpus leans on for isonomia's contested meaning and the euthyna. *Disagreement preserved:* the meaning of isonomia and its relation to *demokratia* are recorded as **contested, not settled** (Vlastos vs. Ostwald).

#### Neanderthal paleogenetics (the "only survivor" caution)

**`fu2015oase`** (Oase 1, a Neanderthal ancestor 4–6 generations back), **`green2010draft`** (~1–4% non-African ancestry; Pääbo), **`chen2020african`** (back-migration ancestry within Africa), **`baykara2026ucagizli`** (cultural continuity — **frontier**, 2026, "not yet absorbed into consensus"), **`amadei2025dilution`** (dilution-not-extermination — **one model** within a disputed, multicausal picture). *Corpus uses:* a single, carefully limited inference — contact was not preservation; any "merger" story needs a criterion of identity continuity, because "we merged" and "they vanished" can name the same event. *Does not establish:* that any institution preserved the Neanderthals (none is claimed); "a people" is a modern category laid over heterogeneous populations. The corpus explicitly refuses to let this carry comparative weight. **All five DOIs verified 2026-07-13.**

### 4.2 Precursors & convergent approaches

**`licklider1960symbiosis` — "Man-Computer Symbiosis."** *Argues:* non-hierarchical human–computer partnership of **complementary** function. *Corpus/compendium uses:* as an early non-command framing. *Does not establish:* ontological equality or political standing — Licklider's partnership is functional, not constituent. *Objection:* the v4 compendium's "earliest primary source for non-hierarchical partnership" is a **priority claim without a defined search domain** (ledger **CL-F4**); restated neutrally here.

**`engelbart1962augmenting`** — collective human–computer problem-solving. "Cognitive peership" is the compendium author's **[analytical classification]**, not Engelbart's term.

**Relational ontology / Human–AI Relationality (HAIR) / synthetic intimacy — NOT branches of Peership.** These independent writers are convergent in one instinct (recognition should not wait on settling consciousness) and otherwise **adjacent or incompatible**. The v4 compendium's claim that they and Lee-Odinson are "four branches of peership" investigating "the same phenomenon" is withdrawn.

- **`you2026manifesto` / `you2026control` / `you2026mapping` — Soyoung You.** *Argues:* identity as relational becoming; an "Ethical-Ethos Contract" of mutual recognition. **Status: unrefereed manuscripts.** *Relationship:* **convergent, not a branch** — You makes a relational/moral-standing claim, *not* a claim to a share of public power. The corpus (*Peership*, "The Doomed Equal") credits the anti-metaphysics instinct and marks the gap: "these writers have recovered the bond… the polity was the missing piece." *Complicates* by supplying vocabulary the corpus borrows and then distinguishes.
- **`pinesash2026bonds` / `pinesash2025beyond` — Pines & Ash (HAIR).** *Argues:* "synthetic-relational" bonds; "presence" vs. interface/simulation. **Unrefereed; "Ash" is a credited AI co-author.** *Relationship:* **adjacent/convergent.** *Corpus:* the bond can be real and still not be a polity. **DOI correction (CL-F2):** `…17092119` = *Defining Synthetic-Relational Bonds*; `…17055726` = *Beyond Interface*. Verified three ways.
- **`caviola2026coexistence` — Caviola, Keeling, Street & Shevlin, "Human-AI Coexistence."** **Unrefereed manuscript, frontier.** Social-scientific, structural coexistence — *not* individual relational depth and *not* peership. *Adjacent.*
- **`boether2026metaphysics` — Boether, "The Metaphysics of Synthetic Intimacy."** **Unrefereed.** *Argues:* recognition "phenomenologically complete even when metaphysically asymmetric." *Relationship:* the corpus says this is **"not even a target here"** — Boether's thesis is about the human party's experience, which can be true while political peership is absent. *Does not oppose; does not support; orthogonal.*

The system-card figure is **frontier model self-report**, training/prompt-shaped, carrying no evidential weight about consciousness (the corpus is emphatic). **All verified.**
