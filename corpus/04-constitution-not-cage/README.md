## Role in the corpus

Paper IV addresses the objection that Peership must mean unrestricted autonomy. It gives an account of when constraint on a governed entity can be legitimate rather than dominating, and states the constitutional limits any implementation — including [ISONOMIA](../03-isonomia-commons/) — would have to satisfy. It is the paper that keeps the corpus's argument compatible with safety engineering rather than opposed to it.

What it does **not** establish: it does not claim that alignment, safety engineering, or containment is inherently illegitimate, nor that any particular existing constraint is legitimate. It does not show that a design satisfying its limits would thereby be legitimate — meeting a necessary condition is not meeting a sufficient one — and it does not license immunity from legitimate safety constraints for any entity, present or hypothetical. It settles no question about whether any present AI system is a candidate for standing at all.

## A note on the numbering

The frozen PDF's own front matter describes this work as **"third in a series."** That count includes the essays only, the ISONOMIA design having preceded it as a specification rather than as an essay. The corpus's canonical reading order places [*THE ISONOMIA COMMONS*](../03-isonomia-commons/) third and this paper **fourth**.

The frozen PDF has not been altered. The revision-1.3 apparatus (`.bib` rev. 1.3; sourcebook and ledger at a v1.2 header with a revision-1.3 deposit-day note) and [*The Peership Thesis*](../05-peership-thesis/) already use the canonical order; only the older frozen PDFs differ. The discrepancy is recorded as **D4** in [`VERSION_MANIFEST.md`](../../VERSION_MANIFEST.md). Readers encountering "third in a series" in the PDF should read it as referring to the essay sequence, not to the corpus order used by this repository.
