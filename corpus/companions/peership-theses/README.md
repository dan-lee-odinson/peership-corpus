## This is not a sixth corpus paper

*The Peership Theses* is a **derivative** one-page companion to the canonical thesis, [*The Peership Thesis: A Constitutional Research Program for Human–Machine Relations*](../../05-peership-thesis/). It is not a sixth work in the corpus and carries no independent standing.

**It has no DOI of its own.** It was deposited as a file *inside* Zenodo record 21359124 — the record for the canonical thesis — and is cited by that same DOI, [10.5281/zenodo.21359124](https://doi.org/10.5281/zenodo.21359124). There is no separate record to cite, and none should be minted for it. The corpus proper consists of five works; this is not one of them.

## Role in the corpus

The companion condenses the thesis onto a single page for readers who want the program's claims and boundaries at a glance. It is a summary artifact and is strictly downstream of the paper it summarises.

What it does **not** establish, and what it must never be used to do: it adds no argument, no evidence, and no claim not already made and defended in the canonical thesis. Where the one-pager and the thesis appear to differ, **the thesis controls** — the compression is the companion's purpose and also its only real risk. In particular, the thesis must never be compressed to anything like "anything governed deserves rights." The canonical statement retains three antecedent conditions and a defeasible conclusion:

> When an artificial entity has interests capable of being wronged, can understand and answer to public norms, and is enduringly governed by a shared basic structure without feasible exit, it acquires a defeasible claim to contestatory membership in that structure. Full Peership adds a consequential share in shaping and revising the basic rules.

A one-page summary is not a short-form authority. Read it as an index to the thesis, not as a substitute for it.
