## Role in the corpus

Paper II carries the normative and political argument of the corpus. It sets out Peership as a relation of non-domination — standing, contestation, and co-authorship of the rules one is governed by — and argues that this relation, rather than welfare protection or consultation, is what would be owed to an entity that meets the antecedent conditions. It is the paper the rest of the corpus depends on and the one against which the others should be read.

What it does **not** establish: it does not claim that present AI systems are conscious, persons, or peers, and it does not treat capability, intelligence, usefulness, or power as grounds for any entitlement to rule or to vote. It does not show that moral concern automatically entails political membership, that welfare protection is already Peership, or that consultation amounts to co-authorship. It does not entail any particular institutional design — see [Paper III](../03-isonomia-commons/) — and it does not license immunity from legitimate safety constraints; the conditions under which constraint can be legitimate are the subject of [Paper IV](../04-constitution-not-cage/).

The claim is conditional and defeasible throughout. Peership is a candidate constitutional research program, not an established school with independent uptake. The canonical statement of the thesis is given in [Paper V](../05-peership-thesis/):

> When an artificial entity has interests capable of being wronged, can understand and answer to public norms, and is enduringly governed by a shared basic structure without feasible exit, it acquires a defeasible claim to contestatory membership in that structure. Full Peership adds a consequential share in shaping and revising the basic rules.
